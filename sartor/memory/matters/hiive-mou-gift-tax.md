# Matter: Hiive Anthropic-01 nominee structure §2503 gift-tax exposure

## Issue

Whether the December 24, 2025 Hiive purchase, where Alton's father (Alton Oliver Sartor) wired $282,044.70 from his Schwab account but the MOU allocated 300/300/300 beneficial-share split to father, Alton, and mother, creates a gift-tax filing obligation (or actual gift-tax liability) for the $94,014.90 attributable to Alton or to mother.

## Facts

- Father's Schwab account: $282,044.70 wired 2025-12-24 to purchase 900 HII Anthropic-01 shares.
- MOU dated 2025-12-24 allocates 300/300/300 to father, Alton, mother.
- Alton's portion: 300 shares, $94,014.90 attributed.
- Mother's portion: 300 shares, $94,014.90 attributed.
- MOU on file shows Emmett (Alton)'s signature only; father + mother signature lines blank in the copy on file.
- 2025 annual exclusion: $19,000 per donor per donee (2025 inflation-indexed).

## Authority

- **IRC §2503(b)**: Annual exclusion gifts (2025: $19,000 per donor per donee).
- **IRC §2511**: Definition of taxable gift.
- **Treas. Reg. §25.2511-1**: General gift principles.
- **Form 709**: U.S. Gift (and GSTT) Tax Return. Due April 15 of the year following the gift.

## Analysis

Two competing characterizations:

**Characterization 1: Co-investment with capital from each.** The MOU language is "memorandum of understanding and allocation," not "gift." The MOU could memorialize a pre-existing co-investment arrangement where each beneficiary contributed economic value (cash, services, or pre-existing claims) in exchange for the beneficial allocation. If father held the wire-source position only as administrative convenience, no gift occurred.
- For this to hold: each beneficiary must have provided consideration. Documentation needs to support this — bank records of pre-MOU transfers from Alton/mother to father, or service agreements, or assignments of pre-existing claims.

**Characterization 2: Gift from father to Alton + mother.** If father wired all $282K with his own money and the MOU simply allocates beneficial interest, IRS could view this as two gifts of $94,014.90 each — one to Alton, one to mother (spousal gift fully excluded under §2523, but father→son gift exceeds annual exclusion).
- $94,014.90 − $19,000 annual exclusion = $75,014.90 taxable gift for father→Alton.
- Reduces father's lifetime exemption (~$13.99M federal in 2025) by $75K.
- No gift tax actually due unless father's cumulative lifetime gifts exceed exemption.
- BUT Form 709 filing required.

Mother→father side: gift to spouse, fully excluded under §2523. No issue.

### Risk grade: MEDIUM

Position depends on facts not yet documented (whether Alton/mother had pre-existing claims to the funds). Form 709 non-filing penalty is modest absent actual tax owed. But the §2503 framing creates a taxable-gift record that matters for father's eventual estate.

## Position

**Need fact pattern from father.** If father's $282K wire was 100% his own funds with no pre-existing claim from Alton or mother → file Form 709 for father, $75K of taxable gift to Alton (within lifetime exemption, no tax owed).

If pre-existing claims exist (e.g., Alton had given father funds previously, or there was a prior agreement) → no gift, no Form 709, but document thoroughly.
