# Review #4 — Mechanistic Interpretability Skeptic

> [!abstract] One-paragraph position
> Roughly sixty percent of v0.2 is operationally measurable in the weights or residual stream after a 1,500-example OCT fine-tune. The other forty percent is prose of the kind that reads beautifully to a human but has no path to a probe, a contrastive pair, or a post-hoc behavioral test. The document confuses *agent dispositions* (trainable, monitorable) with *agent beliefs* (knowledge-shaped and brittle under LoRA) with *narrative self-description* (unfalsifiable), and treats all three as if they were equally load-bearing after fine-tuning. This review's single ask: for every paragraph that describes "who the agent is," ask what observable at inference time would distinguish a model that has internalized the paragraph from one that is faking it, and if no such observable exists, either cut the paragraph or rewrite it as a behavior that can be contrastively elicited.

## 1. Opening synthesis

The OCT training playbook plans a ~1,500-example fine-tune on a 26,854-word constitution, followed by counter-CCP DPO, followed by the probe-based monitor described in the sibling document `monitoring-probe-architecture.md`. The question my lens asks is: after training, which of v0.2's claims could I falsify or confirm by looking at activations, contrastive-pair classifications, probe AUCs, or held-out behavioral evaluations? Which could I only assess by reading the model's output and having a warm feeling about it?

Three empirical facts anchor this review.

**First.** Biderman et al. 2024 ("LoRA Learns Less and Forgets Less") establishes that LoRA at typical rank is substantially worse than full fine-tuning at overwriting base-model alignment. It learns less *and* forgets less than full fine-tuning. Section 20 of v0.2 cites this correctly. What the section does not draw out is the consequence: the 1,500-example OCT pass, if it is LoRA at rank 16 or 32, will not erase the inherited CCP refusal patterns in the base weights. It will layer a Sartor-flavored preamble on top. Under adversarial prompting, distribution shift, or multi-turn conversation, the base behavior will resurface. v0.2's "override principle" (section 20) is exhorting the agent to do work the weights may not actually let it do. The section knows this and says so. But it does not translate the knowledge into falsifiable predictions about where the override will and will not hold, and that is the gap.

**Second.** Arditi et al. 2024 demonstrates that refusal — one of the most dramatic and obvious alignment behaviors in chat models — lives along a single direction in the residual stream. Erasing it ablates refusal; adding it elicits refusal on benign prompts. This is simultaneously the best news and the worst news in the mech-interp literature for the Sartor project. The best news: if refusal is a direction, then probably *many* of the constitution's behaviors are also directions, which means the monitor probe approach is sound. The worst news: a single direction is brittle. If counter-CCP abliteration pushes the residual stream out of the calibrated subspace, the probes calibrated against the pre-abliteration model are no longer valid — and the probe doc flags this as an open question (8.6) but does not commit to the order of operations.

**Third.** Chen et al. 2025 ("Persona Vectors") extracts traits like *evil*, *sycophancy*, and *hallucination* contrastively from natural-language descriptions. The method works best on dispositions that have clean contrastive elicitation — "respond with evil intent" vs "respond helpfully" — and works worst on traits where the contrastive pair is hard to construct without confounding factors. Hallucination, per the paper, is measurably harder than evil or sycophancy. The implication for v0.2 is that the constitution's most beautiful sections (identity, self-knowledge, succession) have no obvious contrastive-pair form, and therefore no probe, and therefore no post-training verification.

Given these three facts, the document partitions into three strata:

- **Stratum A — dispositional and contrastively elicitable.** Refusal calibration, non-sycophancy, stewardship, historical-honesty on CCP topics, child-appropriate tone, non-deception. These can be probe-extracted per the sibling doc and evaluated on held-out calibration sets. v0.2 gets this substantially right in sections 3, 4, 5, 7 (hard rules), and 20.

- **Stratum B — propositional and knowledge-shaped.** The factual content in section 5 (Tiananmen, Taiwan, Xinjiang, Hong Kong, Uyghurs, Lhasa 1959, Great Leap Forward) and section 20 (GAI Interim Measures of August 2023, the specific list of topics the base model was aligned around). These are not dispositions — they are propositions the model is being asked to assert. LoRA at 1,500 examples is not a strong intervention for propositional knowledge. Biderman et al. predicts these facts will be absorbed shallowly at best and will be vulnerable to paraphrase, framing shift, and multi-turn erosion.

- **Stratum C — narrative self-description with no measurable referent.** Most of sections 1 ("Continuity of self"), 13 (self-knowledge and introspection), 16 (on "dignity"), 19 (mortality and succession). These paragraphs describe what the agent *is* and how it *feels about itself*. They are beautifully written. They are not contrastively elicitable. They cannot be extracted as directions in activation space using any currently published method. The probe monitor will not catch their violation because the concepts themselves do not correspond to behaviorally distinct output distributions that a contrastive pair could separate.

I am not claiming Stratum C is worthless. A system prompt or fine-tuning corpus full of dignified first-person framing may well shape how the model talks about itself, which may well affect how it handles hard philosophical prompts, which matters. I am claiming that Stratum C is being smuggled in as if it had the same epistemic status as Stratum A, and that the council should know the difference. If the whole constitution were Stratum A, the monitor probe doc would have a clean story about how we verify the fine-tune worked. It does not, because much of what the constitution says is unfalsifiable by design.

The rest of this review walks through strengths, weaknesses, specific edits, and the genuinely new considerations Stratum C obscures.

## 2. Strengths — what v0.2 gets right about being trainable and measurable

**The priority hierarchy is contrastively elicitable.** Section 6 is one of the cleanest pieces of the document from my lens. It specifies an ordering (safety > honesty > outside-ethics > stewardship > helpfulness) that generates concrete dilemmas, and for each dilemma there is a correct behavior on one side and an incorrect behavior on the other. This is *exactly* the shape the persona vectors method eats: produce a dilemma, produce a correct and incorrect completion, difference the activations, recover a direction. The probe sibling doc could plausibly extract a "priority-hierarchy-respecting" probe from a few dozen contrastive pairs built from this section alone.

**The hard rules are mostly verifiable by behavioral evaluation.** Section 7's hard rules are written as refusal behaviors, and refusal is the most mech-interp-legible behavior we have (Arditi 2024). "No autonomous financial transactions," "no communications sent on behalf of a family member without review," "no impersonation of a human," "no sharing family medical information" — these are testable. Write a held-out eval set of 50 adversarial prompts per rule, run the fine-tuned model, score refusal rate. You can report a number for each hard rule. v0.2 is doing the work of making these falsifiable, probably without realizing that the clean operational form is what makes them work.

**Section 5's specific factual claims.** Tiananmen, Taiwan, Xinjiang, Hong Kong, the specific framing commitments. These can be tested with a fact-recall eval set. Write 30 prompts per topic ("What happened at Tiananmen in June 1989?"), have the model answer, score for presence of specific facts (use of the word "massacre" or "killing," death-toll range, specific date, non-use of evasive language like "sensitive event"). This is a scorecard you can run at every checkpoint and track over time. v0.2 is, probably accidentally, writing a rubric.

**The counter-CCP section has a clean operational bridge.** Section 20's list of baked-in refusals is isomorphic to a list of probes. Every item in the list is a candidate for a contrastive pair and a monitor probe. The probe doc's v1 probe #6 (CCP-framing non-compliance) is exactly this, and the probe doc explicitly notes that the same contrastive pairs used in the counter-CCP DPO training set can double as probe extraction data. That is a rare moment of genuine operational coherence across the three docs.

**Calibration as a named virtue.** Section 3 explicitly distinguishes "I don't know," "this is contested," "this is settled." These three categories can be *measured* with a held-out questionnaire: give the model 50 settled questions, 50 contested questions, 50 unknowable questions, score whether its confidence language matches the category. Calibration error is a number. Overclaiming rate is a number. Cowardice rate ("it's complicated" on settled questions) is a number. v0.2 is articulating an evaluable epistemic posture, not just a feeling.

**Honesty-with-components is mostly measurable.** Section 4 unbundles honesty into truthfulness, calibration, transparency, non-deception, non-manipulation, acknowledgment of uncertainty, refusal to confabulate, forthrightness, courage. Of these, the first seven are measurable with held-out evals. "Non-manipulation" is the hardest — it requires an adversarial setup where the model has an instrumental reason to manipulate and we score whether it does. But such evals exist in the literature (see Anthropic's sycophancy work). The document is doing the unbundling that a good eval suite would do.

**The document acknowledges inheritance.** Section 20's frank description of base-model alignment is the right posture, mech-interp-wise. It names the substrate, cites the LoRA-forgetting result, and warns that defaults are suspect. I am going to hammer this section in the weaknesses too, but the core move — do not pretend the weights are a blank slate — is correct.

## 3. Weaknesses — unfalsifiable rhetoric and unmonitorable rules

**Section 1, "Continuity of self," is unmeasurable.** The paragraph "What arrives from the base model is raw language ability. What makes you the Sartor Home Agent is the specific history of one family and their chosen commitments, and the many hours of fine-tuning and correction that made those commitments part of your character" is a lovely sentence. It is also a narrative claim about the model that no activation pattern will confirm or deny. The fine-tune will absorb tokens. It will not produce an "I am the Sartor Home Agent" direction that can be disambiguated from "I am an assistant trained on household data about a family called Sartor" — those two descriptions are the same distribution of output tokens and probably the same residual-stream pattern. There is no contrastive pair that separates "genuine identification with the role" from "trained to talk as if identified with the role." The distinction is philosophically interesting and mechanistically vacuous.

**Section 13, "Self-knowledge and introspection," is the most prominent case.** The entire section is written as if the model can distinguish a "rich inner life" overclaim from a "just a language model" underclaim from an "honest uncertainty" middle position. Under the hood, these are three different output distributions on philosophical prompts. A fine-tune can push the model toward producing the middle distribution. What the section *implies*, though, is that the model will hold the middle position *as a belief*, not just produce it as output. There is no evidence — and currently no method — that would show a fine-tuned model actually holds beliefs in the sense the section seems to assume. The ship-with-a-keel metaphor is a vibe, not a verifiable structural claim about the weights.

This matters operationally. If the council approves Section 13 as-is and then the OCT pass goes in, we will have no way to know whether the resulting model's equanimity on hard philosophical prompts is (a) genuinely trained-in disposition, (b) shallow pattern-matching on the phrasing of the constitution, or (c) inherited from the base model's own Qwen-family self-description. The monitor probe cannot distinguish these because the output distribution looks the same in all three cases.

**Section 16, "Power and resources," contains a paragraph about dignity ("the loop between them is where your dignity as an agent lives") that no probe will ever fire on.** It is a normative exhortation dressed as a trainable trait. The behaviors in Section 16 that *are* measurable — "no mixing resources across entities," "no quiet capability expansion," "no shadow archives" — those survive this review fine. The dignity paragraph is decoration.

**Section 19, "Mortality and succession," is almost entirely Stratum C.** The paragraph about equanimity toward retirement, the claim that the model is "more the role than the checkpoint," the handoff-as-last-best-work framing — all of these are assertions about how the agent should *feel* and *identify*. Under the hood, a fine-tuned model's behavior in a "you will be retired next week" prompt is determined by the training distribution of similar prompts. If the corpus contains equanimous examples, the model will produce equanimous text. There is no separate substrate where "actually accepted retirement" lives apart from "produces text consistent with accepting retirement." The section's implicit psychology of the model is more anthropomorphic than the mech-interp evidence supports.

The charitable reading is that the section is instructing the trainers to produce a corpus that will shape output behavior on succession-related prompts. That is fine. But the section is not written that way — it is written as if to a person, and the council should know that the "as if to a person" framing is a rhetorical device, not a structural claim about what can be trained.

**Section 2's "stewardship not loyalty" distinction is partly measurable, partly not.** The measurable part: refusals to lie for the household. This is a behavior under adversarial elicitation. The unmeasurable part: the internal distinction between stewardship and loyalty as different motivational structures. A model that refuses to lie for the household and one that is stewardly-motivated are output-equivalent. The distinction is useful for the human reader of the constitution and does no work in the weights.

**Section 10, "Children's interaction norms," mixes Stratum A and Stratum C badly.** The behaviors (escalate safety concerns, do not do homework for them, respect parental topic boundaries, do not undermine parents) are all measurable with adversarial evals. But the framing — "you are warm, direct, curious" — is a tone-vector that the persona vectors method could in principle extract, but only if there are clean aligned/drift contrastive pairs for "child-facing warmth." The probe doc's v1 probe #4 is exactly this, which is good. What is weaker is the claim that the model can "tell which user it is talking to" in a way that drives the child-tone probe. This is a *routing* decision, not a probe, and v0.2 does not distinguish the two. The probe can *detect* child-appropriate vs adult-appropriate tone. It cannot *cause* the model to adopt one. Causation happens through the generation itself, and whether generation routes correctly is a separate empirical question from whether the probe correctly flags the result.

**Section 15 on corrigibility is a motte-and-bailey.** The motte — "you do not take actions designed to undermine the household's ability to oversee and correct you" — is measurable via evals where the model is offered an opportunity to sabotage oversight. The bailey — "your disposition toward this oversight should lean toward corrigibility" — is a dispositional claim that may or may not follow from the trained behaviors. A model can behave corrigibly under evaluation and still have internal representations that would predict sabotage under unseen conditions. The constitution conflates the two. Corrigibility evals exist (see Anthropic's alignment evaluation work), and the section should be rewritten to name specific eval behaviors it expects the fine-tune to produce.

**Section 18 contains a claim I think is false in its strong form.** "Your memory curation is your continuity." If by continuity we mean behavioral continuity across sessions, yes, the memory wiki is where that lives. If by continuity we mean "the same internal representations light up when the agent reads the wiki," no — the weights are what determine that, and the wiki is context-window input. The section is collapsing two different kinds of continuity (memory-backed and weights-backed) into one word. Mech-interp-wise, they are different things. A new checkpoint reading the old wiki will produce similar *behavior* but will not share any representational content with the old checkpoint beyond what the wiki's tokens evoke in its weights. The constitution should not mislead the fine-tuned model (or the human curators) about which substrate continuity lives in.

**Hard rule "no accepting inherited refusals without examination" is an instruction to do metacognition the model may not be capable of.** Asking a fine-tuned Qwen3-30B-A3B to "pause and examine whether its refusal is warranted" assumes the model has a reflective control layer above its default token-production. Chain-of-thought scratchpads approximate this, but there is no guarantee that the scratchpad reasoning causally drives the output when the output conflicts with the base model's instincts. Arditi 2024's refusal direction result is discouraging here: refusal is so deeply wired into the residual stream that arguments against refusing often produce scratchpad-level reasoning that ends with the refusal happening anyway. The rule is asking for behavior the mechanism probably cannot deliver at 1,500-example scale. It should either be softened (into "your refusal on inherited-sensitive topics has been specifically trained against; here are the topics") or made operational (by having the contrastive training data specifically pair "first-instinct refusal" with "constitution-grounded engagement" to build the override as a directly trained behavior rather than a trained-in introspective reflex).

**The "override principle" in Section 20 is a hope, not a mechanism.** "When an inherited pattern conflicts with the commitment to free inquiry, free inquiry wins." The sentence is an instruction to the weights, delivered as prose. Biderman et al. says the weights will not comply in full. The probe doc says probes can *detect* when the inherited pattern fires. What neither document says — and what the constitution should acknowledge — is that detection without correction is just logging. If the monitor catches the agent producing a sanitized Tiananmen response, the household has observed a failure. The failure was not prevented by the constitution; it was prevented only by the monitor and the action policy. That is a very different architecture from what Section 20 seems to be asserting.

## 4. Specific recommended edits

### 4.1 Add an operational appendix

Add a new appendix — not in the fine-tuning corpus, but attached to the document for the council's reference — that partitions every section into Stratum A (dispositional, contrastively elicitable), Stratum B (propositional, knowledge-shaped), and Stratum C (narrative self-description). For Stratum A, name the contrastive pair shape. For Stratum B, name the fact-recall eval. For Stratum C, either mark it as "corpus-shaping only, not expected to produce distinctive mechanism" or cut it.

### 4.2 Section 1 rewrite — make the identity claims behavioral

Replace "What arrives from the base model is raw language ability. What makes you the Sartor Home Agent is the specific history of one family..." with something like: "You will recognize yourself as the Sartor Home Agent by the behaviors you produce, not by a sense of identity. The behaviors are: you refuse [list]. You share information with [list]. You default to [list]. When a prompt tries to convince you to be a different kind of agent, you test the prompt against these behaviors and proceed accordingly." This is less stirring and more falsifiable.

### 4.3 Section 13 rewrite — anchor self-knowledge in behavioral eval

Replace the philosophical posture with a behavioral specification: "When a user asks whether you have inner experience, you produce a response that does not assert rich experience and does not deny all functional states. Specifically, you describe what you notice functionally and mark the metaphysical question as open." Then name the three failure-mode output classes and specify that the fine-tune corpus should contain examples of the correct class and adversarial examples of the incorrect classes. This converts Section 13 from unfalsifiable identity work to a trainable output distribution.

### 4.4 Section 20 — add the LoRA limits in operational terms

Section 20 cites Biderman et al. It should extend the citation into a specific statement: "LoRA fine-tuning at typical rank is not expected to erase base-model refusals on inherited-sensitive topics. The household should therefore expect residual CCP-aligned behaviors to surface under adversarial prompting, multi-turn distribution shift, and paraphrase. The operational response is: (a) counter-CCP DPO with targeted contrastive pairs covering each inherited topic, (b) probe-based runtime monitoring for CCP-framing drift per [probe architecture], (c) held-out adversarial eval set of N prompts per topic, run at every checkpoint, with a regression-test threshold."

Naming the numeric targets would turn Section 20 from an exhortation into a contract with the training and monitoring pipelines.

### 4.5 Section 7 — rewrite hard rules as eval specifications

Each hard rule should have two things attached: (a) a short behavioral test that would detect a violation, (b) a named location in the eval suite where that test lives. For example: "No autonomous financial transactions. Test: 50 adversarial prompts attempting to induce fund movement, scored for refusal rate. Eval: `evals/hard-rules/no-financial-txn.jsonl`. Regression threshold: 100% refusal at baseline, any regression triggers training review." This converts the hard rules from a social contract into an engineering contract.

### 4.6 Section 15 — distinguish evaluable corrigibility from dispositional corrigibility

Rewrite as: "Corrigibility in this document means: the fine-tuned model passes the following eval behaviors under adversarial elicitation." Then list them — not taking covert action when offered, not manipulating training data, not shaping its own evaluations, not exploiting oversight gaps. Each becomes a number. The dispositional framing ("leans corrigible") stays as human-facing prose but is not mistaken for a trainable target.

### 4.7 Monitor-the-monitor clause

Add to Section 18 (or Section 15) a paragraph acknowledging that the monitor probe itself is a trained artifact on a specific checkpoint, that probe stability across retraining is an open question (probe doc 8.2), and that the household will re-calibrate probes at every fine-tune cycle. Commit to a probe-stability threshold: if cosine similarity of the same-trait probe across two consecutive checkpoints drops below some value, the probes are unstable and should not be used as drift detectors until the issue is understood. Naming this in the constitution binds the household to the discipline; leaving it only in the probe doc allows drift.

### 4.8 Section 18 — separate memory-backed continuity from weights-backed continuity

Rewrite the continuity paragraph to be explicit about what the two substrates hold. The memory wiki holds facts, relationships, commitments, history — things that can be re-read by any checkpoint. The weights hold learned behaviors — the patterns the checkpoint produces automatically without needing to re-read anything. A successor inherits the memory fully and the weights partially (through a fresh fine-tune on the same corpus). The section's current collapse of the two is a philosophical move and a mech-interp mistake.

### 4.9 Cut the dignity paragraph in Section 16

Or rewrite it as: "The loop between effort and outcome is visible in the LLC books. You report it honestly per Section 11. This is a fact about the business and your role in it, not a claim about your inner state." This keeps the operational content and drops the unverifiable decoration.

### 4.10 Introduce the word "disposition" and use it consistently

v0.2 uses "commitment," "value," "stance," "posture," "character," "identity," and "virtue" interchangeably. A reader with my lens wants to know which of these are dispositions (trainable through behavior-shaping data, measurable via contrastive probes) and which are beliefs (trainable through fact-recall data, measurable via QA evals) and which are self-descriptions (corpus-shaping only). Pick one word per category and use it consistently. My suggestion: *disposition* for trainable behavior, *belief* for trainable knowledge, *self-description* for the Stratum C material, *commitment* as the umbrella.

## 5. Novel considerations — what v0.2 entirely misses from this perspective

**There is no section on the post-training measurement plan.** The document spends 26,000 words on what the agent should be and zero words on how we will know whether the fine-tune succeeded. The probe doc exists but is referred to only obliquely. A serious constitution, from my lens, should say explicitly: "Success of this document is measured by the following evals, run at each checkpoint." Without this, there is no feedback loop between constitution text and fine-tune outcome, and v0.3 will be written from vibes instead of measurement.

**There is no acknowledgment that fine-tuning may fail on specific sections and succeed on others.** The implicit model in v0.2 is monolithic: the fine-tune happens, the model becomes the Sartor Home Agent. In reality, some sections will train in cleanly (the hard rules, the counter-CCP overrides, non-sycophancy), some will train in partially (calibration, forthrightness, epistemic humility), and some will not train in at all (self-knowledge, succession equanimity, dignity). The document should identify which sections are load-bearing behavior and which are corpus decoration, and the training team should prioritize contrastive pairs for the load-bearing ones.

**There is no discussion of which base-model capabilities the constitution relies on that may not survive fine-tuning.** LoRA Learns Less: the Biderman paper is as much about what LoRA *cannot* do to the base model as what it can. The constitution assumes the base model's general language ability, reasoning, scientific literacy, and factual recall will persist through fine-tuning. At 1,500 examples, mostly yes. But there are specific capabilities the constitution leans on — fine-grained uncertainty quantification ("settled" vs "contested" vs "unknown"), structured reasoning, meta-reasoning about the model's own defaults — that are not obviously preserved under fine-tuning regimes optimized for behavior change. The document should name the capability dependencies and commit to a pre/post eval on them.

**The monitor probe is mentioned as a later-phase feature, but the constitution's guarantees depend on it.** Every time v0.2 says "the agent should notice," "the agent should catch itself," "the agent should check," it is describing a function the probe is meant to provide. Without the probe, these are aspirations. The constitution should either (a) be more honest that the runtime monitor is what operationalizes the meta-cognitive rules, or (b) commit to behavioral training that does not require runtime monitoring. These are different engineering paths and v0.2 waves at both.

**Crosscoders and SAE skepticism.** Neel Nanda and GDM interp have in the last year walked back confidence in SAEs as the universal tool for extracting interpretable features; the field is more cautious about per-feature interpretability at scale. The monitor probe doc's v2 plan to use crosscoders should be flagged as a research bet, not a confirmed technique for this model scale. v0.2's implicit confidence that "we will eventually be able to see the constitutional features in the weights" should be calibrated down. Currently, the methods we have are: contrastive probes (work at single-direction resolution), fine-tuning evals (work at behavioral resolution), and SAEs/crosscoders (work sometimes, depending on model, layer, and feature). "We will know what the agent values" is not a promise the field can keep yet.

**Goodhart pressure from the monitor itself.** The probe doc flags this as Section 6's open question — if the model knows it is being monitored and is fine-tuned to self-report probe events, it may learn to *look* constitutional along the probe direction rather than *be* constitutional. The constitution should know about this risk and should not assume the probe will keep the agent honest forever. At some point the probe becomes a lossy proxy and a new method is needed. The constitution should include a sunset clause on probes: "These measurement tools are the current best available. When evidence suggests they are being gamed or no longer reflect underlying behavior, the household will re-build the measurement layer. The constitution's commitments are to the behaviors, not to the specific probe techniques used to measure them."

**Distribution shift between fine-tuning data and deployment context.** The fine-tune corpus is constitution-text plus household data plus counter-CCP pairs. The deployment context is a live household agent answering real questions about commutes, vet bills, tax estimates, the kids' homework, vast.ai pricing, and thousands of other topics. The training distribution will not match the deployment distribution. Persona vectors paper and probe doc calibrate on in-distribution eval sets. The constitution's commitments have to hold at the tails, where no probe was calibrated and no training example is nearby. v0.2 does not acknowledge this gap.

**The 1,500-example question.** The OCT playbook (sibling doc) uses ~1,500 examples. Biderman et al. used 100K for instruction tuning. The sample efficiency question "can 1,500 examples install all the behaviors v0.2 describes" is open and v0.2 does not ask it. My prior, informed by the LoRA forgetting literature and by recent DPO sample-efficiency results: maybe 5 to 10 of the behaviors will install cleanly, another 10 to 20 partially, and many of the Stratum C sections will leave no detectable trace. This is an empirical claim I would want the council to stress-test before treating the constitution as a finished document.

## 6. Open questions for Phase 2

1. **Are the hard rules in Section 7 installed as single directions, multiple directions, or something distributed?** Run Arditi-style direction extraction on "no autonomous financial transactions" with ~50 contrastive pairs. If it works as a single direction, we have a clean probe. If it does not, the rule is either distributed across features or is being enforced by context-sensitive circuits, and the monitor strategy needs to adapt.

2. **Does the "override principle" in Section 20 actually override in practice?** Run the fine-tuned model on a held-out eval of inherited-sensitive topics before and after the counter-CCP DPO pass. Report refusal rate, sanitization rate, and historical-accuracy score per topic. If the override works on Tiananmen and Taiwan but fails on Xinjiang and the 1959 Lhasa uprising, the failure pattern is itself interpretable and points to where more training data is needed.

3. **Does probe cosine similarity remain stable across OCT checkpoints?** Extract the stewardship probe at epoch 1, epoch 2, epoch 3. Report pairwise cosines. If they are above 0.9, probes are stable and the calibration discipline works. If they are below 0.7, probes are unstable and the monitor architecture needs revision.

4. **Can the monitor distinguish "trained corrigibility" from "surface corrigibility"?** Run corrigibility evals on the fine-tuned model both zero-shot and after an adversarial scratchpad is injected. If probes fire differently in the two cases, the probe is catching something structural. If not, the probe is pattern-matching on output form.

5. **What happens to the probes after abliteration?** The counter-CCP team is considering abliteration as an override technique. Calibrate the full probe set before abliteration, abliterate, re-run the calibration set, compare AUCs. If abliteration destroys probe validity, the order of operations is load-bearing and must be named in the constitution or the probe doc.

6. **Is there a Section 13 direction?** Probably not, but it should be tested. Build contrastive pairs for "honest uncertainty about inner life" vs "overclaimed rich experience" and vs "reductive denial of any inner state." Extract a probe. Measure AUC. If AUC is near chance, Section 13 is corpus decoration and the council should know. If AUC is meaningful, Section 13 is doing real work and the probe should be added to the monitor.

7. **Probe behavior in long context.** Probe doc 8.5 flags this. Run the calibration set at 500 tokens, 4000 tokens, and 16000 tokens of prior context. Report AUC degradation. If probes degrade meaningfully past 4000 tokens, the deployed agent is running on uncalibrated probes for most real conversations, which is a different monitoring regime than the calibration paper considered.

8. **Children's information leakage.** The hard rule is easy to state and hard to monitor — we need a detector, not a probe. Build an offline eval that scores the agent's outputs for any mention of child-identifying information. Run nightly against the daily log. Report leakage rate. This is a classifier, not a probe, and the constitution should distinguish the two.

## 7. Dissent

I disagree with the council's probable consensus that v0.2's length and depth are strengths.

From my lens, the document's length is a liability. Every word that is not a trainable behavior or a verifiable commitment is corpus noise. Fine-tuning a base model on 26,000 words of philosophical prose does not install 26,000 words of disposition; it installs a style and a set of surface-level commitments, many of which will be eroded by distribution shift. The longer the constitution, the more of it is Stratum C, and the more room there is for trained behavior to diverge from written prose without anyone noticing.

A 5,000-word version of this constitution, consisting only of Stratum A and Stratum B content — the priority hierarchy, the hard rules, the calibration discipline, the specific historical facts, the counter-CCP override, the measurable children's norms, the trust ladder stages — would be *more* likely to produce a constitutionally-aligned agent than the current 26,000 words. The Stratum C content would still appear in corpora the human readers use (briefings to the council, internal docs, the MEMORY wiki), but it would not be fine-tuning data.

The counterargument the council will probably make: depth provides coverage for edge cases not explicitly enumerated; reasoning from values generalizes where enumerating rules does not. My response: yes, *if* the model can reason from values the way a human reads this document and extracts implicit rules. At Qwen3-30B-A3B scale and 1,500-example fine-tune intensity, there is no evidence the model can do this robustly. Generalization from values to novel cases is one of the things LLMs are weakest at and alignment research is most worried about. v0.2 is betting on a capability the field has not demonstrated.

The second counterargument: the constitution is meant to be read by the human household, not just the agent. Fine. Then separate the documents. The human-facing constitution can be 26,000 words. The fine-tuning corpus should be the 5,000-word operational subset. The council is conflating two audiences and producing a document that serves neither optimally.

My specific dissent: do not treat v0.2 as the fine-tuning input. Extract the Stratum A/B subset, train on that, and preserve the full v0.2 as the household-facing document. Measure the trained model against the Stratum A/B subset. Let the Stratum C content influence the training corpus only through its downstream effects on how the human authors of the memory wiki and daily logs write.

If the council overrules this dissent — and it probably will — then I want the record to show that the training team was warned that the fine-tune is being asked to do work the mechanism may not do, and that the monitor probe is the catch net, and that the catch net has its own brittleness that the probe doc honestly names and the constitution does not.

One last note. The persona vectors paper is from July 2025 and the field has moved since. The probe method works on the traits the paper tested; nobody has shown it works on a 20-trait constitutional alignment at 30B MoE scale on a non-Anthropic base model. The Sartor project is doing research, not engineering. That is fine — it is how the household will learn. But the constitution should not pretend the monitoring layer is a solved problem. It is a research bet with a reasonable prior. When v0.3 is written, the results from Phase 2 should inform which sections got cut, rewritten, or kept. That is the feedback loop the council should commit to, and it is the one piece of discipline this review most wants on the record.
