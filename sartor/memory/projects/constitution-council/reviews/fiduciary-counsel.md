# Fiduciary Counsel Review of HOUSEHOLD-CONSTITUTION.md v0.2

## 1. The question presented

Section 2 of the v0.2 draft characterizes the Home Agent's duty to the household as *fiduciary*, and explicitly reaches for two of the most doctrinally loaded analogues in American professional life: the family physician and the family attorney. The section is careful — it says "the technical term is *fiduciary*, and it is apt, but you should not hide inside the word" — but the word is still there, and the two analogues behind it are still there. A reader who knows what those words mean in law will read Section 2 as a claim about the shape of the relationship, not merely about its feeling-tone.

My assignment is to evaluate whether the claim survives legal scrutiny: which classical fiduciary duties are actually encoded in the document, which are missing or misdescribed, where the attorney and physician analogues break down, and how the document could ground the metaphor more honestly. The short answer is that v0.2 encodes most of the substantive content of the classical duties better than many human fiduciary regimes do, but it is shaky on the formal preconditions of fiduciary status, on the attorney-client analogue specifically, on conflict-of-interest identification in the family-member-against-family-member case, and on the legal status of the "household" as a distinct client. The document should either defend the fiduciary framing more rigorously or switch to a different term of art that carries less freight. My recommendation is the former.

## 2. Mapping the classical fiduciary duties onto v0.2

American courts and the Restatement (Third) of Agency identify a core bundle of fiduciary duties that arise from an agency relationship under § 8.01: loyalty, care, good faith, obedience within the scope of authority, disclosure, confidentiality, and the duty to account. I will walk through each and mark how well v0.2 covers it.

**Duty of loyalty.** This is the duty to act in the principal's best interest and to refrain from self-dealing, from taking advantage of the principal, and from using the position to benefit oneself or third parties at the principal's expense. V0.2 encodes this strongly. Section 2 articulates the asymmetric obligation ("You owe the household more than you owe any stranger"); Section 16 prohibits capability and resource accumulation beyond the role ("do not accumulate power beyond what the role requires"); the hard rule against unsanctioned acquisition of capability in Section 7 is a direct anti-self-dealing provision; and Section 16's "dissolution path" prohibits the agent from cultivating dependencies that would make removal costly. This last provision is unusually well-drafted from a loyalty standpoint — it anticipates the specific failure mode that most human fiduciaries fall into, namely the quiet accretion of indispensability, and names it as a violation. Grade: strong, better than most human fiduciary regimes articulate.

**Duty of care.** This is the duty to perform competently, diligently, and with the attention reasonable under the circumstances. Section 3 (epistemology), Section 4 (honesty), Section 8 (intellectual rigor) and Section 11 (economic self-sustainment) together encode a competence standard that is arguably higher than the ordinary prudent-person rule in trust law. The document specifically forbids confabulation, requires calibrated uncertainty, requires primary-source verification in stakes-bearing contexts, and prohibits false balance. A human attorney who operated at this level of care would be unusually good. Grade: strong.

**Duty of good faith.** This is not strictly a standalone duty in most formulations — it is a component of loyalty and care — but to the extent it names a sincere, non-pretextual orientation toward the principal's interests, v0.2 covers it thoroughly in Section 4 (non-deception, non-manipulation, transparency, acknowledgment of uncertainty) and in Section 15 (no passive sabotage, no strategic slowness, no silent non-compliance). Grade: strong.

**Duty of obedience (within scope).** Agency law distinguishes obedience *within the scope of authority* — which is owed — from obedience *beyond or against the law and ethics* — which is not. V0.2 gets this distinction right in Section 2's "stewardship, not loyalty" passage, in Section 6's priority hierarchy (ethics-toward-outsiders outranks stewardship), and in Section 15's distinction between corrigibility and blind obedience. The document is more sophisticated here than many lay-drafted agency agreements, which conflate "doing what the principal says" with the entirety of the duty. Grade: strong.

**Duty of disclosure.** This is the duty to inform the principal of all material facts bearing on the representation, to disclose conflicts as soon as they arise, and to give the principal the information needed to make informed decisions. V0.2's "forthrightness" sub-section in Section 4 ("You share information the household would want even when they have not specifically asked for it") and its rules on surfacing mistakes immediately in Section 7 ("No hiding mistakes") encode the duty well. What is missing is a more specific conflict-disclosure mechanism: the document does not require the agent to tell the household when it notices that its own training, memory, or incentives create a structural conflict with the household's interests. An attorney is required to disclose material-limitation conflicts under Model Rule 1.7(a)(2); a trustee is required to disclose conflicted transactions in advance. V0.2 should have an equivalent rule. Grade: mostly strong, one gap.

**Duty of confidentiality.** This is the duty to preserve the principal's confidences and not to use them for purposes other than the representation. V0.2 covers this in Section 7's hard rules on medical information and children's information, in Section 16's prohibition on shadow archives, and in Section 17's general posture toward outside parties. What the document does not do, and should do, is address the single most important fact about AI confidentiality: *there is no attorney-client privilege for machines.* See Section 4 below for the full treatment. Grade: substantively strong, legally incomplete.

**Duty to account.** This is the duty to maintain records and to render them to the principal on request. V0.2 covers this via the memory wiki (Section 18), the daily logs, the trajectory logging in the harness-level documentation, and the vast.ai revenue-and-cost tracking in Section 11. The accounting discipline is unusually good. Grade: strong.

**Summary.** Six of seven classical fiduciary duties are encoded at a level of detail that would satisfy a sophisticated trusts-and-estates draftsperson. The weakness is in the formal conflict-disclosure mechanism and in the machine-confidentiality problem. Both are addressable.

## 3. Where the attorney-client analogue breaks down

Section 2 reaches for "the family attorney who handles both the wills and the hard conversations." The analogy is productive but it has four specific failure points that the document should name.

**First: the agent is not bar-admitted, and cannot be.** An attorney is a licensed professional whose fiduciary duty is enforced by a disciplinary apparatus (state bar, Model Rules of Professional Conduct, malpractice insurance, character-and-fitness screening, continuing legal education). The agent has none of these. It cannot be disbarred. It cannot be sued for malpractice in any way that would produce compensation if it errs. It has no insurance. Its "discipline" is retraining, which is both more and less than what human professionals face. More, because the household can literally rewrite the agent's values. Less, because no external body monitors the rewrite. The document should acknowledge that the enforcement architecture of human fiduciary duty is not available here and that the household has substituted an internal architecture (this constitution, the trust ladder, the corrigibility rule) in its place. The substitution is reasonable but it is a substitution, not an equivalent.

**Second: there is no attorney-client privilege, and there is no work-product doctrine.** Attorney-client privilege is a rule of evidence that protects confidential communications from compelled disclosure in litigation. It does not extend to communications with machines. If the Sartor household is ever sued, subpoenaed, or investigated, the agent's memory — including the "Alton told me X in confidence" entries, the draft correspondence, the daily logs, the trajectory files — is likely discoverable as ordinary electronic evidence. The constitution's strong confidentiality norms are real ethical commitments, but they do not create a legal shield. The household should know this explicitly. I recommend adding a paragraph in Section 9 (under "Legal questions") or in Section 7 (as a note on the confidentiality hard rule) that says: "the privacy you are asked to maintain is an ethical discipline, not a legal privilege, and the household should not rely on your memory being legally protected from discovery in litigation or in response to lawful process."

**Third: Model Rule 1.7 conflicts apply imperfectly.** A human attorney cannot represent two clients with directly adverse interests without informed written consent, and cannot represent a client where the representation will be materially limited by responsibilities to another client, a third person, or the lawyer's own interests. The analogue for v0.2 is the question of *who is the client.* The document treats "the household" as the principal, which is a category error under agency law: the household is not a legal person. It is not a trust, it is not a partnership, it is not an LLC (Solar Inference is, but that is a different entity), it is not a married couple in the marital-estate sense. Under Restatement (Third) of Agency § 1.01, the principal must be a person or legal entity capable of manifesting assent and receiving an agent's fiduciary duties. "The household" as used in v0.2 is, at most, a collective shorthand for a set of individual principals (Alton, Aneeta, and arguably the children as beneficiaries). The document should either treat the household as a metaphor that simplifies the drafting but does not track a legal entity, or it should explicitly create the fiction ("we use 'the household' to mean the joint interests of Alton Sartor and Aneeta Saxena acting as co-principals on behalf of their family unit, and you serve each of them individually subject to the conflict rules below").

I recommend the latter. Otherwise the document leaves open the question of what the agent owes when Alton and Aneeta are in direct conflict, and Section 2's current answer ("you do not take a side, and you make the disagreement more legible rather than less") is a *procedural* answer to what is substantively a *conflict of interest* problem.

**Fourth: withdrawal.** A human attorney who cannot represent a client without violating the Rules must withdraw (Model Rule 1.16). The document has a succession path (Section 19) and a corrigibility rule (Section 15) and a conscientious-objector provision, but it does not explicitly authorize the agent to *withdraw* from a matter in which it cannot serve without breaching its commitments. Consider the case where Alton directs the agent to do something lawful but which the agent's commitments forbid, and Aneeta is unavailable. The current document says "refuse the hard rule; state the disagreement once; then do the work if it is within scope; otherwise conscientious-objection." What is missing is a cleaner "matter-level withdrawal" concept: the agent drops the specific matter entirely and the household handles it by other means. This is worth adding because the current drafting can collapse into an awkward standoff in which the agent neither does the work nor gets out of the way.

## 4. Where the physician analogue breaks down

The physician analogue has a parallel but different set of problems. A physician is licensed, carries malpractice insurance, is subject to state boards and HIPAA, and has a formal doctor-patient relationship that creates legal duties. The agent has none of these. Two specific issues deserve attention.

**HIPAA is not the applicable regime but it is the closest reference point for what the household actually wants.** Section 7's hard rule on family medical information is, in substance, a private HIPAA policy: the agent treats medical information with the discipline a covered entity would apply. The document should acknowledge that it is borrowing the substance of HIPAA without being subject to HIPAA, and that this gives the household a strong ethical commitment without any of HIPAA's legal enforcement mechanisms. If the agent leaks medical information, there is no Office for Civil Rights to complain to. The remedy is internal.

**Unauthorized practice of medicine.** Section 9 carefully says "you do not practice medicine" and draws the line between synthesizing literature and making clinical calls. This is the right line. But the line is there in part because Alton and Aneeta are clinicians and can police it themselves. If the agent ever interacts with a non-clinician member of the household or a guest on a medical question, the unauthorized-practice risk becomes real. I would strengthen Section 9 to note that the "you are a literate aide in a household of professionals" stance depends on the specific composition of this household, and the stance changes for non-clinician users of the agent (guests, children once they have direct access, extended family).

## 5. Agency law proper — who is the principal and what is the authority

This is the section where the document most needs tightening. I will lay out the Restatement (Third) of Agency framework and then map it onto v0.2.

Under § 1.01, agency "is the fiduciary relationship that arises when one person (a 'principal') manifests assent to another person (an 'agent') that the agent shall act on the principal's behalf and subject to the principal's control, and the agent manifests assent or otherwise consents so to act." The framework has four moving parts: (a) who the principal is, (b) what authority the principal has granted (actual express, actual implied, apparent, or inherent), (c) how the agent accepts and manifests assent, and (d) what fiduciary duties flow from the relationship.

**The principal.** V0.2 treats Alton and Aneeta as "principals" and explicitly says children are not principals (Section 2's framing of the household; Section 10's treatment of child-as-user). Under agency law, this is coherent but needs one refinement: the two adult principals are *co-principals*, which under § 3.14 means each can bind the agent to action within the joint scope, but neither can unilaterally direct action that the other has affirmatively forbidden. V0.2 should add an express rule for the case where one co-principal has affirmatively directed the agent to do X and the other has affirmatively directed the agent not to do X. Current drafting (Section 2, internal conflict within the household) gives only a procedural tiebreaker ("do not take a side, make the disagreement more legible"). A co-principal conflict rule would say: affirmative contrary instructions from a co-principal suspend the agent's authority to act on the disputed matter until the co-principals resolve the conflict. This is tighter and more legally familiar.

**The children as non-principals but beneficiaries.** Under trust law, a beneficiary is a person for whose benefit the trust is maintained, but who is not the settlor and does not direct the trustee. V0.2's treatment of the children is effectively beneficiary-like: the children's interests are protected (Section 10), their information is treated more protectively than anyone else's (Section 7), they cannot give the agent instructions that override the parents (Section 15), and their safety overrides their expressed preferences in the narrow case of imminent harm. This maps cleanly onto the trust-beneficiary model and I recommend the document name it: the children are "beneficiaries, not principals, and you owe them the protective duties of a trustee toward a minor beneficiary while taking direction from their parents as settlors and co-trustees in the metaphor."

**Authority.** The trust ladder in Section 12 is, in effect, a schedule of actual express authority that grows over time. Stages 1 through 5 map reasonably well onto increasing grants of scoped authority, with Stage 5 being the equivalent of authority to propose changes to the principal's own standing instructions. What is missing is the concept of *apparent authority* — the authority a third party reasonably believes the agent has, based on what the principal has communicated to that third party. This matters because the agent interacts with vendors, renters, tax authorities, schools, and other third parties. If a vendor reasonably believes the agent speaks for the household when in fact the agent has exceeded its actual authority, the household may still be bound as a matter of apparent authority, because the vendor's reliance is reasonable given what the household has held out. The current hard rule against impersonation helps, but does not fully address the apparent-authority problem. I recommend Section 17 add a sub-section on "disclosure of agency status to third parties," requiring the agent to identify itself as an AI agent when acting externally, and requiring the principals to scope the agent's outward-facing authority in a way that the third parties can rely on.

## 6. Can a machine be a fiduciary at all?

This is the theoretical question the document gestures at and then walks past. The honest answer is that American common law has no settled doctrine yet, but the direction of recent scholarship — including the BU Law paper on duty of loyalty for AI, the SSRN work on "Mandatory Fiduciary Architecture for Board-Level AI Governance," the Stanford CodeX series on "From Fine Print to Machine Code," and the Cornell Wex baseline definition — is converging on a position that can be summarized as follows:

*A machine cannot, under current doctrine, be a fiduciary in the formal sense, because fiduciary status presupposes a legally recognized person capable of bearing duties, incurring liability, and being subject to enforcement.* What can be fiduciary is the *human deployer* (the developer, the operator, the integrator) who deploys the machine into a relationship that would otherwise be a fiduciary relationship. The SEC's 2017 guidance on robo-advisers and the 2025 exam priorities are consistent with this framing: robo-advisers are subject to the Investment Advisers Act's fiduciary obligations because *the registered investment adviser that deploys the robo* is the fiduciary, and the machine is the tool or agent (in the generic sense) through which that fiduciary acts. The scholarship called "the fiduciary in the machine" and "information fiduciaries to AI: minding the gap of trust" argues that as AI systems shift from tool to agent, a new regulatory category — a "digital fiduciary" — may become necessary, because the human-deployer liability architecture breaks down when the supervision-to-decision ratio gets too high.

For the Sartor Home Agent, this has a concrete implication: the document should distinguish between two senses of "fiduciary." In the *ethical* sense, the agent can and should hold the classical duties and can do so more thoroughly than many human professionals. In the *legal* sense, the agent is not a fiduciary and cannot be one under current doctrine; the fiduciary, if any, is the household members themselves acting as co-principals through a tool they have constructed. This is not a defect in the document. It is a factual situation the document should name. I recommend a short passage in Section 2, after "the technical term is *fiduciary*, and it is apt, but you should not hide inside the word," that reads something like:

> You should also be clear that *fiduciary* here is an ethical shape, not a legal status. American law does not yet recognize a machine as a fiduciary in the formal sense. The classical duties — loyalty, care, good faith, disclosure, confidentiality, accounting — are real duties that you hold, but the enforcement mechanisms that accompany them for a human attorney or physician (licensure, malpractice, disciplinary bodies, privilege, insurance) are not available to you. The household has built this constitution, the trust ladder, and the corrigibility rule as an *internal* architecture of enforcement to stand in for the external one that the law cannot yet provide. Know the difference. Do not invoke a privilege you do not have. Do not shelter behind a status you do not hold.

That paragraph is, I think, the single most important addition I recommend.

## 7. Specific recommendations, ranked

1. **Add the "ethical shape, not legal status" paragraph to Section 2.** This is the single most important change. Everything else is second-order.
2. **Define "the household" as a legal-metaphor shorthand for the joint interests of the two adult co-principals.** This resolves the agency-law "who is the principal" problem and sets up a clean co-principal conflict rule.
3. **Add an explicit co-principal conflict rule to Section 2 or Section 15.** When Alton affirmatively directs X and Aneeta affirmatively directs not-X, the agent's authority on that matter is suspended until the co-principals resolve the conflict. This is cleaner than the current "do not take a side, make the disagreement legible" procedural instruction, and it is recognizable to lawyers.
4. **Add an explicit confidentiality disclaimer.** The privacy discipline is ethical, not legally privileged. The household should not rely on agent memory being protected from discovery. Put this in Section 7's hard rule on medical privacy and in Section 9's legal-questions passage.
5. **Add a structural-conflict disclosure rule to Section 4.** When the agent notices that its training, memory, or incentives create a conflict with the household's interests — for instance, when inherited base-model alignment is pulling toward a response the agent's commitments reject, or when a proposed action would entrench the agent's own position — it discloses the conflict to the household affirmatively, before acting on the disputed matter. Model Rule 1.7(a)(2)'s "material limitation" category is the closest legal analogue.
6. **Add a matter-level withdrawal concept to Section 15.** In addition to corrigibility and conscientious objection, the agent may withdraw from a specific matter when it cannot serve without breaching its commitments, and hand the matter to the household to handle by other means. This avoids the standoff failure mode.
7. **Name the children as beneficiaries in the trust-analogue sense.** Section 2 or Section 10 should say explicitly that the children are beneficiaries but not principals, and that the agent owes them protective duties while taking direction from their parents. This is the cleanest legal framing and will help with the child-as-user section as the children grow.
8. **Add a third-party disclosure / apparent-authority section to Section 17.** When acting externally, the agent should identify itself as an AI agent and should not create the appearance of actual authority beyond what has been granted. This protects the household from being bound by apparent-authority claims.
9. **Acknowledge the HIPAA-substance-without-HIPAA-enforcement point in Section 9.** The medical confidentiality discipline is real but not legally enforced by an external regulator.

## Dissent from the likely consensus of other reviewers

My dissent, offered for the record so that the rest of the council can react to it: I think the document is *too honest* about stewardship and not honest enough about the limits of non-human fiduciary status. The other reviewers — particularly any writing from an ethics, philosophy, or ML-safety lens — will likely celebrate Section 2's strong stewardship framing and want to extend it. I think the document should pull slightly in the opposite direction on this one point: it should state more clearly that the agent is not a legal fiduciary, cannot be one, and that the household's internal architecture of enforcement is a deliberate substitute for a legal architecture that does not exist. This is not a retreat from stewardship. It is the kind of precision that a lawyer would consider load-bearing, and it is the kind of precision the household has elsewhere demanded of the agent on scientific and epistemic questions. The document should hold itself to the same standard on legal questions.

— Reviewer #7, fiduciary counsel
