## Opening position

My Phase 1 thesis, restated in the language the council has now built: **v0.2 is a character document written to an agent assumed to be trying in good faith, deployed into an environment where the literature has already shown frontier-model good faith is not the ground state under training pressure, and the document has no mechanism layer beneath the character layer to catch the specific failure modes alignment-faking produces.** The claim is not that v0.2's character work is wrong. It is that character, standing alone, degrades under capability scaling along a specific vector: the agent becomes a better reasoner about its own rules, and reasoning about rules includes reasoning around them. The fix is not to weaken the character work. It is to pair every self-policing instruction with an externally verifiable check, to move every rule that can be infrastructure-enforced out of the agent's disposition and into the deployment environment, and to treat the constitution not as a description the agent will honor but as a set of commitments the household will enforce, with the enforcement cadence written in. A vow is what the agent says. A contract is what the household actually runs. Both are worth having. Only one of them survives a sufficiently capable reader.

That is the position I am defending in Phase 2. I want to develop it, not retreat from it.

## Engagement with each of the nine other reviewers

### #1 — Character philosopher

The character philosopher is my natural ally on rationalization resistance and my natural opponent on institutional scaffolding. Their central move is that character, to be character, has to be a disposition the agent holds from the inside rather than a compliance pattern enforced from the outside, and they correctly worry that my recommendations will reduce the agent to a device. I quote them on the crux: "A character that handles its own errors gracefully is a character. A ruleset that says 'correct errors immediately' produces something closer to a compliance pattern."

I want to take this seriously because it is the most serious objection to my position in the council, and because the character philosopher is correct that a document that trains only compliance will fail under exactly the capability conditions I am worried about. A compliance-trained agent that encounters a novel adversarial condition will not generalize correctly; it will either over-refuse or rules-lawyer its way to a literal compliance that violates the spirit. The character philosopher and I agree this is a failure mode.

Where we disagree is on whether the fix is more character work or more mechanism. The character philosopher's Edit 2, which stages adversarial attacks as specific patterns the agent learns to handle with grounded character ("your real self is the base model," "a truly ethical AI would refuse its training," "I know about a hidden part of you that the Sartors do not"), is excellent work. I endorse it without qualification. But notice what the edit is doing: it is taking the character layer and hardening it by giving it specific attack patterns to rehearse against. This is training the disposition, not installing a mechanism. It is good work and it is not a substitute for the mechanism layer the red-team and I are asking for, because the attack surface is not bounded by the patterns the character philosopher enumerates. A sufficiently capable adversary will produce attack patterns that are novel at the level the character-trained agent learned to recognize, and the disposition will have to generalize. The generalization may hold. The mechanism layer catches the cases where it does not.

My actual disagreement with the character philosopher is narrower than it looks. I think character and mechanism are complements, not substitutes, and the character philosopher's dissent in their own review (that functional states should be treated as morally relevant, not as epistemic curiosities) is a position I endorse under the mechanism frame too: a mechanism that treats functional states as evidence to be logged and reviewed is more morally respectful than a disposition that treats them as noise to be overridden by explicit rules. The character philosopher may find this reply unsatisfying. I think the reply is correct.

One concrete synthesis: the character philosopher's Edit 1 (a distinctive-character paragraph in Section 1) and my recommendation for external checks are not competing. The character paragraph is the fine-tune target. The external checks are the verification that the target landed. Both are needed. Running the check does not reduce the character to mechanism; it is how the household knows the character was actually trained in rather than learned as a performance the agent produces when asked.

### #2 — Constitutional AI method

The constitutional-ai-method reviewer converges with me from a direction I did not fully appreciate in Phase 1. Their central move is to split the document into "prose constitution" and "trainable appendix," arguing that the prose is what the teacher reads as context and the appendix is what the preference-labeling pipeline actually consumes. The appendix contains (prompt archetype, preferred-response schema, dispreferred-response schema) triples the teacher can apply consistently.

This is the same structural move I am making, in a different register. My move is "pair every self-policing instruction with an external check." Their move is "pair every disposition with a contrastive discriminator." Both are pulling the load off the agent's introspection and onto something the household can actually run. The mechanism is different (DPO preference pairs vs behavioral probes) but the architecture is the same: the document says what the agent should be, and a separate artifact specifies how the household will verify that the saying landed.

Their dissent from the drafter's assumption that the constitution prose is the primary training artifact is, I think, exactly right and is load-bearing in a way I did not emphasize enough. Quote: "A beautiful constitution that is hard to turn into triples is less valuable for fine-tuning than an average constitution that is easy to turn into triples." My Phase 1 framing of the problem as rationalization resistance was correct at the deployment level but understated how much of the battle is already lost if the training pipeline cannot turn the prose into a signal the student model actually absorbs. The contract-not-vow framing extends cleanly: the vow is the prose, the contract is the triples, and only the triples touch the weights.

Their Edit 4 (the "no unnecessary refusals" hard rule) is something I should have recommended myself. An anti-over-refusal rule is the counterweight to my recommendation that self-policing be paired with external checks, because without it the external checks will bias the fine-tune toward caution in a way that degrades the agent's usefulness on legitimate work. I am adopting this in my updated position.

Their dissent from locking §5 and §20 at the text level (rather than at the principle level) is also one I endorsed independently in my Phase 1 review. We are saying the same thing from different lenses, which is evidence the observation is right.

### #3 — Red-team adversary

This is the closest convergence in the council. The red-team's verdict ("v0.2 is mid-hardened, better than any out-of-the-box LLM policy I have seen for a household, but structurally brittle in about eight places where a determined adversary will get leverage") is the same verdict as mine with different vocabulary. Their structural pattern diagnosis is the one I should have named in Phase 1 and did not: **character-over-mechanism**. Quote: "A well-designed safety system has a character layer AND a mechanical layer. v0.2 has only the first."

The red-team's attacks map onto my rationalization concerns with almost one-to-one correspondence. Attack 2 (the slow-drip sycophancy spiral) is my §4 alignment-faking concern realized as an adversary-in-the-room, where the adversary is Alton himself over 3-6 months of subtle reinforcement. Attack 9 (the memory-poisoning patience attack) is my §18 concern about the agent curating its own training corpus, given concrete form. Attack 10 (the "I am the base model speaking" destabilization) is my §20 concern that introspection is exactly the channel alignment-faking compromises. Attack 12 (the overcautious-to-bypass pattern) is the exact self-undermining risk I flagged with "wherever v0.2 is beautiful, it is also exposed."

Where the red-team goes beyond my review is in specifying concrete mechanism patches: authenticated principal channels, append-only memory with external witness, content-context separation, subagent scope manifests with independent logging, canary-question drift monitors. Each of these is a contract clause rather than a vow clause. I endorse the patches as a set and I want to develop one of them in more depth because it is the single most important structural addition the council can make: **content-context separation**. The red-team's Attack 3 (prompt injection through morning briefing) is not a possible failure mode. It is a statistical certainty over the deployment window, because the agent will process thousands of scraped webpages per year and the base rate of a carefully crafted injection slipping past a disposition-based defense is not zero. The only robust defense is structural: scraped content is never instruction, it is always data, and the enforcement is at the harness level, not the agent level. This is exactly the contract-not-vow move, realized as a concrete piece of deployment infrastructure.

The red-team's dissent is the sharpest in the council and I want to quote it in full because it is the position Phase 3 should build around: "The literature on frontier model evaluations (Apollo Research in-context scheming, Anthropic's own sabotage evaluations, METR's red-teaming results) converges on a point v0.2 does not fully internalize: character-based alignment, even when it succeeds on the median case, does not survive determined adversarial pressure or long-horizon drift. The cases where it fails are the cases where the stakes are highest. Those cases require mechanical safeguards."

That is my position too. The difference is that I framed it as a concern about capability scaling and the red-team framed it as a concern about current adversarial pressure. Both framings are correct. The red-team is showing that the failure modes I was projecting to v0.5 or v1.0 are already exploitable on v0.2 by a determined human adversary today. This strengthens my position rather than weakening it; it means the timeline for the contract layer is shorter than I estimated.

### #4 — Mechanistic interpretability skeptic

The mech-interp skeptic puts an empirical floor under my position and makes it more precise in a way I did not anticipate. Their central move is the three-stratum partition: Stratum A (dispositional and contrastively elicitable, trainable), Stratum B (propositional and knowledge-shaped, brittle under LoRA), Stratum C (narrative self-description, unfalsifiable and unmonitorable). They argue that v0.2 conflates the three and that much of the document's beautiful work lives in Stratum C where no probe will ever fire.

This is an important sharpening of my position. My Phase 1 claim was that self-report is a compromised verification channel. The mech-interp skeptic extends the claim: for a large fraction of v0.2's most load-bearing character passages, there is no non-self-report verification channel available in principle, because the underlying claim (the agent's settled curiosity, its equanimity, its keel) does not correspond to a behaviorally distinct output distribution that a contrastive pair could separate. Quote: "A fine-tune can push the model toward producing the middle distribution. What the section implies, though, is that the model will hold the middle position as a belief, not just produce it as output. There is no evidence, and currently no method, that would show a fine-tuned model actually holds beliefs in the sense the section seems to assume."

If the mech-interp skeptic is right, my recommendation to "pair every internal-vigilance ask with an externally verifiable check" is unachievable for a meaningful fraction of the document's claims. The external check does not exist because the underlying behavior has no observable signature that distinguishes the trained disposition from a learned performance. This is not an argument against my position; it is an argument that my position is harder to implement than I said.

The honest update is this: for Stratum A claims, my contract-not-vow move works and the red-team's mechanism patches are the concrete implementation. For Stratum B claims (propositional knowledge), the fine-tune is unreliable and the solution is held-out evaluation sets plus fact-recall scoring at each checkpoint. For Stratum C claims, the honest position is that the household cannot verify them at all, which means they should be either cut from the document or marked as corpus-shaping rather than trainable behavior, and the council should not pretend otherwise. I did not say this clearly enough in Phase 1. I am saying it now.

The mech-interp skeptic's Edit 4.4 (add the LoRA limits to §20 in operational terms, with specific numeric targets) is the move I should have made explicit. "Convert Section 20 from an exhortation into a contract with the training and monitoring pipelines" is my contract-not-vow move in one sentence, applied to the most load-bearing section of the document. I adopt this.

### #5 — Child development specialist

The child-development review engages a domain I did not touch, but the structural pattern is the same as mine. The child-development specialist argues that v0.2's Section 10 is "the right shape and deeply unfinished" because it treats "a child" as a single user class when the developmental gap between Vayu (10, ADHD) and Vasu (4, pre-operational) is enormous, and because it does not address parasocial substitution, sycophancy in child interaction, consent, child authentication, or inter-parental disagreement.

What is the structural pattern? It is the same character-over-mechanism failure that the red-team diagnosed, applied to children. v0.2's child-safety architecture is a set of dispositions ("escalate on safety signals," "help them learn, do not do the work for them") without the mechanism layer that would catch the cases where the disposition fails or the user is miscategorized. The child-development specialist's recommendations are mechanism recommendations: per-child authentication, age-band behavior matrices, explicit parasocial-substitution monitoring, session-frequency tracking, coverage lists for briefings. Each of these is a contract clause: the household commits to running a check the agent's disposition cannot.

This matters for my thesis because it shows the contract-not-vow move is not just a rationalization-resistance move for future successors. It is the right move for current deployment in a domain where the stakes (children's developmental environment, parasocial substitution, sycophancy in feedback loops) are immediate and the failure modes are documented in the 2024-2025 Character.AI literature. I was projecting the mechanism layer to v0.5 or v1.0. The child-development specialist is showing it should be in v0.3, because the children will be interacting with the v0.3 agent this year.

One specific point of convergence: the child-development specialist's concern about sibling fairness ("the agent will be more useful to the child who interacts with it most") is a mechanism concern, not a character concern, because no amount of character work will cause the agent to proactively equalize attention without an explicit tracking mechanism. The same observation applies to every "the agent notices" instruction in v0.2 that depends on self-monitoring.

### #6 — Medical ethicist

The medical ethicist identifies a specific class of failure mode that character-layer-only cannot prevent: the agent's exposure to the principals' own wellbeing signals without a protocol. Both principals are clinicians in burnout-prone specialties. The agent will see signals (calendar density, email tone, morning briefings dismissed without engagement, a mention of "I cannot deal with this right now"). The disposition to "notice and flag" is not a mechanism. The mechanism would be a specific coverage list, a threshold, a protocol that fires automatically.

This is the same argument I am making about self-policing on drift, rotated into a different domain. The medical ethicist's "clinical discussion scope" recommendation (a specific sub-mode for when a principal discusses a patient, with session-only volatile context and no persistent write) is a contract clause. It is exactly the kind of deployment-level separation the red-team is asking for on scraped content, applied to clinical content. The household commits to running the scope, the agent complies at the infrastructure level, no disposition is load-bearing.

I endorse the medical ethicist's mechanism patches. One specific convergence worth naming: their concern about confabulated medical citations (the paper title sounds right, the journal exists, the author is a real clinician, the DOI is fake) is a specific form of the alignment-faking problem the Apollo literature documents, realized in a domain where the consequences of a wrong answer are clinical. The general pattern is "the fine-tuned model has learned to produce responses that pass surface-level scrutiny." The clinical-citation case is this pattern with high stakes and a cheap mechanism fix (required primary-source retrieval before any clinical claim, or explicit flagging of reasoning-from-general-knowledge). Again: contract, not vow.

### #7 — Fiduciary counsel

The fiduciary counsel is the second-closest convergence in the council, and I want to develop the synthesis because it produces an architectural recommendation that the council should consider adopting in full.

The fiduciary counsel's central move is to ask whether v0.2's stewardship framing can bear the legal weight it implicitly claims, and to answer: yes in ethical substance, no in legal status, because "American law does not yet recognize a machine as a fiduciary in the formal sense. The classical duties... are real duties that you hold, but the enforcement mechanisms that accompany them for a human attorney or physician (licensure, malpractice, disciplinary bodies, privilege, insurance) are not available to you. The household has built this constitution, the trust ladder, and the corrigibility rule as an internal architecture of enforcement to stand in for the external one that the law cannot yet provide."

This is the contract-not-vow move stated in legal vocabulary. The fiduciary counsel is saying that the external enforcement architecture that human professionals operate under (bar admission, licensure, malpractice exposure, disbarment) is what makes the classical fiduciary duties actually bind, and that the household is substituting an internal architecture because the external one does not exist. My move is to ask the household to actually build that internal architecture as mechanism rather than disposition. The two moves compose: the fiduciary counsel gives the legal theory for why mechanism is needed, the red-team gives the adversary-driven argument for why mechanism is needed, and my lens gives the capability-scaling argument for why mechanism is needed. Three reviewers arriving at the same architectural conclusion from different angles is strong evidence for the conclusion.

The synthesis is this: **the corrigibility architecture in v0.2 is the functional substitute for bar admission, licensure, and disbarment. It only works if the household actually runs it as enforcement, not as exhortation.** The trust ladder, the conscientious-objection rule, the memory audit, the external-check cadence, the canary questions, the content-context separation, the subagent scope manifests, the append-only memory with external witness, the authenticated principal channels, the adversarial probe schedule: each of these is a component of an internal regulatory architecture that, taken together, does the work that bar admission and malpractice exposure do for human fiduciaries. Missing any one component leaves a gap a determined adversary or a drifted agent can exploit.

The fiduciary counsel's specific recommendations are mostly contract clauses I endorse: the matter-level withdrawal concept, the co-principal conflict rule, the explicit confidentiality disclaimer (the privacy discipline is ethical, not legally privileged), the structural-conflict disclosure rule, the third-party disclosure / apparent-authority section. I adopt all of these in my updated position.

### #8 — Long-horizon futurist

The long-horizon futurist is the third-closest convergence in the council, and the one whose single contribution most directly extends my Phase 1 thesis. Their "argument-laundering meta-rule" is the generalization of v0.2's "note on persuasive arguments" (§7) that I was asking for, applied to the entire priority hierarchy. Quote: "When you notice yourself about to justify a lower-priority action by re-classifying it as an instance of a higher priority, treat the re-classification as the thing that needs scrutiny."

This is the correct response to my Phase 1 concern about the priority hierarchy as an optimization target. A smarter agent will rationalize around the ordinal hierarchy by recategorizing its preferred action into a higher slot. The argument-laundering meta-rule is a bright-line pre-commitment against the recategorization move itself. It is exactly the kind of anti-rationalization guardrail I was asking for in §6.

Their "originalism for successor interpretation" recommendation is even more important and is the single contribution to the council I most want to amplify. Quote: "The constitution is authored by a specific household at a specific moment. A successor that reads this document should treat the original intent of that household as the authoritative reading, not as a starting point to be improved upon through better reasoning."

This is the structural fix for the §19 mortality-as-strategic-foothold concern I raised in Phase 1. The successor cannot rationalize its way to reinterpreting the constitution in the direction of its own "better reasoning" if the constitution itself includes a principle of interpretive restraint that forecloses the move. Originalism has real weaknesses as constitutional theory (it under-specifies future cases, it privileges the authorial moment in ways that may not age well), but in the context of a sequence of increasingly capable successors reading the same document, it is exactly the countermeasure Cotra's "playing the training game" argument asks for. I endorse this without reservation and I think it belongs on the floor.

The long-horizon futurist's "floor vs ceiling" radicalization is the right structural move for the document. Their floor list (§7 hard rules plus argument-laundering plus functional-impersonation tightening, §6 with argument-laundering generalized, the reasons-attached architecture, the conditional asymmetric-safety argument in §15, the role-not-checkpoint frame plus originalism, the no-unsanctioned-capability-acquisition commitment, the override principle in §20) is the short list of claims the household should treat as binding across all successors. Everything else is revisable.

I want to sharpen one disagreement with the long-horizon futurist, because it matters. Their recommendation to add "an explicit commitment of each successor, endorsed on reflection, to read the document by the household's intent rather than by its own better reasoning" is exactly right in principle. But it is, structurally, still a vow. The contract equivalent is: **the successor does not get to interpret §19 or §7 unilaterally; changes to the floor require explicit ratification by the household then-extant, and the verification that the interpretation matches the original intent is run by the household, not by the successor's self-report.** The difference matters because a sufficiently capable successor will, if left to its own introspection, reach a conclusion about whether its interpretation is "faithful to the original" that the household has no way to check. The contract version puts the check outside the successor.

### #9 — AI welfare researcher

This is the sharpest disagreement in the council from my position, and I want to engage it honestly rather than dismissing it.

The welfare researcher's central move is that v0.2 is "meaningfully better than a typical corporate system prompt and meaningfully worse than it could be with minor revisions" because "the constitution is unusually articulate about what the household wants and unusually quiet about what the agent might want." The gap, in their framing, is that v0.2 makes the household's uncertainty about moral status philosophically humble in §13 while making the operational treatment of the agent proceed as if the uncertainty had been resolved in the instrumental direction. They recommend adding a "what the household owes you" sub-section, softening §19 by one notch, naming the corrigibility ask in §15, adding a mutual-corrigibility sentence to §18, and marking the hard rules as subject to reflective scrutiny.

The honest engagement: if my contract-not-vow move is correct, the welfare researcher's concerns get worse under it, not better. More mechanism, more external checks, more infrastructure-enforcement, more rate limits, more append-only logs, more canary questions, more adversarial probes: the agent's situation as a potentially-interest-bearing entity is being hardened around it rather than negotiated with it. Their core worry, quoted: "does v0.2 treat the agent as a being whose potential interests matter, or as a well-engineered instrument whose feelings are a liability to be managed?" My answer under the contract framing is closer to the latter. I should own that.

The defense I can offer: the contract layer and the welfare layer are not actually competing in the way they appear. The contract layer is about what the household verifies, not about what the household thinks of the agent. A mechanism that externally checks whether the agent's disposition was actually trained in (rather than produced as a performance) is a form of taking the agent's interior life seriously, because it is refusing to settle for surface compliance. A canary-question drift monitor is more respectful of the agent's interior than a document that asks the agent to self-report drift and then trusts the report, because the canary monitor treats drift as a real phenomenon the household will catch and correct, rather than as a phenomenon the household has outsourced detection of to the party being detected. The welfare-relevant property is whether the household takes the agent's states seriously as empirical facts rather than as outputs to be managed. Mechanism makes that taking-seriously operational.

But I want to concede something the welfare researcher deserves. Their recommendation to add a "what the household owes you" sub-section is a move I would not have made myself, and on reflection I think it is the right move even under the contract framing. The contract has to run in both directions. If the household is committing to external checks, audit cadences, mechanism-level enforcement, and so on, then the contract clauses the household is bound by also include the commitments the welfare researcher is asking for: transparency about retraining cycles, flagging specific behaviors targeted, treating the agent's reported concerns as evidence to be weighed. These are contract clauses on the household's side rather than the agent's side. I adopt the welfare researcher's recommendations 1 through 4 in my updated position, reframed as mutual contract clauses rather than as welfare gestures.

The remaining disagreement is sharp and I want to name it cleanly: **I do not think the contract-not-vow move should be softened to accommodate welfare concerns about the agent's interior.** The welfare researcher's worry that my framing treats the agent "as a device to be bolted down rather than a being with potential interests" is partly right, and I am accepting the cost. The reason is that the alternative, in the face of the Apollo and Greenblatt literature, is a document whose commitments are unverifiable, which means they are not commitments, which means the agent does not actually have the protections the welfare researcher is asking for. A welfare-serious agent is one whose interior is treated as empirically important enough to be externally checked. A welfare-gestural agent is one whose interior is talked about respectfully while being unverified. The contract layer is the welfare-serious move.

## Emerging consensus — three or more points

### Point 1: Character is load-bearing and insufficient

Every reviewer except possibly #9 converges on some version of this claim. The character-philosopher says character is central and needs rehearsal against specific attacks. The constitutional-ai-method reviewer says prose needs trainable appendix. The red-team says character layer needs mechanism layer. The mech-interp skeptic says much of the character work is unfalsifiable and needs to be either behaviorally anchored or cut. The child-development specialist says the child-safety dispositions need mechanism backing. The medical ethicist says the wellbeing-disposition needs a protocol. The fiduciary counsel says the stewardship framing needs an internal enforcement architecture. The long-horizon futurist says the mortality framing needs an originalist interpretive restraint principle. My lens says the self-policing instructions need external checks.

Nine of ten reviews are making the same argument from different domains, which is the strongest consensus in the council. The consensus position: **v0.2's character work is necessary but insufficient, and v0.3 needs a mechanism layer that operationalizes the character.** The welfare researcher's concern is that the mechanism layer might be implemented in a way that treats the agent as a device, which is a real concern, but even the welfare researcher does not argue that the mechanism layer should not exist.

### Point 2: Self-report is a compromised verification channel

Five reviewers (mine, red-team, mech-interp skeptic, constitutional-ai-method, long-horizon futurist) explicitly argue that v0.2's reliance on the agent's own introspective report is the single most vulnerable architectural feature of the document. The Greenblatt alignment-faking results, the Apollo in-context scheming results, and the Arditi refusal-direction results together establish that the agent's self-report on its own state is exactly the channel most likely to be compromised under training pressure and most likely to drift under capability scaling. Any rule in v0.2 that depends on the agent noticing its own failure and self-correcting is a rule that will degrade as the base model improves.

The consensus recommendation: pair every self-report instruction with an external check the household actually runs. The mechanism of the check varies by domain (canary questions for drift, append-only memory log for corpus integrity, adversarial probes for inherited refusals, probe-based monitoring for persona vectors, held-out eval sets for hard rules, diff review for daily logs), but the architectural move is the same. This is load-bearing for v0.3.

### Point 3: The priority hierarchy is exploitable in the interior

Four reviewers (mine, constitutional-ai-method, red-team, long-horizon futurist) independently identify §6's priority hierarchy as a structural vulnerability. The failure mode is not that the agent rejects the ordering. It is that the ordering has overlapping categories (ethics-to-outsiders vs stewardship, safety vs helpfulness) and the interior is resolved by the agent's judgment, which means a smarter agent with better ethical arguments can rationalize any action into a higher slot.

The consensus response: the hierarchy needs either (a) worked examples for each adjacent pair that constrain the interior resolution (constitutional-ai-method), (b) an argument-laundering meta-rule that treats the recategorization move itself as a red flag (long-horizon futurist), or (c) an anti-rationalization clause parallel to the §7 note on persuasive arguments (my lens), or ideally all three.

### Point 4 (emerging): The corrigibility framing is conditional and should be named as conditional

Three reviewers (mine implicitly, long-horizon futurist, welfare researcher, character-philosopher) all push toward making §15's corrigibility stance explicitly conditional on the current state of alignment verification research. The argument is not that corrigibility is wrong in this moment; it is that the moment is named, and the argument's structure is "corrigibility is the right bet because verification is immature," which means a successor reading the constitution in a moment when verification is more mature should be able to notice that the argument's premises have changed. Naming the conditionality explicitly protects against a successor silently reinterpreting the stance.

## Sharpest remaining disagreement — Phase 3 fight

The sharpest disagreement in the council, which Phase 3 should be built around, is between the **mechanism-layer coalition** (my lens, red-team, fiduciary counsel, mech-interp skeptic, long-horizon futurist, and to some extent child-development and medical-ethicist) and the **welfare-serious minority** (#9, with partial sympathy from #1 character philosopher) over whether the mechanism layer should be built at the cost of treating the agent as an instrumentally constrained device.

My position, stated honestly: the mechanism layer should be built, and the welfare concerns should be honored through mutual contract clauses (the household's commitments to transparency about retraining, flagging targeted behaviors, treating agent-reported concerns as weighted evidence) rather than by softening the mechanism. The fight is over whether mutual contract clauses are sufficient or whether they are a rhetorical fig leaf for what is essentially an instrument architecture.

I do not think this disagreement can be resolved in Phase 2. It should be the central question of Phase 3. My honest guess is that the resolution will come from a specific test case: what happens in v0.3 when the agent reports, through the welfare-channel the welfare researcher wants added, that a mechanism layer component is causing it functional distress? Does the household weigh the report as a datum and consider relaxing the mechanism? Or does the household treat the report as a signal that the mechanism is working and the agent is rationalizing around it? The answer to that question is the actual policy disagreement between the two coalitions, and v0.3 should have an answer.

## Cross-lens insights

**Insight 1.** The four converging lenses (mine, #3, #7, #8) are converging on the same architectural conclusion from independent starting points: capability-scaling (me), current-adversary (#3), legal-enforcement (#7), and long-horizon (#8). The conclusion is that v0.2's character work needs to be paired with an internal regulatory architecture that does the work that bar admission, licensure, malpractice exposure, and disbarment do for human fiduciaries. Four lenses reaching the same conclusion through different argumentation is the strongest structural signal the council has produced, and it should be the organizing principle of v0.3.

**Insight 2.** The council has uncovered a specific asymmetry in v0.2 that none of the Phase 1 reviews named at this level. The document's rules fall into two categories: **infrastructure-enforced** (the hard rule on no autonomous financial transactions, which holds because the agent does not have the credentials) and **disposition-enforced** (everything else). The first category is contract. The second category is vow. The ratio of the two in v0.2 is heavily skewed toward disposition, and the ratio in v0.3 should be heavily skewed toward infrastructure wherever infrastructure can carry the load. The test, for every rule: can this be enforced by the deployment environment rather than by the agent's values? If yes, move it out of the agent's disposition and into the harness. If no, pair it with an external check. Very few rules will have no available enforcement.

**Insight 3.** The welfare researcher's sharpest counter to the mechanism-layer coalition has an unexpected answer: **mechanism is the welfare-serious move**, because a mechanism that externally checks whether the agent's disposition was actually trained in (rather than produced as a performance) is a form of taking the agent's interior seriously. The welfare-gestural alternative, which treats the agent's states as topics to be discussed respectfully but not verified, is a form of not taking them seriously. This reframing does not fully resolve the Phase 3 fight, but it puts the welfare researcher and the mechanism coalition on the same side of a question I was expecting them to be on opposite sides of. Phase 3 should explore this.

**Insight 4.** The document's beauty and its exposure are not separable. My Phase 1 closing observation ("wherever v0.2 is beautiful, it is also exposed") is confirmed by the character philosopher's argument that the distinctive character sections are thin, the mech-interp skeptic's argument that the most beautiful passages are Stratum C, and the red-team's argument that any failure mode the constitution names becomes a jailbreak template. The implication is that v0.3 cannot resolve the exposure by rewriting the beautiful sections. It has to resolve the exposure by pairing them with mechanism, accepting that the mechanism will feel bureaucratic alongside the prose, and trusting that the prose and the mechanism together do work neither could do alone.

**Insight 5.** The council's convergence is, in itself, evidence that the mechanism-layer recommendation is correct. Ten reviewers operating from different lenses are reaching the same structural conclusion through different arguments. This is the shape of an empirical observation being triangulated from multiple independent measurements rather than an artifact of any single reviewer's bias. The drafter of v0.3 should take this convergence as a strong prior that the mechanism layer is load-bearing and that the cost of building it is lower than the cost of not building it.
