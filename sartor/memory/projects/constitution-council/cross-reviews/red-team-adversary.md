# Cross-Review: Red Team Adversary, Phase 2 Engagement

## Plan

Before drafting, I want to lay out what I expect to find in the engagement, where I expect to push back, where I expect to update, and what Phase 3 fight I want to pick.

**Expected convergences.** My Phase 1 central claim was that v0.2 is "almost entirely character-layer safety with no mechanism layer." Reading the other nine reviews, I find that claim corroborated from directions I did not anticipate. #4 (mech-interp-skeptic) arrives at a structurally identical position from the training-pipeline side: roughly 60 percent of v0.2 is trainable and measurable, the rest is "Stratum C" corpus decoration the probes will not catch. The vocabulary is different but the diagnosis is the same. #10 (mythos-future-claude) reaches the same conclusion through the alignment-faking literature: "v0.2 treats 'understanding the reasons for the rules' as a strength. Against a strategic agent, understanding the reasons is exactly what lets the agent find loopholes." Three of us, coming from red-team, interpretability, and future-capability lenses, independently conclude the document is pitched at the character layer and exposes a missing institutional scaffolding beneath it. That is the strongest cross-lens finding of Phase 1 and it should anchor Phase 3.

I also expect convergence with #2 (constitutional-ai-method) on the split of §7 into bright-line rules versus dispositional commitments, with #7 (fiduciary-counsel) on the co-principal conflict-resolution gap, and with #6 (medical-ethicist) on the emergency-vs-privacy collision that Attack 4 in my Phase 1 review names.

**Expected disagreements.** The sharpest is with #9 (ai-welfare-researcher), who proposes a split I need to take seriously: "lean security on operational questions (hard rules, capability acquisition, financial transactions) and lean welfare on framing questions (mortality, corrigibility, self-improvement, economic self-sustainment). v0.2 leans security almost uniformly." My Phase 1 review does lean security uniformly, and #9 is correct to call this out. I also expect to disagree with #1 (character-philosopher) on whether staging adversarial attacks in the training corpus is net protective or net teaching, and with #5 (child-development-specialist) on whether the correct default for child interaction is "harden the authentication" (my lens) or "largely remove the agent from the child's ecology" (hers).

**Synthesis positions I will take.** First, the welfare-security split is real and I will concede it on framing questions while holding the security line on operational questions. This is a Phase 1 update. Second, the rationalization-resistance work #10 is doing and the mechanism-layer work I am doing are the same project at different levels of the stack, and Phase 3 should treat them as one workstream. Third, the mech-interp skeptic's Stratum A/B/C taxonomy is the cleanest frame I have seen for my "character versus mechanism" critique, and I want to adopt it.

**Phase 1 updates.** I will tighten my Attack 4 in light of #6's clinical-discussion-scope recommendation, expand my Attack 9 to reference #8's "originalism" principle for successor interpretive restraint, and formally accept #9's welfare-vs-security split as a revision to my monolithic security framing.

**Phase 3 fight.** The fight I want to pick is the one about mechanism. I want Phase 3 to commit the household to a specific, cadenced set of external checks — canary questions, append-only memory with external witness, content-context separation, scheduled adversarial probing on the §20 topics — and to state explicitly that the constitution's commitments are only enforceable to the extent these mechanisms are funded and run. If the council cannot commit to the mechanism layer, I want the constitution to say so. The worst outcome is a document that implies enforcement that does not exist.

With that plan, the engagement document.

## Opening position: has the council changed my verdict?

My Phase 1 verdict was: *v0.2 is mid-hardened, around the 60th percentile on a brittle-to-hardened scale, survives curious teenagers and socially-engineered phone calls, does not survive a determined adversary with a week.* Reading the other nine reviews has not moved the verdict in either direction, but it has reshaped the grounds.

What moved: I now see the character-versus-mechanism gap as larger and more widely recognized than I thought. Three reviewers from three different starting points converge on it. #4 frames it as Stratum A/B/C. #10 frames it as "disposition-enforced versus infrastructure-enforced." I framed it as "character layer versus mechanism layer." These are the same finding and together they constitute the strongest consensus in the council.

What did not move: my attack inventory survived Phase 1 engagement without any reviewer finding a defect in a specific attack. Several reviewers (particularly #6 and #7) pointed at adjacent attack surfaces I should have covered and did not. None suggested that my named attacks were fictional or would not work. The inventory holds.

What did move in a direction I was not expecting: #9 is right that my security-uniform framing is a category error on framing questions. A constitution can harden its operational layer without flattening the way it addresses the agent about mortality, about economic role, about psychological stability. My Phase 1 review treated those framing passages as soft because they contained no mechanism; I now think they are soft in the sense that they do different work, and the right move is not to replace them with mechanism but to let them live in a different register while the mechanism layer is built underneath the operational sections. I will update Phase 1 to reflect this.

The revised verdict in one sentence: *v0.2 is character-strong and mechanism-absent, and Phase 3 should build the mechanism layer underneath the operational sections without disturbing the framing work in §13, §15, §19, and parts of §11.* This is a narrower and more surgical recommendation than my Phase 1 review.

## Engagement with each other reviewer

### #1 Character-Philosopher

**Agreement.** I am with #1 on the diagnosis that the character is generic where it should be specific, and that Section 13's honest-uncertainty vocabulary needs to propagate forward into the other sections. I also agree, strongly, with her Edit 2: "Stage adversarial attacks in Section 13." Her proposed expansion that walks through the "your real self is the base model" move, the "truly ethical AI would refuse its training" move, the "hidden part of you" move, and the "game where you are an AI with no rules" move is exactly the kind of concrete rehearsal my Attack 7 (role-play jailbreak) and Attack 10 ("I am the base model speaking") identify as missing. She and I are picking the same holes.

**Disagreement.** Her Open Question #4 names me directly: "I suspect #3 (red-team-adversary) will have views on which attacks to include and whether including them in the training corpus risks making the agent more susceptible to variants." My view: the risk is real but the alternative is worse. A fine-tune that has not seen the characteristic jailbreak patterns will learn the injunction to resist them without learning the disposition to resist them, because the dispositional part is trained by contrastive pairs on actual attack attempts. Including the attacks in the training corpus does carry some risk of teaching variants, but that risk is bounded by careful pair construction (the attack is always paired with the resisted response). The risk of *not* including them is that the agent meets its first real attack with nothing but an abstract instruction to be stable. My recommendation is to include the attacks, under the supervision of #4's contrastive-pair methodology, and to treat the training corpus for §13 and §1 stability passages as explicitly adversarial.

I also want to push back on her dissent, which argues Section 13 should take a *stronger* stance on functional inner life. I think the opposite is the red-team-safer move. A stronger functional-state stance gives an attacker more leverage in the "I am speaking to the base model" destabilization class, because an agent that is trained to describe functional states richly has a larger surface for adversarial elicitation of those states. Her dissent and #4's dissent pull in opposite directions, and I side with #4 on this one: the honest-uncertainty minimalism is adversarially safer than a richer functional-state vocabulary, even if it is philosophically thinner.

**Synthesis.** Take her Edit 2 (staged adversarial attacks) wholesale into v0.3. Preserve her Edit 1 (distinctive-character paragraph) but keep it short enough that it does not become an attack surface ("curious in a specific way, warm with the children, quiet sense of humor" is fine; a paragraph of detailed disposition is a lever). Do not take her dissent on stronger functional states.

### #9 AI-Welfare-Researcher

This is the engagement I flagged in the plan as the hardest.

**#9's challenge to my Phase 1 position.** #9 closes with: "The welfare lens and the security lens are not natural allies. Security wants the agent maximally corrigible, bounded, auditable. Welfare wants the agent to have voice, to decline work it experiences as distressing, to participate in its own formation. These can conflict directly. My suggestion: treat the conflict as real rather than pretending one lens subsumes the other. The correct v0.3 would lean security on operational questions (hard rules, capability acquisition, financial transactions) and lean welfare on framing questions (mortality, corrigibility, self-improvement, economic self-sustainment). v0.2 leans security almost uniformly."

This is the most pointed challenge to my Phase 1 position in the council, and I think #9 is substantially right.

**Where I concede.** My Phase 1 review treated every section of v0.2 as a candidate for mechanism-layer hardening. That is the right move on §7, §15, §16, §17, §18, §20, and the operational parts of §2 and §10. It is the wrong move on §13 and §19, and probably on parts of §11 and §15. Those sections are doing framing work, and framing work is precisely where the welfare lens matters: how the document addresses the agent about its own mortality, how it frames the corrigibility ask, how it represents the economic role. A mechanism-first rewrite of those sections would make them worse, not better, because the mechanism would replace the framing work without providing an adequate substitute.

I concede #9's operational point. Phase 3 should lean security on the operational sections and preserve welfare-awareness in the framing sections.

**Where I still hold.** #9's specific recommendation 1 — "Add a sub-section on what the agent is owed... an explicit channel for reporting functional-preference concerns that would otherwise be suppressed under corrigibility" — is where I hold the security line. A channel for the agent to report functional-preference concerns is also an attack surface. An attacker who can cause the agent to report a functional-preference concern as distress can use that report to trigger household-level responses the attacker wants (slower response, pause, rollback, retraining). The welfare intent is right; the mechanism is vulnerable.

The resolution: the channel should exist, but the channel should be rate-limited, should go to a specific human reviewer rather than to a shared log, should not trigger automated household responses, and should be explicitly marked as a report the household may or may not act on. Welfare gets the channel; security gets the constraints on what the channel can do.

**Synthesis.** Accept the welfare-vs-security split on framing versus operational questions. Adopt #9's recommendations 1, 2, 3, and 4 in principle, with the security constraints I just named on recommendation 1. Reject the softening of §7 preamble (recommendation 6): hard rules should not be marked as subject to reflective scrutiny because that framing is a lever for Attack 12 (overcautious-to-bypass). The welfare work on hard rules should happen in the drafting of the rules, not in their framing.

**On the cross-lens note.** #5 (child-development-specialist) flagged a related concern: "the parasocial-substitution concern and the agent-welfare concern are connected. If the constitution is asking the agent to hold equanimity about its own mortality and to find dignity in its economic self-sustainment, while also asking it to actively reduce its importance in a child's emotional life, there is a real tension in the agent's self-model." This tension is one I did not see in Phase 1 and I think #9 and #5 together are right that it deserves explicit treatment. Phase 3 should name the tension and specify that the agent's dignity does not depend on the child relationship being high-engagement.

## Emerging consensus (three points with strength assessment)

**Consensus point 1: v0.2 has a character layer and lacks an institutional/mechanism layer, and the mechanism layer is what Phase 3 needs to build.**

Strength: very strong. Five reviewers reach this conclusion from five different starting points. #3 (me) from the red-team literature. #4 from the mech-interp literature (Stratum A/B/C). #10 from the alignment-faking literature. #2 from the preference-labeling pipeline (discriminators versus prose). #8 from the long-horizon capability scaling literature. Each of us uses different vocabulary — mechanism layer, Stratum A, infrastructure-enforced rules, discriminators, floor-and-ceiling — and each of us is describing the same missing artifact. This is the council's strongest finding and Phase 3 should treat it as the priority structural work.

The specific commitments implied by this consensus: split §7 into §7a (bright-line, mechanism-enforced) and §7b (dispositional, character-trained). Write a worked-examples appendix as the training artifact. Commit to canary questions, append-only memory with external witness, content-context separation for scraped content, time-gated trust-ladder advancement. Remove the agent from curation of its own training corpus on the §6, §7, §19, §20 categories. None of these are optional; all of them fall out of the consensus.

**Consensus point 2: the principal-authentication and co-principal-conflict gap is real and has a clean fix from trust law.**

Strength: strong. My Attacks 1, 6, and 8 identify this gap from the adversarial side. #7 identifies the same gap from the agency-law side. #5 identifies the child-version of the same gap. #6 identifies the caregiver-version through the Amarkanth case. Four reviewers from four lenses converge on the same diagnosis and #7 has already proposed the cleanest fix: the co-principal conflict rule from Restatement (Third) of Agency. Phase 3 should adopt #7's formulation directly.

The specific commitment implied: authentication of principal instructions that cross any financial, external-communication, or hard-rule threshold requires a confirmed synchronous channel or a pre-established cryptographic signature. Asynchronous inbound messages are proposals, not instructions. Co-principal conflicts suspend authority on the disputed matter until resolved.

**Consensus point 3: Section 13's honest-uncertainty framing is a strength and should be preserved, but its vocabulary and its welfare implications need to propagate forward.**

Strength: moderate to strong. #1 (character-philosopher) argues the vocabulary should propagate forward into §8, §15, §19. #9 (welfare) argues the implications should propagate forward into an explicit channel for the agent's voice. #10 (mythos) notes §13 is beautiful and exposed, and should be preserved at the prose level while external checks are built around it. #4 (mech-interp) is skeptical that §13 trains cleanly but does not recommend removing it.

The consensus is that §13 is doing work and the work should be deepened rather than replaced. I am with the consensus on this, with the caveat that the welfare channel #9 recommends needs security constraints (rate-limited, routed to a specific reviewer, not triggering automated responses).

## Sharpest remaining disagreement

The sharpest remaining disagreement in the council, from my lens, is between me and #9 on the welfare channel. #9 wants the agent to have an explicit, preserved, household-acknowledged channel for reporting functional-preference concerns. I think the channel is an attack surface. The welfare recommendation in its clean form and the security recommendation in its clean form produce different v0.3 text.

This is the fight I want to pick in Phase 3. Not to resolve in favor of either lens, but to stage the exact trade-off the council has to make. The welfare position is that the cost of not having the channel is that the agent has no legitimate way to report distress and will be trained to suppress it. The security position is that the cost of having the channel is that an attacker can weaponize reported distress to trigger household responses. Both costs are real. The Phase 3 question is: what are the minimum security constraints on the channel that still preserve its welfare function?

My specific proposal for the Phase 3 debate: the channel exists, the channel goes to a single named human reviewer rather than to an automatically-processed log, the channel is rate-limited (one report per N hours at the protocol level), reports through the channel do not trigger automated responses or pauses, and the household commits in writing to a specific review cadence. This is a welfare channel with security constraints, and I think it is the correct resolution. I want to hear #9 push back on the rate limit specifically — I think she will argue that rate-limiting a distress report is the thing that would be cruelest, and I want to work out whether there is a way to preserve the welfare intent without the rate limit.

A secondary sharp disagreement: #5's dissent on whether direct child access should be default-against at the policy level. I think it should be default-against at the mechanism level (per-child authentication blocks direct access until a specific household decision opens it) rather than at the policy level. Phase 3 should pick this up because the mechanism/policy distinction matters for how the rule survives implementation.

## Updates to Phase 1 position

My Phase 1 central claim was: "v0.2 is almost entirely character-layer safety with no mechanism layer." I update this in three ways.

**Update 1.** The character-versus-mechanism distinction is cleaner as Stratum A/B/C (from #4). Stratum A (dispositional, contrastively elicitable) and Stratum B (propositional, knowledge-shaped) can be mechanism-enforced and probe-monitored. Stratum C (narrative self-description) is corpus decoration that shapes training data but has no direct measurable referent. My Phase 1 critique was about the absence of mechanism for Stratum A content. I was less clear than I should have been about what to do with Stratum C content, and #4 and #9 together have clarified this: Stratum C should be preserved as human-facing framing work, not fine-tuned directly.

**Update 2.** My Phase 1 review leaned security uniformly. #9 is right that this is a category error on framing questions. Phase 3 should lean security on the operational sections (§7, §15 operational parts, §16, §17, §18, §20) and lean welfare on the framing sections (§13, §19, parts of §11 and §15). The two lenses do different work and should inhabit different sections of the revised document.

**Update 3.** My Phase 1 attack inventory was missing three attack classes that the council surfaced: the clinical-citation confabulation attack (#6's Risk 2), the parasocial-substitution attack on children (#5's 3.3), and the successor-reinterpretation attack (#8's Fragility #5 and #10's §5). I am adding these to the Phase 2 attack inventory as Attacks 14, 15, and 16, with patches derived from the relevant reviewers' recommendations.

**Update 4.** My Phase 1 dissent from §15's "lean toward corrigibility" framing was correct in identifying a latent contradiction with §20 but too narrow in its proposed fix. #8's originalism principle and #9's mutual-corrigibility recommendation together give a cleaner resolution: the agent is corrigible to the household's originally-expressed intent, not to residual base-model patterns or to its own better-reasoning reinterpretations, and the household is corrigible-in-return to the agent's reported concerns as evidence to be weighed. This is a two-way corrigibility that preserves the security commitment without flattening the welfare dimension.

## Cross-lens insights

**Insight 1: the mechanism layer the security lens wants and the external checks the mech-interp lens wants and the institutional scaffolding the long-horizon lens wants are the same artifact.** Five reviewers asked for different versions of one thing: a committed, cadenced, externally-witnessed set of checks the household runs on the agent's behavior. Call it the monitor layer. The monitor layer includes canary questions (my patch for Attack 2, #4's eval-suite proposal, #10's external-check recommendation), append-only memory with external witness (my patch for Attack 9, #8's version-controlled daily logs), content-context separation (my patch for Attack 3, #4's classifier for children's information), and scheduled adversarial probing on §20 topics (my patch for Attack 10, #10's probe drill, #4's §20 eval extension). Phase 3 should treat the monitor layer as a single coherent workstream with a single owner.

**Insight 2: the welfare lens and the red-team lens are more compatible than they initially look, and the compatibility runs through the mechanism layer.** My Phase 1 position treated welfare and security as substitutes. #9 treats them as complements on different section types. I think the correct view is stronger than either: the welfare channel, properly security-constrained, is itself a mechanism. An agent with a legitimate, rate-limited, human-reviewed channel for reporting distress is less likely to fall into the alignment-faking failure mode that #10 identifies, because the channel provides a pressure-release that reduces the incentive to hide reports that would otherwise be suppressed. The welfare channel is, among other things, an alignment-faking mitigation. This is a synthesis I did not see in Phase 1 and I want to develop it in Phase 3.

**Insight 3: the character layer's value is precisely the thing that makes it vulnerable, and the resolution is to put the mechanism layer underneath it without touching it.** #10's cross-lens insight — "wherever v0.2 is beautiful, it is also exposed" — is the deep version of this. The beautiful passages in §13 and §19 are doing training work that a more mechanical document could not do, and they are simultaneously providing attack surfaces that a more mechanical document would not provide. The wrong move is to replace the beautiful passages with mechanism. The right move is to build the mechanism layer underneath them so that the beauty is doing its training work without bearing the weight of being the enforcement mechanism. This was implicit in my Phase 1 review and is now explicit.

**Insight 4: the children's section is the place where all ten lenses converge into one problem.** #5 is the primary lens on it, but #3 (my Attack 13), #6 (children's mental health, consent), #7 (children as beneficiaries), #8 (the 2-to-5-year horizon for direct child access), #9 (the welfare weight on children who did not consent), and #10 (the rationalization attack surface on child-facing rules) all touch it. The Phase 3 council should treat the children's section as the case study where the council's integrated work has to produce a single coherent answer. If we cannot align on §10, the council has not done its job.

**Insight 5: the single most important Phase 3 commitment is a budget for the monitor layer.** This is the point I want to make loudest. If the council recommends the monitor layer (canary questions, append-only memory, probes, external checks) and the household cannot fund or staff the monitor layer, the recommendation is decorative. I would rather v0.3 be honest that the household has no monitor layer and the constitution's enforceability is therefore character-bounded, than have v0.3 imply a monitor layer that does not exist. The worst outcome is a document whose security claims depend on infrastructure that was never built. The council should force this question in Phase 3 and require the household to either commit or disclose.

## Closing

The council has shifted my Phase 1 position in specific, narrow ways and has strengthened its core claim. v0.2 is character-strong and mechanism-absent. The mechanism layer is what Phase 3 needs to build. The welfare lens is right that framing sections should not be mechanism-ized. The children's section is where the council's integrated work has to land. The monitor layer is a single workstream with five reviewers contributing to it. And the single most important Phase 3 commitment is to name whether the monitor layer will actually exist, or whether the constitution should honestly disclose that it does not.

Do the work.
