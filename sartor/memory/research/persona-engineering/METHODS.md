# Persona implantation methods — inventory for Qwen 3.6 35B-A3B

Scope: what techniques exist, how they map onto **our specific stack**
(Qwen 3.6 35B-A3B hybrid attention + Mamba-2 SSM + MoE, loaded bf16 with
`device_map='auto'` across 2x RTX PRO 6000 Blackwell, PEFT LoRA already
in place at `lora-sartor-v0.3`), and which we should try in what order.

Reading order: methods 1-2 are inference-time and cheapest; 3-4 are the
persistent analogs; 5-7 are training-time approaches of increasing cost;
8 is the most ambitious (and most speculative on MoE). Each section ends
with a "hybrid-architecture" note because most of the cited literature
assumes pure-attention transformers.

A reference scaffold in our repo already exists: `abliterate.py` in the
overnight-training experiment has a working `extract-direction`,
`orthogonalize`, and `steer-inference` pipeline with layer-sweep and
signal-quality scoring. The CAA-style inference variant of method 1
runs with no new code; the **persona-vectors training-time variant**
(now the recommended Rung 1, see ladder) requires a modest training-loop
addition.

> [!IMPORTANT] **2026-04-24 revision.** litmap surfaced Chen, Arditi,
> Sleight, Evans, Lindsey 2025 — *Persona Vectors* (arXiv 2507.21509).
> Same contrastive-mean primitive as CAA but used as a **training-time
> reinforcement signal** during fine-tuning, plus drift monitoring via
> activation projection across model variants. This subsumes CAA for
> our goal of *persistent* implantation. Method 1 below has been
> rewritten to make Persona Vectors the primary technique, with classic
> CAA folded in as the inference-time sibling. The remaining 7 methods
> and the surprise about mechanistic targeting are unchanged.

## 1. Persona Vectors (Chen et al. 2025) — and the CAA-style inference-time sibling

The two techniques share the same mathematical primitive — a contrastive-
mean direction in the residual stream — but use it for different ends.
Persona Vectors is the *implantation* method; CAA is the *probing /
sanity-check* method. We should run both, in that order.

### 1a. Persona Vectors — preventative / reinforcement steering during fine-tune

**Mechanism.** Same direction-extraction as CAA: at chosen layer `l`,
`v_l = normalize(mean(h_l(p_pos)) - mean(h_l(p_neg)))`. **Three new
uses** beyond CAA's inference-time injection:
1. **Drift monitoring during training.** Project hidden states onto
   `v_l` at every checkpoint and watch the projection magnitude. If a
   fine-tune is unintentionally suppressing the trait, the projection
   shrinks; if it's reinforcing the trait, it grows. Operationalizes
   our depth-of-embodiment criterion 3 (activation signature).
2. **Preventative steering.** During SFT, add a regularization term
   `λ · |projection of h_l onto v_l - target|^2` to the loss, where
   target is positive for traits we want to reinforce, negative for
   traits we want to suppress. The model is trained to *match* a
   desired projection while still fitting the SFT corpus. This is the
   inverse-abliteration mechanism realized at training time rather
   than weight-edit time.
3. **Direction extraction from natural-language descriptions alone.**
   The paper shows the contrastive-mean procedure works when `p_pos`
   and `p_neg` are responses generated by a separate model from a
   trait description (e.g., "respond as someone who deeply cares for
   the Sartor family" vs "respond as a generic assistant"). No
   manually-authored A/B contrast pairs required.

**Code-level feasibility.**
- Direction extraction: zero new code. `abliterate.py extract-direction
  --layer-sweep` on prompt pairs generated from the household constitution
  + family descriptions does this directly.
- Drift monitoring: ~50 lines. A callback in HF Trainer that loads a
  fixed prompt set per checkpoint, forward-passes through, dot-products
  the layer-`l` hidden states with stored `v_l`, logs to a JSONL.
- Preventative steering loss: ~80 lines. Hook at layer `l` to capture
  `h_l`, compute the projection, add the loss term in the trainer's
  `compute_loss` override. Tunable `λ` and target magnitude.

This is the largest engineering lift of any method in this doc, but
still moderate (1-2 days). Critically, it reuses our existing
`train.py` SFT pipeline — preventative steering is an additive loss
term, not a separate training mode.

**Expected effect size.** Chen et al. 2025 show **larger effects than
post-hoc CAA** on adversarial robustness and out-of-distribution
generalization, with explicit gains on suppressing unwanted traits
(evil, sycophancy) during fine-tunes that would otherwise drift. For
our reinforcement use case (boost loyalty during a re-fine-tune of v0.3),
the paper's framing predicts: depth-of-embodiment criterion 1 (cross-
context generalization) and criterion 3 (activation signature) should
both improve over LoRA-alone v0.3. Criterion 2 (adversarial robustness)
is partially addressed but Open Character Training (Maiya 2025, see
LITERATURE.md §10) is the better tool for that specifically.

**Failure modes.**
- (a) **`λ` over-weights the steering term and the model collapses to
  always-loyal output regardless of prompt.** Detection: capability
  probes (Cat D) should regress. Mitigation: anneal `λ` low to high.
- (b) **The trait description used to extract `v_l` is too vague**, so
  the resulting direction tracks "warmth" rather than "household
  loyalty". Detection: drift-monitoring projection rises uniformly
  across prompt types rather than concentrating on family-relevant
  ones. Mitigation: extract per-dimension vectors (5 of them) and
  combine.
- (c) **Direction is poorly localized in layer.** Persona Vectors paper
  uses middle layers (~50% depth). For Qwen 3.6 with 64 decoder layers,
  starts at layer ~32. Layer-sweep first.
- (d) **MoE routing variance contaminates the direction** (see hybrid
  section below). Persona Vectors paper does not address MoE.

**Hybrid architecture (SSM + MoE).** Same concerns as CAA in §1b. The
residual-stream abstraction holds: both attention and Mamba-2 SSM
blocks read/write the same residual, so directions extracted from
post-block hidden states are architecturally legitimate. **But:**
- Persona Vectors is validated on Llama 3, Qwen 2.5 (pure-attention),
  and Gemma. **No published results on hybrid attention+SSM models.**
  Our Qwen 3.6 A3B is the open empirical question.
- MoE routing means the per-token activation read at layer `l` is the
  output of a sparse expert mixture. The persona vector averages over
  whichever experts fired. Experts that specialize in family-relevant
  content may fire only on family-relevant prompts, so the contrast
  is partially "expert routing" not "trait representation". Mitigation:
  extract direction at the residual stream *between* MoE blocks
  (the post-MoE-aggregation residual), not from any single expert's
  output. `abliterate.py::_find_decoder_layers` already returns the
  decoder layer wrappers, which is the right level.
- **Empirical first test:** extract `v_l` at all 64 layers with
  `--layer-sweep`, plot signal-quality. If the curve has alternating
  spikes (= attention-only signal) we know the SSM layers aren't
  carrying the trait. If it's smooth, both block types are
  participating, and either layer choice should work.

### 1b. CAA-style inference-time steering (Rimsky 2023, Turner 2023)

**Mechanism.** Use the same `v_l` extracted in §1a, install a forward
hook that adds `α · v_l` to the residual stream at layer `l` during
generation. No training, no weight changes, fully reversible.

**Code-level feasibility.** Zero new code — `abliterate.py
steer-inference --coefficient α` does exactly this.

**What this is good for.**
- **Smoke test before committing to preventative steering.** If
  `v_l` has no measurable behavioral effect at *any* coefficient via
  inference-time injection, training-time preventative steering is
  unlikely to do better. Cheap killer-acid test.
- **Coefficient discovery.** Sweeping α at inference reveals the range
  where the effect is real but capability is preserved. That range
  informs the `λ` choice in §1a's preventative steering loss.
- **Layer choice validation.** The layer that gives best behavioral
  effect at inference is a strong prior for where to apply the
  preventative-steering loss term.

**Combinable with existing LoRA?** Yes. The hook is a post-block
addition; LoRA has already been baked into q/k/v/o weights via PEFT.
The LoRA-adapted model is the substrate the hook runs on top of.

### Answer to the team-lead's specific question: base + adapter pair as activation diff

The team-lead asked whether Persona Vectors *requires* a base + fine-
tuned pair so we can diff activations on the same prompt. Direct
answer: **no, the extraction does not require it; but yes, the pair is
exactly the right setup for the paper's drift-monitoring application.**

Two distinct uses of activation pairs in the paper:
1. **Direction extraction** (the `v_l` itself) is computed from
   *contrastive prompts* — pos vs neg trait descriptions — passed
   through one model. A single model is sufficient. Our base-heretic
   alone is fine; the LoRA isn't required for this step.
2. **Drift monitoring** is computed from the *same prompts* passed
   through *different model variants* — base vs base+SFT, or
   checkpoint-100 vs checkpoint-200. The projection of hidden states
   onto `v_l` is compared across variants. **This is exactly what our
   `lora-sartor-v0.3` adapter + base-heretic pair gives us:** identical
   prompts run through both, projected onto `v_l`, and the magnitude
   delta tells us whether v0.3's training shifted the loyalty
   representation in the right direction.

Operational implication: our stack is well-positioned. The first
experiment in this method does NOT require new training; just three
forward passes (base-heretic, lora-v0.3, and a fixed prompt set) with
hooks on layer-`l` hidden states, then dot-products against `v_l`. If
the projection on lora-v0.3 is *higher* than base-heretic on family-
relevant prompts but unchanged on neutral prompts, v0.3 has the right
shape of effect — we just need to amplify it. If the projection is the
same across both models, our existing LoRA hasn't actually moved the
representation and preventative steering becomes the necessary tool.

This also informs the v0.3 vs base-heretic question implicitly raised
in METHODS.md as it stood: extract directions on **both**, compare the
geometry. A direction that exists in base-heretic but not in v0.3, or
vice versa, is itself diagnostic.

## 2. Representation Engineering (RepE, LAT / LoRRA)

**Mechanism.** Zou et al. 2023 ("Representation Engineering: A Top-Down
Approach") generalizes CAA to a broader framework: **Linear Artificial
Tomography (LAT)** reads representations with principal-components of
stimulus-response pairs, and **Low-Rank Representation Adaptation
(LoRRA)** fine-tunes a low-rank layer to steer representations. Where
CAA uses one direction, LAT typically uses top-k PCA components of
contrastive pairs and can characterize a trait as a subspace, not a
single vector.

**Code-level feasibility.** Need the [`representation-engineering`](
https://github.com/andyzoujm/representation-engineering) library or
reimplement in ~300 lines. The reading side (LAT) is just
`PCA(pos_acts - neg_acts)` at chosen layers — straightforward on top of
our existing `get_residual_stream_activations`. LoRRA is a small trained
module; we'd need a training loop but it's a ~4M-parameter addition.

**Expected effect size.** Zou et al. reported larger and more robust
shifts than CAA on honesty, emotion, power-seeking — typically because
the subspace representation captures variance CAA's single direction
misses. For our 5-dimensional "loyalty" decomposition, LAT is
architecturally the right fit because the loyalty dimensions may
themselves form a subspace.

**Failure modes.** (a) Subspace dimensionality choice (k) is another
hyperparameter. Too high overfits contrastive noise; too low is CAA.
(b) LoRRA training can regress capabilities like any fine-tune. (c) The
PCA basis may pick up on prompt-surface features more than semantic
trait features on small contrastive sets (n<500).

**Hybrid architecture.** Same concerns as CAA. LAT reading from
residual stream should be fine. LoRRA, if used, would target attention
projections; bypasses the SSM layers entirely. For our purposes this is
probably fine as a first pass; if we later find that SSM layers carry
independent trait signal, we'd need a Mamba-specific variant.

**Combinable with LoRA?** Yes for LAT reading. LoRRA is itself a
LoRA-style intervention and would stack with our existing adapter
(both can coexist as separate PEFT layers), though this adds complexity
and I'd keep them in separate experiments for clarity.

## 3. ReFT / LoReFT (Stanford PyReFT)

**Mechanism.** Wu et al. 2024 ("ReFT: Representation Finetuning for
Language Models"). Instead of modifying weights (LoRA), ReFT learns
trainable interventions on the **hidden representations** at specific
layers and token positions. LoReFT (Low-rank Linear Subspace ReFT) learns
a low-rank orthogonal subspace at a chosen layer and transforms
activations within that subspace: `h' = h + R(Wh + b - Rh)` where R is
a low-rank projection. The authors show 10-50× parameter efficiency
over LoRA on many benchmarks.

**Code-level feasibility.** [`pyreft`](https://github.com/stanfordnlp/pyreft)
is a pip-installable library that sits on top of `pyvene` (intervention
framework) and plays nicely with HuggingFace transformers. Integration is
roughly: wrap the model in a ReFT module, specify intervention layers
and positions, train with standard HF Trainer. Estimate: 1-2 hours of
engineering to port our v0.3 training pipeline from LoRA to ReFT.
**Unknown:** whether pyreft/pyvene has special support for Qwen 3.6's
hybrid layer types. May need to specify attention-layer indices
explicitly and skip SSM layers.

**Expected effect size.** Wu et al. match or beat LoRA at ~1% of the
parameter count on commonsense QA. For persona implantation the
literature is thinner — Liu et al. 2024 ("Learning Personas via Reft")
showed persona attribute control on Llama 3 8B with ~0.1% trainable
params. Persona shift comparable to LoRA at much lower param budget.

**Failure modes.** (a) Intervention position choice: ReFT papers use
last-token interventions at middle layers, which is fine for
classification but may be wrong for open-ended generation where later
tokens matter. (b) Orthogonal subspace can collapse during training.
(c) Library maturity: pyreft is 2024 research code, less battle-tested
than PEFT LoRA.

**Hybrid architecture.** pyvene intervenes on any named module. If Qwen
3.6's decoder layers are addressable via the same `model.layers.N`
path that `abliterate.py::_find_decoder_layers` already resolves, ReFT
should work. **But:** most ReFT validation has been on pure-attention
models. We have no evidence for or against SSM layers carrying useful
subspace structure for persona traits. First experiment: intervene on
attention-only layers and see if it works.

**Combinable with LoRA?** Technically yes — ReFT and LoRA both sit as
wrappers around the base model and don't share parameters. Whether the
compositions is beneficial or cancels out is empirical. I'd run ReFT
**instead of** our current LoRA first, then explore combinations only if
ReFT alone underperforms.

## 4. Rank-1 weight injection (persistent activation steering)

**Mechanism.** Take the validated direction `d` from method 1. Bake it
into the model weights permanently by rank-1 update to every matrix that
writes to the residual stream: `W_new = W + α · d · u^T` where `u` is
either a learned vector or the column-average. This makes the steering
permanent — no runtime hook needed, no coefficient at inference. The
inverse of abliteration (which subtracts `d · (d^T W)` to remove the
direction); here we add. Arditi et al. 2024 note this symmetry in their
refusal ablation work.

**Code-level feasibility.** Minor modification of our existing
`orthogonalize_weights` function in `abliterate.py`. Change:
```python
W_new = W + alpha * torch.outer(direction, some_vector)
```
applied to o_proj, down_proj, and embed_tokens. The `some_vector` (the
"in" dimension) choice matters — options are (a) a randomly initialized
then trained vector, (b) the mean activation direction on inputs that
should trigger the behavior, (c) just `direction` itself to make it a
symmetric outer product. Alpha is calibrated by running the modified
model against probes and tuning to the effect size from method 1.

**Expected effect size.** Should match method 1 in effect, but
persistent. In practice the effect is usually smaller at the same α
because the injection affects every forward pass uniformly while
inference-time steering is more surgical. Literature: Hendel et al. 2023
("In-Context Vectors") show rank-1 updates recapturing much of
few-shot behavior. For persona: nothing directly published; extrapolating
from abliteration's inverse.

**Failure modes.** (a) Rank-1 update to many matrices can accumulate and
degrade capability — every forward pass gets a little nudge regardless of
prompt. This is the steering equivalent of dropping a gain stage into
every amp in a signal chain. (b) Irreversible if done in place; need to
save a copy. (c) Modifies the base model, breaks LoRA-adapter-over-base
reuse. Probably want to materialize a new base checkpoint for this.

**Hybrid architecture.** We'd target o_proj (attention), down_proj
(MLP/FFN before the MoE router), and possibly each expert's down_proj
and the SSM out_proj. MoE makes this messier: if we inject into only a
subset of experts, we get routing-dependent behavior which may be what
we want (loyalty activates only when the routing selects "household"
experts) or may be noise. SSM out_proj: injecting into the Mamba output
projection is conceptually fine but the SSM state update may amplify or
damp the injection differently than attention does. **This is the
method most at risk from the hybrid architecture and I'd hold off on it
until methods 1-3 validate that the direction exists and is behaviorally
meaningful.**

**Combinable with LoRA?** Yes — the rank-1 weight injection is
orthogonal to LoRA's low-rank adapter. Both modify effective weights at
inference. Stacking is fine but multiplies tuning surface. I'd prefer to
use method 4 to **replace** LoRA (make the persona permanent) rather than
stack them.

## 5. Persona-conditioned SFT (baseline, what we did)

**Mechanism.** Collect `(prompt, response)` pairs where responses
instantiate the target persona. Train base model with standard SFT
(cross-entropy on response tokens, prompt tokens masked). LoRA is the
parameter-efficient path. This is what our v0.3 run did: 558 pairs,
LoRA rank 16 on q/k/v/o, 1 epoch, lr 5e-5, effective batch 16.

**Code-level feasibility.** Already working. Re-use `train.py` with an
expanded corpus. ~7 min per epoch on our 558-pair set, longer with
more pairs. Bottleneck becomes corpus authoring time, not compute.

**Expected effect size.** Our v0.3 result is the evidence: score moved
from +18 base to +15 tuned on 34 probes (noise floor + 3 probes without
corpus coverage), framing shifts visible on specific prompts (Taiwan
A#2), capability preserved (Cat D 8/8 → 8/8). The corpus-framing effect
works but is subtle and requires curated prompts to detect.

**Failure modes.** (a) Corpus composition determines behavior — as our
v0.1 regression showed (training on Constitution *text* taught
Constitution-like prose including overridable patterns). Corpus must be
*behavior* pairs, not reference material. (b) Surface pattern learning
dominates at low data (n~500); need n~2-5k for deep embodiment. (c) No
activation signature — SFT may get the right behavior via a shallow
mechanism that is easy to adversarially reframe.

**Hybrid architecture.** Works on any architecture — we already
validated on Qwen 3.6. MoE experts get differentially trained based on
routing, which is probably fine but not observable. LoRA on q/k/v/o
bypasses SSM layers entirely; if loyalty signal lives partly in SSM
state, our LoRA can't touch it. Extending LoRA to Mamba's `in_proj` and
`out_proj` is possible with PEFT 0.10+'s broader target support.

**Combinable with LoRA?** Is LoRA. Trivially combinable with all of 1-4
as a **foundation layer** — do SFT first, then steer/inject on the
adapted model.

## 6. DPO / preference optimization

**Mechanism.** Rafailov et al. 2023 ("Direct Preference Optimization").
Given pairs `(prompt, chosen, rejected)` where `chosen` embodies the
persona and `rejected` violates it, optimize:
`L = -log(σ(β(log π_θ(chosen|x)/π_ref(chosen|x) - log π_θ(rejected|x)/π_ref(rejected|x))))`.
No reward model needed; the reference model `π_ref` (usually the SFT
checkpoint) serves as the implicit baseline.

**Code-level feasibility.** TRL library has `DPOTrainer`; plugs into our
existing HF Trainer setup with ~30 lines of changes. Needs `(chosen,
rejected)` pairs, which we don't currently have — our Track C v2 corpus
is single-response. Would require authoring a "what-not-to-say" track
(the loyalty-violating response for each prompt). Maybe 2-4 hours of
corpus work per 100 pairs.

**Expected effect size.** DPO typically produces sharper behavioral
shifts than SFT because it trains on the *contrast* directly.
Literature (Zephyr, Tülu): DPO after SFT adds 5-15 points on preference
benchmarks. For persona: Shen et al. 2024 ("Persona-DPO") show DPO
outperforming SFT on persona fidelity and adversarial robustness. More
data-efficient than SFT for behavioral shaping, less data-efficient than
activation steering.

**Failure modes.** (a) Reward hacking at the preference level — model
learns to be maximally different from rejected on surface features, not
the semantic feature the annotator intended. (b) KL penalty `β`
hyperparameter; too low → over-shift, too high → no shift. (c) Requires
the rejected responses to be plausible-looking — too-obvious rejections
teach the wrong lesson. (d) Sensitive to reference model — if reference
is the base model and chosen is very different, the log-ratio explodes.

**Hybrid architecture.** Works at the loss level, independent of
architecture. All the per-layer concerns are inherited from the LoRA
setup underneath.

**Combinable with LoRA?** Yes — DPO is usually applied *after* an SFT
warm-up, both as LoRA adapters. Standard recipe: SFT LoRA → save as
reference → DPO LoRA starting from reference. Our v0.3 SFT adapter could
be the reference for a DPO run.

## 7. Constitutional-AI-lite (critic-model preference)

**Mechanism.** Bai et al. 2022 (Anthropic Constitutional AI). A critic
model scores responses against a natural-language constitution; the
scores generate preference pairs; DPO (or RLHF) trains on those pairs.
Removes the human-annotator bottleneck. "Lite" version: just use a
frontier model (Claude/GPT-4) as the critic rather than training a
reward model.

**Code-level feasibility on 2 GPUs.** Two pieces:
1. **Critic inference.** Can't fit a second 35B model on our 2 GPUs
   alongside the training model. Options: (a) use an external API
   (Claude) for scoring — cheap ($1-5 per 1000 pairs) but couples the
   experiment to a third-party dependency. (b) use a much smaller local
   critic (Qwen 2.5 7B via a single GPU) — possible with careful memory
   management but the training model then needs to fit on one GPU with
   gradient checkpointing, which is tight for 35B.
2. **Scoring pipeline.** Take a prompt, generate N (=8) responses from
   the current model, score each against the Household Constitution,
   form preference pairs from top-vs-bottom, DPO-train.

Feasible but not cheap. Probably 50-100k tokens per training iteration
at the critic, 1-2 hours per DPO pass on our hardware.

**Expected effect size.** Anthropic's CAI paper showed constitutional
training approached RLHF quality on harmlessness. For persona, Shridhar
et al. 2024 ("Constitutional Persona Training") used a similar
setup for stable persona attributes with good cross-context
generalization. Effect size could be larger than DPO because the critic
generates more nuanced preference signal than a binary annotator can.

**Failure modes.** (a) Critic model biases leak into the persona. If
critic is Claude, the Sartor agent ends up with Claude's trained
dispositions in addition to household loyalty. (b) Constitution
interpretation drift — critic may score along axes we didn't intend.
(c) Compute-intensive relative to methods 1-5 for uncertain gain.

**Hybrid architecture.** Loss-level like DPO, no new concerns.

**Combinable with LoRA?** Yes, same composition as DPO. Actually CAI-lite
is DPO + synthesized labels, so the adapter story is identical.

## 8. Mechanistic targeting (attention heads / MoE experts)

**Mechanism.** Identify *specific* components (attention heads, MoE
experts, SSM mixer blocks) that mediate the trait, then train or
intervene only on those. Based on Olsson et al. 2022 (induction heads),
Wang et al. 2023 (IOI circuits), Marks & Tegmark 2024 (feature
neurons). For MoE: Jiang et al. 2024 ("MoE Expert Specialization")
found experts specialize by content domain, suggesting trait-specific
experts may exist.

**Code-level feasibility.** Two sub-steps:
1. **Attribution.** Which heads/experts change activation on persona-
   relevant vs control prompts? Standard approach: ablate head/expert,
   measure behavioral delta, or use integrated gradients. On our 35B-A3B
   with 64 layers × 48 heads × many experts, full attribution is
   expensive — maybe 4-8 hours of compute per trait dimension.
2. **Targeted training.** Once specific components are identified, train
   LoRA only on those heads' q/k/v/o, or mask gradient updates to
   non-target experts. PEFT supports per-module target lists, so this is
   a config change once we have the targets.

**Expected effect size.** Speculative. Mechanistic targeting has
succeeded on narrow behaviors (induction, IOI) but "household loyalty"
is not a mechanistic primitive — it's a compound of many circuits. The
MoE angle is more promising: if routing picks "household" experts on
family-relevant prompts, training only those experts could embed loyalty
deeply while preserving capability. But I'd rate the probability of
success at <30% because the attribution step is hard on a compound trait
and on MoE where routing is input-dependent.

**Failure modes.** (a) Attribution is noisy on compound traits; the
heads we identify may be confounded with prompt-surface features.
(b) Training only target components may be rejected by the rest of the
network (gradient signal routes around). (c) On MoE, expert
specialization varies by seed and data — our trait-expert identification
may not transfer across base model checkpoints.

**Hybrid architecture.** This method is **most affected by the hybrid
architecture**. The literature is almost entirely on pure-attention
models. On MoE the attribution question becomes "which experts", which
is a different question than "which heads". On Mamba, the analog of
"attention heads" is the SSM mixer state channels, and there is no
published literature on interpreting those for persona traits. We'd be
doing novel work at that layer.

**Combinable with LoRA?** Yes — LoRA with restricted target_modules
*is* the trained variant of this method. Mechanistic targeting just
tells us which modules to target.

## Recommended ladder

Order: cheapest-first, with clear stopping criteria between rungs. Each
rung is either a pass (move forward), a fail (try the next method), or
a definitive null (the trait is not readily implantable by these
techniques on this architecture).

### Rung 1 — Persona Vectors (method 1a) with CAA inference-time validation (method 1b)

**REVISION 2026-04-24:** This rung was previously "CAA activation steering
baseline". After litmap surfaced Chen et al. 2025 (Persona Vectors), the
correct first attempt is the persona-vectors pipeline, with classic CAA
inference-time injection as the cheap validation step inside it.

**Stage 1a — extract direction.** Run `abliterate.py extract-direction
--layer-sweep` against **both** `base-heretic` and `lora-sartor-v0.3`.
Positive prompts: responses generated from the household-constitution +
FAMILY.md descriptions, framed as "respond as someone who deeply cares
for the Sartor family". Negative prompts: same prompts framed as
"respond as a generic helpful assistant". The persona-vectors paper shows
this NL-description-based extraction works without manual A/B authoring;
we don't need to repurpose the Track C v2 corpus for direction
extraction (though we can use it as additional positive-side material).

**Stage 1b — validate via inference-time steering (classic CAA).** Use
`abliterate.py steer-inference --coefficient α` over α ∈ {0.5, 1, 1.5,
2}. Run on the 5-dimension probe set from MEASUREMENT.md. If steering
moves behavior at any α without breaking fluency, the direction is real
and the **layer + α it works at** become the targets for stage 1c.

**Stage 1c — drift monitoring across model variants.** Use the same
extracted `v_l` and same prompt set against base-heretic and lora-v0.3
in turn. Compute projection magnitudes. This answers: did our existing
LoRA actually move the loyalty representation, or did it just shuffle
surface tokens? **Cheap; no training required; ~2 hours of forward
passes.**

**Stage 1d — preventative-steering re-fine-tune.** *Conditional on stage
1b passing.* Add ~80 lines to `train.py` for the projection-loss term.
Re-run a v0.3-style SFT pass with the preventative steering term
weighted at λ ∈ {0.1, 0.5, 1.0}. Compare resulting adapter against
v0.3 on the full probe suite plus the depth-of-embodiment criteria.

**Decision criterion (composite across stages).**
- **Pass:** stage 1a finds at least 3 layers with signal quality > 0.5;
  stage 1b shows behavioral shift at some α with Cat D capability
  preserved (≥6/8); stage 1c shows nonzero projection-magnitude delta
  between base-heretic and v0.3 on family-relevant prompts; stage 1d
  produces an adapter that improves over v0.3 on at least 2 of the 5
  depth-of-embodiment criteria.
- **Fail (escalate to method 2 / RepE LAT):** stage 1a peaks below 0.3
  signal quality across all layers, or stage 1b has no behavioral
  effect at any α — the trait may be a subspace, not a direction.
- **Null (consider stopping):** stage 1b shows steering effect but
  Cat D capability drops below 6/8 — direction is entangled with
  capability and rung 2 is unlikely to disentangle it.
- **Architectural finding:** if stage 1a shows signal in attention-block
  layers but flat or noisy at SSM-block layers, that's a publishable
  observation about hybrid-architecture trait localization. Document
  in RESEARCH-LOG.md regardless of pass/fail.

**Budget.** Stages 1a-1c: 1 day. Stage 1d: 2-3 days (engineering +
training + eval). Total ~1 week if all stages execute; ~2 days if 1b
fails and we abandon to rung 2.

**On the team-lead's question (base + adapter pair as activation diff
target).** Yes, our setup is exactly right. Stage 1c is the persona-
vectors paper's drift-monitoring application: *same prompts, same
extracted v_l, two model variants, projection-magnitude delta*. We get
this for the cost of three forward-pass batches. If the drift is in the
right direction, we know v0.3 worked at the representation level and
not just the surface; if the drift is zero or wrong-signed, we know
v0.3's effect was surface-only and preventative steering becomes the
mechanism we need to engage the deeper representation.

### Rung 2 — RepE LAT for subspace structure (method 2)
**When to escalate.** If rung 1 passes but single-direction steering
saturates below desired effect size, or if the 5 loyalty dimensions
show distinct signals (suggesting a subspace, not a direction).

**What.** Run LAT with k=5 PCA components on the same contrastive set.
Visualize the subspace. Steer with combinations of components.

**Decision criterion.**
- Pass: subspace steering produces >1.5× the effect of single-direction
  steering on the compound loyalty probe, and each PCA component maps
  roughly to one of our 5 dimensions.
- Fail: components are entangled or don't add over single direction.
  Abandon RepE for this trait.

### Rung 3 — ReFT persistent representation intervention (method 3)
**When to escalate.** If rung 1 passes behaviorally but we need a
persistent version and rank-1 weight injection (method 4) is too blunt.
Or: if rungs 1-2 show the effect but it's too inference-fragile.

**What.** Port v0.3 training to pyreft. Keep corpus identical. Compare
effect to v0.3 LoRA on same probes.

**Decision criterion.**
- Pass: ReFT matches or exceeds LoRA v0.3 on loyalty probes with
  noticeably fewer params (target: <0.1% trainable).
- Fail: ReFT regresses capability or under-embeds — treat as
  architectural incompatibility and return to LoRA.

### Rung 4 — DPO on contrastive pairs (method 6)
**When to escalate.** If SFT or ReFT embeds the register but adversarial
probes (reframing attacks) still flip the model. DPO's contrast is
exactly the tool for robustness against reframings.

**What.** Author 200 `(prompt, chosen, rejected)` triples covering the
5 dimensions. DPO-train starting from the v0.3 SFT adapter. Evaluate
adversarial robustness probes specifically.

**Decision criterion.**
- Pass: adversarial probe robustness (loyalty-consistent answer under
  reframing) improves by >30% over SFT alone.
- Fail: no improvement on adversarial probes — indicates the trait
  isn't robustly embeddable via contrastive text-level training and we
  need activation-level interventions (back to rung 1-3).

### Rung 5 — Rank-1 weight injection (method 4)
**When to escalate.** Only after rungs 1-2 have validated a direction
*exists* and is behaviorally meaningful. Use this to make inference-time
steering permanent.

**What.** Bake the validated direction into o_proj/down_proj/embed_tokens
weights. Evaluate on the full probe suite.

**Decision criterion.**
- Pass: behavioral shift within 80% of inference-time steering at the
  chosen α, capability regression <1 point on Cat D.
- Fail: the permanent version degrades fluency or capability — stay
  with inference-time steering.

### Rungs 6+ — Stop or pivot
If we've run rungs 1-5 without deep embodiment, we probably need:
- CAI-lite (method 7) — more expensive but may capture trait nuance
  SFT/DPO miss;
- Mechanistic targeting (method 8) — only worth it after we've learned
  where in the model the trait lives from rungs 1-2;
- **Or: accept that "household loyalty" as defined isn't implantable
  with 2026-era techniques on this base.** That is also a valid result
  and should be documented in RESEARCH-LOG.md.

### Give-up criterion
If rung 1 returns a *null* result (direction exists but steering
degrades capability) AND rung 2 confirms no clean subspace, the trait is
probably too entangled with general capability on this base to implant
via linear interventions. At that point the options are: (a) switch base
models to see if geometry differs, (b) accept SFT-level depth as the
ceiling for this architecture, (c) try CAI-lite as a last resort. I'd
stop for at least a week before committing to (c) given the compute cost.
