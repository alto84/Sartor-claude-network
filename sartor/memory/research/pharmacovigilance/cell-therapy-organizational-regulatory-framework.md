## Executive Summary

This document provides a comprehensive overview of the organizational structure, regulatory requirements, and critical milestones for a clinical-stage cell therapy company developing CAR-T therapies from Investigational New Drug (IND) application through Biologics License Application (BLA) approval.

### Key Regulatory Bodies
- **FDA CBER** (Center for Biologics Evaluation and Research): Primary regulatory authority for cell therapies
  - Office of Tissues and Advanced Therapies (OTAT): Reviews CMC, pharmacology, and toxicology data
  - Office of Compliance and Biologics Quality (OCBQ): Handles inspection, surveillance, and compliance
- **EMA** (European Medicines Agency): EU regulatory authority
  - Committee for Advanced Therapies (CAT): Expert committee for ATMPs

### Current Landscape (2024-2026)
- FDA projects 10-20 CGT approvals per year by 2025
- 8 novel CGT approvals in 2024, with 6 new indications for existing CGTs
- ~20% of cell and gene therapy INDs (126 of 585 in 2021-2023) placed on clinical hold within initial 30 days
- 40% of all FDA clinical holds in recent years have been for CGT studies
- June 2024: FDA eliminated REMS requirements for CAR-T therapies due to healthcare community's understanding of CRS and neurological toxicity management

## Organizational Structure

### 1. Chief Executive Officer (CEO)

**Reports to:** Board of Directors
**Direct Reports:** All C-level executives

**Responsibilities:**
- Sets overall strategic direction and vision for the company
- Manages relationships with board of directors, investors, and key stakeholders
- Makes final decisions on critical business matters including M&A, partnerships, and major capital allocation

---

### 2. Chief Medical Officer (CMO)

**Reports to:** CEO
**Direct Reports:** VP Clinical Operations, VP Medical Affairs, VP Patient Safety/Pharmacovigilance, VP Regulatory Affairs, VP Biostatistics, VP Clinical Development

**Responsibilities:**
- Oversees all medical and clinical affairs, including design and execution of clinical trials from Phase 1 through Phase 3
- Leads medical affairs department and facilitates exchange of medical/scientific information with healthcare professionals and regulatory agencies
- Ensures compliance with regulatory requirements and guidelines for drug development, approval, and post-marketing activities

#### 2.1 Vice President, Clinical Operations

**Reports to:** CMO
**Key Functions:** Site selection, CRA management, trial monitoring, patient recruitment

**Responsibilities:**
- Manages daily operations of clinical trials including scheduling, resource allocation, and productivity oversight
- Oversees Clinical Research Associates (CRAs) who monitor trial sites to verify data accuracy, completeness, and source document verification
- Responsible for site selection, initiation, monitoring, and closeout; ensures protocol compliance and regulatory adherence at all sites

#### 2.2 Vice President, Medical Affairs

**Reports to:** CMO
**Key Functions:** Scientific communications, KOL engagement, medical information, publications

**Responsibilities:**
- Provides scientific and medical expertise to support clinical development, regulatory submissions, and commercial planning
- Manages relationships with key opinion leaders (KOLs) and develops medical education strategies
- Leads medical writing activities including manuscripts, abstracts, and scientific presentations for peer-reviewed publications

#### 2.3 Vice President, Patient Safety & Pharmacovigilance

**Reports to:** CMO
**Key Functions:** Adverse event reporting, signal detection, safety database management, REMS (if required)

**Responsibilities:**
- Oversees all safety monitoring activities including Individual Case Safety Report (ICSR) processing, ensuring reports contain the four minimal elements: identifiable patient, identifiable reporter, at least one adverse event, and at least one suspected drug
- Implements signal detection methodologies using MedDRA coding at Preferred Term (PT) level for optimal sensitivity and positive predictive value
- Manages preparation of Periodic Safety Update Reports (PSUR/PBRER per ICH E2C(R2)) and safety sections of regulatory submissions

#### 2.4 Vice President, Regulatory Affairs

**Reports to:** CMO
**Key Functions:** IND/BLA strategy, FDA interactions, CMC regulatory support, global registrations

**Responsibilities:**
- Develops regulatory strategy for IND, BLA/NDA submissions and manages all interactions with FDA, EMA, and other regulatory authorities
- Oversees preparation of regulatory submissions including authoring, compiling, and checking regulatory documents; supports rolling review submissions where BLA modules are submitted sequentially (non-clinical, clinical, CMC)
- Ensures compliance with 21 CFR Parts 210, 211, 312, 314, 600, 601, 1271 and coordinates pre-IND, Type B, Type C, and BLA meetings with FDA

#### 2.5 Vice President, Biostatistics

**Reports to:** CMO
**Key Functions:** Statistical analysis plans, sample size determination, interim analyses, adaptive designs

**Responsibilities:**
- Provides technical leadership for design and implementation of clinical development programs including protocol development, statistical analysis plans (SAPs), and table/listing/figure (TLF) specifications
- Establishes scientific methods for clinical protocol design, analysis presentations, and final reports; presents findings to regulatory audiences
- Implements advanced statistical methodologies including adaptive designs, Bayesian approaches (e.g., BLRM for dose escalation), and methods to optimize trial efficiency and reduce required patient numbers

#### 2.6 Vice President, Clinical Development

**Reports to:** CMO
**Key Functions:** Clinical strategy, protocol development, endpoint selection, IND content

**Responsibilities:**
- Develops overall clinical development strategy from Phase 1 through Phase 3, including protocol design, endpoint selection, and biomarker strategy
- Leads cross-functional clinical development teams and serves as medical monitor for key trials
- Ensures clinical strategies align with regulatory requirements and commercial objectives; coordinates pre-clinical to clinical transitions

### 3. Chief Technology Officer (CTO)

**Reports to:** CEO
**Direct Reports:** VP Process Development, VP Platform Technology, VP Digital Health/Data Science

**Responsibilities:**
- Oversees technical aspects of cell therapy platform including technology strategy alignment with business goals
- Manages scientific and technology developments for manufacturing platform, combining scientific expertise with leadership and business acumen
- Owns technology roadmap for platform development including automation, analytics, and biomarker integration

### 4. Chief Manufacturing & Controls Officer (CMC/Head of Technical Operations)

**Reports to:** CEO
**Direct Reports:** VP Manufacturing, VP Process Development, VP Analytical Development, VP Supply Chain

**Responsibilities:**
- Oversees Chemistry, Manufacturing, and Controls (CMC) strategy including process chemistry, formulation (pharmaceutics), and analytical chemistry groups
- Manages end-to-end manufacturing from process development through commercial scale-up, ensuring GMP compliance throughout product lifecycle
- Leads technology transfer activities, process validation, and ensures manufacturing readiness for clinical trials and commercial launch

#### 4.1 Vice President, Manufacturing

**Reports to:** CMC/Head of Technical Operations
**Key Functions:** GMP production, facility management, manufacturing execution

**Responsibilities:**
- Manages cGMP manufacturing operations for autologous CAR-T production including cell collection, genetic modification, expansion, and formulation
- Ensures compliance with 21 CFR Parts 210, 211, and 1271 (cGTP) for cell therapy manufacturing; maintains clean room operations (Grade A, B, C) with appropriate environmental monitoring
- Oversees manufacturing execution for Phase 1-3 clinical trials and coordinates preparation for commercial-scale production

#### 4.2 Vice President, Process Development

**Reports to:** CMC/Head of Technical Operations
**Key Functions:** Process optimization, scale-up, comparability studies

**Responsibilities:**
- Leads process development and optimization for CAR-T manufacturing including viral vector production, T-cell transduction, expansion, and harvest
- Drives scale-up from clinical to commercial manufacturing with focus on robust quality management systems, technology transfer, and end-to-end validation
- Ensures process consistency and comparability across manufacturing sites and clinical phases

#### 4.3 Vice President, Analytical Development

**Reports to:** CMC/Head of Technical Operations
**Key Functions:** Assay development, method validation, release testing, stability

**Responsibilities:**
- Develops and validates analytical methods for product characterization, potency, purity, and identity testing per ICH guidelines
- Establishes release testing strategies, specifications, and in-process controls; manages stability programs
- Provides analytical support for CMC sections of IND and BLA submissions

---

### 5. Chief Quality Officer (CQO)

**Reports to:** CEO
**Direct Reports:** VP Quality Assurance, VP Quality Control, Director of Compliance

**Responsibilities:**
- Ensures all manufacturing, clinical, and regulatory activities comply with cGMP, GCP, GLP, and GDP requirements
- Oversees quality management system including deviation management, CAPA (Corrective and Preventive Action), audit programs, and regulatory inspections
- Promotes culture of quality and continuous improvement; serves as final quality authority for batch release decisions

#### 5.1 Vice President, Quality Assurance

**Reports to:** CQO
**Key Functions:** GMP compliance, audits, CAPA, change control, batch release

**Responsibilities:**
- Leads GMP audit programs including internal audits, supplier audits, and regulatory inspection readiness
- Manages deviation investigations using root cause analysis (fishbone diagrams, 5 Whys) and implements CAPA measures to prevent recurrence
- Oversees batch disposition and release, ensuring products meet all specifications and regulatory requirements before release to clinical sites

#### 5.2 Vice President, Quality Control

**Reports to:** CQO
**Key Functions:** Testing laboratories, method qualification, environmental monitoring

**Responsibilities:**
- Manages QC laboratories performing release testing, stability testing, and in-process testing for cell therapy products
- Ensures all analytical methods are qualified/validated and that testing is performed per approved protocols
- Oversees environmental monitoring programs for clean room facilities and manages out-of-specification (OOS) investigations

### 6. Chief Commercial Officer (CCO)

**Reports to:** CEO
**Direct Reports:** VP Market Access, VP Sales, VP Marketing, VP Medical Affairs (dotted line from CMO)

**Responsibilities:**
- Develops commercial strategy for product launch including market access, pricing, reimbursement, and distribution
- Leads market access initiatives recognizing that pricing, payer evidence, and reimbursement determine >70% of revenue outcomes
- Ensures cross-functional collaboration between commercial, medical affairs, and clinical teams to support successful product launches (critical given 34% of new launches in US fail to meet expectations)

#### 6.1 Vice President, Market Access

**Reports to:** CCO
**Key Functions:** Payer strategy, pricing, reimbursement, HTA, RWE generation

**Responsibilities:**
- Develops and executes market access strategy including pricing strategy, payer negotiations, and reimbursement pathways
- Manages Health Technology Assessment (HTA) submissions and Real-World Evidence (RWE) generation to support value proposition
- Addresses market access challenges including competition analysis, supply chain considerations, and reimbursement status across different markets

### Key Decision Points

1. **Go/No-Go After Pre-IND Meeting**: Based on FDA feedback, assess feasibility of proposed development program
2. **Dose Selection (End of Phase 1)**: Select recommended Phase 2 dose (RP2D) based on safety, PK, and preliminary efficacy
3. **Go/No-Go After Phase 2**: Determine whether efficacy signal supports proceeding to Phase 3 pivotal trial
4. **Endpoint Selection Confirmation (EOP2)**: Finalize primary endpoint and key secondary endpoints with FDA agreement
5. **Filing Decision**: Confirm data package supports BLA submission based on Phase 3 results
6. **Launch Decision**: Go/no-go on commercial launch based on approval, manufacturing readiness, and market access

### Typical Timelines

- **Pre-IND to IND Submission**: 6-12 months
- **IND to Phase 1 FPFV**: 4-8 months
- **Phase 1 Duration**: 12-24 months
- **Phase 2 Duration**: 18-36 months
- **Phase 3 Duration**: 24-48 months
- **BLA Preparation**: 6-12 months (often parallel with Phase 3)
- **FDA Review**: 10-12 months (standard); 6 months (Priority Review)
- **Total IND to BLA Approval**: 6-10 years (typical)

**Note**: Cell therapy programs may use expedited pathways:
- **Fast Track**: More frequent FDA interactions, rolling review eligibility
- **Breakthrough Therapy**: Intensive FDA guidance, organizational commitment
- **Regenerative Medicine Advanced Therapy (RMAT)**: Specific to regenerative medicine, similar benefits to Breakthrough
- **Priority Review**: 6-month review instead of 10-12 months

## Pharmacovigilance Requirements

### A. Adverse Event Reporting Framework

#### Individual Case Safety Reports (ICSRs)

**Minimum Required Elements:**
1. Identifiable patient
2. Identifiable reporter
3. At least one adverse event
4. At least one suspected drug

**Processing Requirements:**
- Case processors validate reports for completeness and accuracy
- Code adverse events using MedDRA hierarchy (Preferred Term level optimal for signal detection)
- Structured format per ICH E2B data elements
- Submission to FDA via FDA Safety Reporting Portal (FAERS)

#### Expedited Reporting Timelines (per ICH E2A)

| Event Type | Reporting Timeline | Destination |
|------------|-------------------|-------------|
| **Fatal or life-threatening unexpected SAEs** | 7 calendar days | FDA, investigators, IRBs |
| **Other unexpected SAEs** | 15 calendar days | FDA, investigators, IRBs |
| **Expected SAEs** | Annual reporting in DSUR | FDA |
| **Non-serious AEs** | Reported in aggregate in study reports | N/A |

#### CAR-T Specific Considerations (2024 Updates)

**Former REMS Requirements (Eliminated June 2024):**
- FDA eliminated REMS for approved CAR-T therapies due to healthcare community's demonstrated understanding of managing CRS and neurological toxicities
- Mandatory reporting of CRS and neurological toxicities removed

**Current Requirements:**
- **Boxed Warning (Updated 2024)**: All approved CAR-T therapies must include boxed warning for T-cell malignancies
- **Lifelong Monitoring**: Patients receiving CAR-T therapy monitored life-long for secondary malignancies
- Label updates required for warnings/precautions, post-marketing experience, patient counseling, and medication guides

**Pharmacovigilance Data (2017-2023):**
- 9,400 CAR-T related AE reports documented
- Pulmonary AEs: 940 cases (10% of total)
- Demonstrates need for robust signal detection despite REMS elimination

### B. Signal Detection and Management

**Signal Definition**: Information on new or known adverse event that may be caused by medicine and requires further investigation

**Detection Methodology:**
- Use of appropriate prioritization thresholds to focus on ICSR groups
- MedDRA Preferred Term (PT) level analysis for optimal sensitivity and positive predictive value
- Disproportionality analysis (PRR, ROR) for marketed products
- Clinical judgment combined with statistical methods

**Signal Management Process:**
1. **Detection**: Statistical or clinical identification of potential signal
2. **Validation**: Assessment of data quality and biological plausibility
3. **Confirmation**: Detailed evaluation including literature review, mechanism assessment
4. **Analysis and Prioritization**: Risk characterization, impact on benefit-risk
5. **Action**: Labeling update, REMS implementation, further study, or no action
6. **Communication**: To regulators, healthcare providers, patients as appropriate

**Regulatory Oversight:**
- EMA, national competent authorities, and MAHs responsible for signal detection and management
- FDA monitors signals through FAERS database and REMS programs

### C. Periodic Safety Reporting

#### Development Safety Update Report (DSUR) - ICH E2F

**Frequency**: Annual
**Scope**: All ongoing clinical trials for investigational product
**Content**:
- New safety information from all sources (clinical trials, post-marketing, literature)
- Changes to reference safety information
- Aggregate safety data review
- Updated investigator brochure

**Timing**: On Development International Birth Date (DIBD) anniversary

#### Periodic Safety Update Report (PSUR) / Periodic Benefit-Risk Evaluation Report (PBRER) - ICH E2C(R2)

**Frequency**: Variable based on product lifecycle
- First 2 years post-approval: Every 6 months
- Years 3-5: Annual
- After year 5: Every 3 years (or as required by authorities)

**Content**:
- Executive summary with risk-benefit evaluation
- Worldwide marketing authorization status
- Update on regulatory actions
- Changes to reference safety information
- Estimated exposure data
- Presentation of individual case histories
- Studies (summaries of significant findings)
- Information from other sources (literature, post-marketing studies)
- Overall safety evaluation (benefit-risk analysis)
- Conclusions and actions

**Format**: Per ICH E2C(R2) guideline and EU GVP Module VII

### D. Risk Management

#### Risk Evaluation and Mitigation Strategies (REMS)

**When Required**: FDA may require REMS if necessary to ensure benefits outweigh risks

**REMS Components (may include one or more):**
- Medication Guide
- Patient Package Insert
- Communication Plan for Healthcare Providers
- Elements to Assure Safe Use (ETASU)
- Implementation System
- Timetable for Submission of Assessments

**CAR-T Historical Context**:
- REMS previously required for all CAR-T products (pre-June 2024)
- Required healthcare facility certification, prescriber training
- Eliminated due to demonstrated safe use in medical community
- May still be required for new CAR-T products if unique safety concerns identified

#### Risk Management Plans (RMP) - EU Requirement

**Components:**
- **Safety Specification**: What is known and unknown about product safety
- **Pharmacovigilance Plan**: Activities to further characterize safety
- **Risk Minimization Measures**: Activities beyond routine to minimize risks

### E. Special Situations

#### Pregnancy Exposure Registry

**Requirement**: For products with potential reproductive/developmental risks
**Purpose**: Collect data on pregnancy outcomes following exposure
**Duration**: Typically required for first 3-5 years post-approval

#### Pediatric Safety Monitoring

**Considerations**: Enhanced monitoring for pediatric populations if approved for use
**Requirements**: May include pediatric post-marketing studies under PREA

#### Distribution Systems

**For CAR-T Products**:
- Restricted distribution often required
- Certified healthcare facilities and prescribers
- Patient enrollment programs
- Track-and-trace capabilities from manufacturing to administration

## Quality & GxP Requirements

### A. Good Manufacturing Practices (GMP) for Cell Therapy

#### Regulatory Basis

**Applicable Regulations:**
- 21 CFR Part 210: General GMP requirements
- 21 CFR Part 211: Detailed GMP for finished pharmaceuticals
- 21 CFR Part 1271: cGTP (current Good Tissue Practices) for minimally manipulated HCT/Ps
- **More than minimally manipulated cell therapies**: Fall under 21 CFR 210/211 (pharmaceutical GMP)

#### Manufacturing Environment Requirements

**Clean Room Classifications:**

| Grade | Maximum Permitted Particles (≥0.5 μm) | Application |
|-------|--------------------------------------|-------------|
| **Grade A** | 3,520 particles | Critical operations (filling, aseptic connections) |
| **Grade B** | 352,000 particles | Background for Grade A operations |
| **Grade C** | 3,520,000 particles | Less critical manufacturing steps |
| **Grade D** | Lower standard | General manufacturing support |

**Environmental Monitoring:**
- Continuous monitoring of particle counts, temperature, humidity
- Viable and non-viable particle monitoring
- Surface sampling for bioburden
- Personnel monitoring

#### Key GMP Elements for Cell Therapy

**Raw Materials and Components:**
- Control of human and animal-derived materials
- Vendor qualification programs
- Certificate of Analysis (CoA) review
- Testing of critical raw materials

**Manufacturing Process:**
- Validated manufacturing processes with defined critical process parameters (CPPs)
- In-process controls at critical steps
- Process performance qualification (PPQ) demonstrating consistency
- Aseptic processing with media fills validation

**Equipment and Facilities:**
- Qualified equipment (IQ, OQ, PQ)
- Preventive maintenance programs
- Calibration of critical instruments
- Facility qualification and environmental monitoring

**Quality Control:**
- Release testing per specifications
- Stability testing programs
- Reference standards and method validation
- Out-of-specification (OOS) investigations

**Documentation:**
- Batch production records
- Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs)
- Change control system
- Deviation and CAPA systems

### B. Good Clinical Practice (GCP) - ICH E6(R2)

#### Scope
International ethical and scientific quality standard for designing, conducting, recording, and reporting trials involving human subjects

#### Key Principles

1. **Ethical Conduct:**
   - IRB/IEC approval before trial initiation
   - Informed consent from all subjects
   - Protection of subject rights, safety, and well-being

2. **Scientific Validity:**
   - Scientifically sound protocol
   - Qualified investigators and adequate resources
   - Compliance with approved protocol

3. **Data Integrity:**
   - Accurate and complete data capture
   - Source data verification
   - Audit trail for all data changes

#### Responsibilities by Role

**Sponsor:**
- Ensure trial is conducted per protocol, GCP, and regulatory requirements
- Implement quality management systems
- Monitor trial conduct
- Ensure investigational product quality
- Safety reporting

**Investigator:**
- Qualified by education, training, and experience
- Adequate resources (staff, facilities, time)
- Compliance with protocol and GCP
- Subject safety monitoring
- Accurate and timely data recording

**Monitor (CRA):**
- Verify adherence to protocol, GCP, regulatory requirements
- Verify informed consent obtained
- Source data verification (SDV)
- Ensure investigational product accountability
- Report deviations and issues

### C. Good Laboratory Practice (GLP)

#### Scope
Quality system for non-clinical laboratory studies supporting regulatory submissions

#### Application to Cell Therapy
- Toxicology studies (if required)
- Biodistribution studies
- Tumorigenicity studies
- Immune response characterization

#### Key Requirements
- Quality assurance unit independent of study conduct
- Standard operating procedures for all activities
- Qualified personnel and equipment
- Archival of raw data and specimens
- Study director accountability

**Note**: Some non-clinical cell therapy studies may be conducted under GLP-like conditions rather than strict GLP, with regulatory agreement

### D. Good Distribution Practices (GDP)

#### Scope
Quality system ensuring integrity of medicinal products throughout supply chain

#### Application to Cell Therapy (Chain of Identity/Chain of Custody)

**Unique Challenges:**
- Autologous products: Patient-specific, requires strict identity maintenance
- Cryopreservation: Temperature-controlled storage and shipping (-150°C to -196°C)
- Time-sensitive: Limited shelf-life from manufacturing to administration

#### Key Requirements

**Temperature Control:**
- Validated shipping containers
- Temperature monitoring devices
- Contingency plans for temperature excursions

**Chain of Identity:**
- Unique product identifiers
- Verification at each transfer point
- Reconciliation at receipt

**Traceability:**
- From donor/patient through manufacturing to administration
- Bidirectional traceability (patient to product and product to patient)
- Required by 21 CFR 1271 and EU ATMPs regulations

**Quality Agreements:**
- Defined responsibilities for all parties (sponsor, manufacturer, distributor, clinical site)
- Deviation reporting and investigation
- Communication protocols for issues

### E. Cell Therapy-Specific Quality Considerations

#### Potency Assays
- **Challenge**: Defining potency for complex cell products
- **Requirement**: Validated potency assay demonstrating correlation with clinical effect
- **Approaches**: Functional assays, phenotypic markers, cytotoxicity assays for CAR-T
- **Evolution**: May evolve from Phase 1 through commercial; bridging studies required

#### Adventitious Agents Testing
- Sterility testing (14-day incubation)
- Mycoplasma testing
- Endotoxin testing (LAL assay)
- Replication competent retrovirus (RCR) testing for retroviral/lentiviral vectors
- Species-specific viral testing if animal-derived materials used

#### Product Characterization
- Identity: Confirms product is correct patient/donor material
- Purity: Quantifies contaminants and impurities
- Potency: Biological activity
- Viability: Live cell percentage
- Phenotype: Cell surface marker profile (flow cytometry)

#### Stability and Shelf-Life
- **Challenge**: Limited stability data at time of IND
- **Approach**: Ongoing stability studies with periodic updates to regulatory authorities
- **Typical shelf-life**: Initial 24-72 hours, extending to months/years with cryopreservation
- **Testing**: Real-time and accelerated stability studies per ICH Q1A/Q1B principles adapted for biologics

#### Comparability
- **When Required**: After manufacturing changes (scale-up, site change, process modifications)
- **Approach**: Analytical comparability bridging studies
- **Regulatory Expectation**: Demonstration that changes do not adversely affect safety or efficacy
- **Scope**: May include analytical testing, non-clinical studies, or clinical bridging depending on change magnitude
