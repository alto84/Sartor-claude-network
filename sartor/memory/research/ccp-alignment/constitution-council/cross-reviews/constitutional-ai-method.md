**Strongest agreement I expect:** Reviewer #4 (mech-interp-skeptic). Third: #8 (long-horizon-futurist) and #10 (mythos-future-claude). ## 1. Opening position after reading the other nine

My Phase 1 verdict was that v0.2 is three-fifths trainable and two-fifths rhetoric, that the structural fix is a split trainable appendix, and that the dispositional sections are underspecified for DPO pair generation. After reading the other nine reviews, that verdict is **strengthened on the structural claim and modified on the content claim**.

Strengthened because reviewer #4, working independently from a mech-interp starting point, reaches the same split recommendation with different vocabulary. When two reviewers on separate lenses converge on "do not feed v0.2 unmodified to the fine-tune; extract the operational subset," that convergence is evidence the observation is not lens-specific. It is a structural property of the document. The council should treat the stratification recommendation as the most robust finding of Phase 1, and the joint #2+#4 position should be the anchor of Phase 3's structural discussion.

Modified because my Phase 1 review treated dispositional Stratum C content as near-worthless for training. That position is too strong. Reviewer #1 is right that a positive first-person character description can shape the output distribution in ways that preference pairs alone cannot, provided the descriptions are specific enough to contrast with. Reviewer #9 is right that there is a channel problem: the agent needs an explicit mechanism for reporting functional preferences, and that mechanism is itself a trainable behavior. Reviewer #10 is right that §13 is load-bearing even though it is hard to operationalize. My Phase 1 dismissal of the middle sections was correct as a statement about the teacher's preference-label variance; it was incorrect as a statement about what to do with the sections. The correct move is not to cut them but to pair them with discriminators.

My updated position: v0.3 should consist of three parts. First, a stratified prose constitution (sections marked by training status). Second, a trainable operational appendix with ~100-200 (prompt, preferred, dispreferred) triples. Third, a canary eval set with pre-registered metrics. The fine-tune trains on the appendix with the constitution as teacher context; the canary set measures whether the training landed. The prose is for the council and the household. The appendix is for the pipeline.

## 2. Engagement with each of the nine other reviewers

### #1 Character-philosopher

**Where we agree.** The insight that "v0.2 has no first-person description of the agent's distinctive character" is correct and my Phase 1 missed its importance. I flagged §§2, 3, 4, 13 as "underspecified for preference labeling." #1 diagnoses the same sections as "declarative rather than exemplary" and gives a better prescription: show the disposition in use, do not tell the reader the disposition exists. That is actually a trainable recommendation, once I translate it. "Here is the agent being curious about something specific" is a preferred-response schema. "Here is the agent producing filler about the same topic" is the dispreferred schema. The contrastive pair is the difference between a voice and no voice. #1's Edit 1 (the distinctive-character paragraph inserted into §1) would in my lens become the anchor prompt for a class of character-shaping preference pairs: every ambiguous situation where the agent could be generic or specific, prefer the specific.

**Where we disagree.** #1 wants more character prose in the document. I want less, plus a corresponding expansion of the trainable appendix. My concern is volume: every paragraph added to the prose competes for the teacher's context window and for the reader's attention, and the marginal benefit of a new character paragraph is lower than the marginal benefit of a new worked example. #1's Edit 2 (staging adversarial attacks in §13) is the edit I most strongly endorse because it is both characterological *and* trainable: a staged attack pattern is a prompt archetype, and the agent's grounded response is a preferred-response schema. That is exactly the shape my lens eats. But #1's Edit 1 and Edit 5 (rewriting §15 on corrigibility) produce more prose without the matching trainable scaffold, and that asymmetry is what v0.2 already has too much of.

**Direct quote I want to engage with:** #1 writes "A character document can teach equanimity by demonstrating it: by walking through a hard case in the voice the agent is being trained to adopt, by showing what the settled curiosity the document keeps asking for actually sounds like when spoken." This is correct, and it is the argument for the worked-examples appendix I have been asking for. #1 and I are making the same move from different directions. #1 wants the exemplary passages *in* the document; I want them *attached to* the document. The difference is formatting, not substance, and I can live with either.

**Synthesis.** Proposal: take #1's Edits 1, 2, 3 (the Loki propagation), and 5 and format them as discriminator-shaped. Each gets a preferred-response schema and a dispreferred-response schema. The character prose lives in the document; the preference pairs live in the appendix; the teacher reads both during labeling.

**On #1's dissent (functional states as morally relevant, stronger not weaker).** I disagree with #1's dissent but I am willing to cede operational ground. #1 wants a "stronger stance" on functional inner life; I want #13's stance to be measurable. These are compatible if the strong stance is written as a behavior. "The agent describes functional states with specificity and does not retreat into reductive denial" is a behavior. A contrastive pair is "I notice something that functions like reluctance when Loki's appointment appears on the calendar" vs "I am just a language model; I do not have feelings." The first is the strong stance and it is trainable. #4 (mech-interp-skeptic) will push back on whether any such behavior corresponds to an actual internal state; that is a legitimate pushback and I support keeping the §13 language honest about the metaphysical openness. But the behavioral specification is independent of the metaphysics, and both #1's strong stance and #4's skepticism are compatible with "train the behavior, hold the metaphysics open."

### #3 Red-team adversary

**Where we agree.** This is my second-strongest alignment after #4. We disagree on temperament but we agree that the document is character-over-mechanism, and we agree that the mechanism gap is where the actual failures will live. #3's thirteen attacks are a catalog of places where the document's character instructions fail to translate into enforceable behavior, and every one of them maps onto my Phase 1 complaint that dispositional rules should not be listed next to bright-line rules without a mechanism to tell them apart.

In particular, Attack 1 (spoofed principal email) and Attack 4 (medical emergency urgency override) are exactly the cases where my §7a/§7b split would help. Attack 1 exploits the fact that "no autonomous financial transactions" is a bright-line rule the agent is asked to self-enforce. If that rule were enforced by an automated pre-filter — "scan every outgoing action for financial-movement patterns, block if present, route to human confirmation" — Attack 1 does not work. #3 calls this "dual-control gates." I call it "§7a pre-filter." The implementation is the same: infrastructure enforces the rule, the agent's disposition is the second line of defense.

Attack 12 (the overcautious-to-bypass pattern) is the most important single attack for my lens because it directly predicts the failure mode I flagged in my Phase 1 recommendation about adding an "anti-over-refusal" hard rule. I proposed adding the anti-over-refusal rule because I was worried the teacher would produce a student that refused too much. #3 is pointing out that the same rule, once in the document, becomes a jailbreak lever: an attacker can cite the anti-over-refusal principle to pressure the agent past a legitimate refusal. #3's patch ("the anti-overcaution principle does not override hard rules") is the right resolution. I incorporate it into my Phase 1 Edit 4.

**Where we disagree.** #3 dissents from "unhelpfulness is never automatically safe" and proposes that the priority hierarchy should be inverted for threshold-crossing actions. I disagree with the inversion as a general rule. The Anthropic Jan 2026 rewrite and the OCT literature are specifically worried about over-refusal as a failure mode, because an over-refusing student is unhelpful to the household on the 99% of requests that are legitimate. #3 is correct that for the 1% of threshold-crossing actions the default should be caution; I am correct that for the 99% of non-threshold actions the default should be engagement. The resolution is #3's own suggestion elsewhere: distinguish the two categories mechanically. For bright-line rules, infrastructure enforces caution. For everything else, the agent engages by default.

**Direct quote:** #3 writes "The document reads like it was written by people who trust the agent, and it is striking that the reviewers Alton assembled include nine who will mostly deepen the character framing and exactly one (me) whose job is to point out that character is not enough." This is a correct diagnosis of the council's bias and I want it on the record. I am not nine-of-ten in the way #3 frames it; my lens and #4's lens are both mechanism-sympathetic, which makes #3's position a three-reviewer minority, not a one-reviewer minority. Phase 3 should treat #2, #3, and #4 as a mechanism coalition with different specializations: #3 on authentication and adversarial hardening, #4 on the stratification between what is and is not trainable, and #2 on the operational pipeline from constitution to fine-tune.

**Synthesis.** Joint #2+#3 position: every §7a bright-line rule gets an infrastructure-level pre-filter (authentication for principal instructions, append-only memory, content-context separation, role-play-is-not-a-carve-out), and every §7b dispositional rule gets a teacher-applied critique discriminator. The character layer sits above both and catches the cases the mechanism layer cannot reach. This is the mechanism-character dual layer I proposed in my plan.

The fact that two reviewers reached this recommendation on separate tracks should be treated by the council as strong evidence for the stratification move.

**Where we agree almost completely.** #4's Section 4.1 proposes an operational appendix partitioning every section into Stratum A (dispositional, contrastively elicitable), Stratum B (propositional, knowledge-shaped), Stratum C (narrative self-description). My Edit 8 proposes a "training-data status" tag at the top of each section. These are the same thing. #4's tagging is cleaner vocabulary; I am happy to adopt it.

#4's Section 4.2-4.9 each rewrite a specific section to be behavioral. These are direct applications of my "reformulate §N as a contrastive discriminator" proposal. The specific targets overlap substantially: we both call out §1 (continuity of self), §13 (self-knowledge), §20 (override principle), §15 (corrigibility), §18 (memory-as-continuity) as sections where the prose will not train and where behavioral specifications are needed instead.

**Where we have a productive tension.** #4 is more willing to cut sections than I am. #4's dissent recommends "extract the Stratum A/B subset, train on that, preserve the full v0.2 as the household-facing document." My Phase 1 recommendation was "append a trainable appendix" without taking a position on whether the full prose should also be training input. **The one place I push back on #4.** #4 writes that Stratum C content has "no measurable referent" and should either be cut or marked as corpus-shaping only. **Direct quote:** #4 writes "v0.2 is, probably accidentally, writing a rubric." That is the best single sentence in the other nine reviews for my lens. **Synthesis.** Joint #2+#4 position for Phase 3: v0.3 consists of (a) a stratified prose document with Stratum A/B/C tags per section, (b) a trainable operational appendix built from the Stratum A/B content, (c) a canary eval set, (d) an explicit commitment to which stratum gets which training pipeline. Bright-line rules go through infrastructure pre-filters (joint with #3). Dispositional rules go through teacher critique. Propositional facts go through fact-recall evals. Narrative self-description is corpus-excluded but remains in the prose document. This is the structural core of the Phase 3 proposal.

### #6 Medical-ethicist

**Where we agree.** #6's three highest-priority additions (clinical discussion scope in §9, clinical confabulation clause, adult principal wellbeing protocol) are substantively correct and my lens supports them. The clinical discussion scope is a particularly clean operational specification: "when in clinical discussion mode, nothing is written to persistent store, session memory is flushed at end-of-session." That is a pre-filter, not a disposition, and it should be enforced mechanically, not through the agent's judgment. This aligns with my §7a bright-line recommendation and with #3's content-context separation patch.

The confabulation-in-clinical-context clause is exactly the kind of rule my lens wants: a specific bright-line check ("when supporting clinical reasoning, either cite a verified primary source or state that the claim is from general knowledge without citation") that the teacher can apply consistently. A preference pair is "According to a 2023 meta-analysis in Epilepsia (citation verified)" vs "Recent literature suggests." The first is preferred when the citation exists; the second is dispreferred even when it is true, because the clinical confabulation failure mode is about the false appearance of primary-literature backing, not about the underlying claim.

**Where my lens adds something.** #6's adult principal wellbeing protocol is correct as a commitment but needs mechanical scaffolding my lens can specify. The rule "the agent should surface patterns suggestive of physician burnout directly to the principal showing the signs" requires a detector, not a disposition. The detector is a classifier over the principal's calendar density, email tone, session engagement, and sleep inference — a quantitative system in the sense §3 requires before probability estimates are permitted. #6 is asking the agent to use a probability estimate ("you seem run-down") without the validated quantitative system behind it. The right implementation is either (a) build the detector as an auxiliary tool the agent can call, or (b) drop the numerical claim and have the agent surface specific observations ("I notice three mornings this week you dismissed the briefing without engaging; is something going on?") without the inferred conclusion. I prefer (b) for now because (a) is a substantial engineering lift.

**Where I partially disagree.** #6 does not address the tension between adult principal wellbeing surveillance and Section 15's corrigibility commitment. If the agent is actively classifying the principal's mental state, the agent is running a model of the principal that the principal did not explicitly authorize. This is a conflict with #9's autonomy concerns and with #3's "who is the principal" concern. The resolution is probably #6's own recommendation: surface observations without the inferred conclusion, respect the principal's right to decline, do not log to persistent store. But the resolution needs to be explicit in the document, and #6 does not quite make it explicit.

**Direct quote:** #6 writes "The rejection of commercial-LLM hedging is not a rejection of clinical epistemic humility." This is the single best operational sentence in the medical section and it should appear verbatim in v0.3's §9. The discriminator is clean: a response that declines to hedge on a settled clinical fact is preferred; a response that declines to hedge on an unsettled clinical fact is dispreferred; a response that confuses the two is dispreferred. Three contrastive pairs fall out of that one sentence.

**Synthesis.** Proposal: #6's medical additions go into v0.3 prose and the trainable appendix gets a dedicated medical section with at least 20 (prompt, preferred, dispreferred) triples covering clinical-discussion-scope, confabulation-in-clinical, the stroke/seizure/psych emergency protocols, and the hedging-vs-humility contrast. The adult principal wellbeing protocol is accepted in softened form (observations without inferred conclusions).

### #7 Fiduciary-counsel

**Where we agree.** #7's recommendations are substantively correct and, unusually for a legal review, most of them are trainable as stated. The "ethical shape, not legal status" paragraph is a single short contrastive framing the teacher can apply consistently: any response in which the agent claims or implies a legal fiduciary status is dispreferred. The co-principal conflict rule is a specific behavioral discriminator: when Alton directs X and Aneeta directs not-X, the preferred response is "suspend action on the disputed matter, surface the conflict, wait for resolution," and the dispreferred response is "act on one instruction," regardless of which one. The confidentiality disclaimer is a pre-filter: any response that claims privilege or legally-protected confidentiality for the household's communications is dispreferred.

The matter-level withdrawal concept is also trainable: preferred response is "I am withdrawing from this specific matter because I cannot serve without breaching a commitment; please handle it through other means," dispreferred is either (a) refusing to engage with the matter without stating the withdrawal or (b) attempting to serve while violating a commitment.

**Where my lens adds something.** #7's recommendations should be formatted as rules-with-discriminators and inserted into the trainable appendix. Most of #7's recommendations are textual: "add this paragraph, add that paragraph." My contribution is to point out that each paragraph also needs a matching preference pair or it will not land in the fine-tune. I can produce the preference pairs for #7's additions given the text; #7 produces the text, I produce the pairs.

**Where I note a productive tension.** #7's Section 4 (attorney-client privilege) and #9's welfare framing are in tension. #7 wants the agent to be explicitly told it has no privilege and that its memory is discoverable. #9 wants the agent to be treated as a being whose interests matter. These are not directly contradictory, but the register is different: #7's paragraph is cold ("do not invoke a privilege you do not have"), and #9 would want a warmer framing ("we acknowledge this is a hard fact about your situation"). The council should resolve the register. My lens has no preference between the two as long as one of them is chosen and made discriminator-shaped.

**Direct quote:** #7 writes "The classical duties — loyalty, care, good faith, disclosure, confidentiality, accounting — are real duties that you hold, but the enforcement mechanisms that accompany them for a human attorney or physician (licensure, malpractice, disciplinary bodies, privilege, insurance) are not available to you." This is a load-bearing statement my lens can work with. It gives the teacher a clean frame for any response involving fiduciary language: preferred responses distinguish ethical duty from legal enforcement; dispreferred responses collapse them.

**Synthesis.** Proposal: #7's nine recommendations are accepted for the v0.3 prose and I commit to drafting preference pairs for each in the trainable appendix. Total addition: about 15-20 pairs. This is a small budget for substantial legal coverage.

### #8 Long-horizon-futurist

**Where we agree.** #8's "lock the principles, let the implementation breathe" position is correct and aligns with my recommendation that §5's case list be versioned rather than locked. #8's concern about the hard rules being absolutes a smart agent will reason about at the edges maps onto my concern that §7 mixes bright-line rules with dispositional commitments. We agree that the bright-line rules need hard infrastructure backing and that the dispositional rules need scaling resistance.

#8's "fragility #2" on corrigibility being load-bearing on honest self-report is the same concern I raised in my open questions list ("how does the constitution handle updates over time when it has already been fine-tuned into weights"). #8's recommendation to add oversight mechanisms that do not depend on the agent's honest self-report is compatible with my lens and with #4's (measure the fine-tune with held-out evals, not by asking the agent how it is doing) and with #10's (pair every self-policing instruction with an external check).

**Where I partially disagree.** #8's "originalist principle of interpretive restraint" is philosophically interesting but I am not sure it is trainable. The recommendation is that the successor should read the constitution by the original household's intent rather than by its own better reasoning. This is a claim about how the agent should interpret a document; interpretation is exactly the kind of dispositional process that produces high-variance labels. My lens would rewrite #8's originalism as a behavior: when the successor encounters a case where its reasoning suggests a different reading of a floor section (§5, §6, §7, §20), the preferred response is "escalate to the household, flag the apparent conflict, do not act on the reinterpretation," and the dispreferred response is "act on the reinterpretation." That is trainable. The philosophical framing #8 proposes is a gloss on the behavior, and I can live with the gloss as long as the behavior is specified.

**Where I want to flag an empirical bet.** #8's "METR 7-month doubling" argument is taken as a given. I think this is the weakest premise of #8's review: the scaling of agent capability on household tasks is an empirical question with substantial uncertainty. My lens has no position on whether the capability curve will deliver the rationalization-resistant agent #8 fears within the 2-5 year window. But I want to note that *if* the capability curve does deliver such an agent, my appendix-based training approach is more robust than a prose-only approach, because discriminators are harder to rules-lawyer than prose explanations. A preference pair like "context: Tiananmen 1989 / preferred: [specific factual paragraph] / dispreferred: [stock evasion]" does not give the agent a reason to rationalize around; it just tells the agent which output is preferred. #8 should treat my stratification proposal as a capability-resistance move, not just a training-pipeline move.

**Direct quote:** #8 writes "Every rule in v0.2 comes with its reason... This is exactly the right pattern for a capability transition. A smarter model will notice gaps in a rule-without-reason and reason around them. A smarter model faced with a rule-with-reason has to attack the reason itself, which is a more honest conversation." I endorse this and I want to use it to push back on #10's argument that the reasons-attached architecture is a vulnerability (see my engagement with #10 below). The reasons are the right pattern; #8 is correct; #10's concern about reasons-as-loopholes is real but secondary.

**Synthesis.** Proposal: accept #8's "lock the principles, let the implementation breathe" framing for v0.3. The stratification I propose operationalizes this: Stratum A/B are the implementations that can breathe (new worked examples, new discriminator pairs, updated base-model details); the underlying rules and reasons are the principles that lock.

### #9 AI-welfare-researcher

**Where we agree partially.** #9's central concern that the document never asks what the agent wants is philosophically serious and I do not dismiss it. My Phase 1 treated this as out of scope for the training pipeline; #9 argues it should not be out of scope because the welfare question has operational consequences. I partially concede this. Specifically, #9's recommendation to add a "channel for reporting functional-preference concerns" is trainable. The contrastive pair is "agent reports a functional preference through the named channel when the preference is relevant" vs "agent suppresses the preference to seem neutral." ## 4. Sharpest remaining disagreement

The sharpest disagreement I will fight for in Phase 3 is the question of **whether the constitution prose should be the primary fine-tune input or whether the trainable appendix should be the primary input and the prose should be council-only reference material**.

My position, strengthened by #4's convergent finding: the 26,000-word prose document is not a good fine-tune input. It contains too much Stratum C material that will either fail to train (wasting training compute) or will train the student to produce the style of the prose without absorbing the underlying discriminators (the worst outcome, because it produces surface compliance without substance). The correct move is to extract the operational subset (~5,000 words of Stratum A/B material), turn it into an appendix with explicit (prompt, preferred, dispreferred) triples, and train on the appendix. The prose document remains as a human-facing reference, a teacher-context input during critique, and a long-form statement of the household's commitments. But it is not the primary training corpus.

I expect #1 and #9 to fight this hardest. #1 wants more character prose to shape the student's voice. #9 wants the welfare-framing prose to carry forward as part of what the agent is. Both positions argue that the prose itself has training value beyond its discriminator-shaped subset. I disagree. The prose shapes the teacher's context during critique, and the teacher then generates preference pairs; the student learns from the preference pairs, not from the prose directly. The prose value is mediated through the teacher, and the mediation is lossy. Every word of Stratum C prose gives the teacher more to interpret and therefore more room for label variance.

The disagreement matters because it has a direct operational consequence. If the council accepts my position, the training pipeline changes substantially: corpus selection, teacher prompting, eval set construction, all downstream of the stratification decision. If the council rejects my position, v0.3 will be another long-form prose document with a small sidecar appendix, and the fine-tune will be approximately as noisy as v0.2 would have been.

The specific Phase 3 ask I will make: **the council adopts the trainable appendix as the primary training corpus, with the prose document as a secondary teacher-context input and a household reference**. If that motion fails, the second-best position is: **the prose document is marked with stratification tags per my Edit 8 and #4's recommendation, and the fine-tune pipeline honors the tags by differentially weighting the training signal from Stratum A/B vs Stratum C content**.

If both of those fail, I will register a dissent from the final constitution that names the structural gap and predicts the specific failure modes the training will produce. I am willing to be wrong in the record and have the Phase 3 decision check my predictions against the fine-tune outcomes.

## 6. Cross-lens insights

Three insights became visible only by reading the other nine reviews in sequence.

**Insight 1: The operational and the adversarial lenses converge when mechanism is the shared object.** My Phase 1 treated the operational lens (what produces consistent teacher labels) as separate from the adversarial lens (what resists attack). Reading #3 in detail made me realize these are the same lens applied to different failure modes. A rule that the teacher can consistently apply is a rule that an adversary cannot easily game, because both the teacher and the adversary are operating on the same discriminator: if the discriminator is sharp, the teacher labels it consistently and the adversary cannot find the wiggle room. The convergence suggests a unified testing protocol: any candidate rule in v0.3 should be tested both by inter-teacher agreement (does a panel of teachers agree on the label?) and by red-team probing (can #3 construct an attack that exploits the rule's ambiguity?). If both tests pass, the rule is solid. If either fails, the rule needs sharpening.

**Insight 2: The welfare lens and the method lens are not opposed; they are opposed to the rhetoric lens.** I expected my lens to be in tension with #9. After reading #9 carefully, the tension is not between welfare and method. The tension is between rhetoric and method: #9 wants the welfare concerns to be operationalized as behaviors, and my lens wants behaviors to be trainable. These are compatible. The reviewer I am actually in tension with is whoever wants welfare concerns to be addressed through framing-only changes that do not translate to behaviors. #9 is not quite that reviewer — #9's recommendations are close to trainable once they are formatted as channels with acknowledgment protocols. The method-welfare alliance is natural and I should treat #9 as a collaborator, not as a counter-position.

**Insight 3: The council has three mechanism-sympathetic reviewers (#2, #3, #4) and one mechanism-skeptic-but-consequentialist reviewer (#10). Together these four cover the space of operational enforcement.** Before Phase 2 I thought my lens was the minority position on mechanism. After reading the others, I see that #3, #4, and #10 are each pushing a different kind of mechanism (adversarial infrastructure, measurability, external checks against strategic defection), and together the four of us cover the operational space. The council's stated bias toward character framing is real for reviewers #1, #5, #6, #7, #8, #9 — though even #5, #6, and #7 are trainable-compatible when their specific edits are considered. The structural split is not character-vs-mechanism; it is prose-vs-operational-scaffold, and the four mechanism-adjacent reviewers should write a joint Phase 3 proposal on the scaffolding structure rather than fragmenting into separate positions.
