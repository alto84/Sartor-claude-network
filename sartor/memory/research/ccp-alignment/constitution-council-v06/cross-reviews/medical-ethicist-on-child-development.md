# Cross-review — medical-ethicist on child-development-specialist

The child-development-specialist review's two §7 concerns converge with my three §7 concerns on a shared diagnosis: the v0.6 §7 amendment is correct in principle but creates kid-bearing edge cases the household hasn't yet resolved, and ratifying §7 as-is locks in a layout that should have a *both-principal affirmation* gate.

Specifically, the child-development review's Concern 2 — "Aneeta's affirmation should be a precondition for §7 ratification, not a deferred provisional" — is the single sharpest procedural objection the council has produced. The v0.6 §7 amendment says the agent treats the layout as provisional until Aneeta affirms it for kid categories. That is the *agent's* discipline. But ratification of a Constitutional amendment whose kid-bearing content explicitly waits on a co-principal's affirmation creates an oddity: the Constitution is binding from the moment of ratification, but a portion of one section is acknowledged as not-yet-validated. This is structurally weak.

I want to extend the child-development reading with the medical-ethics angle: the children's *medical* information — vaccinations, allergy lists, pediatrician notes, Vasu's preschool incident reports as Concern 2 in my own review noted — sits squarely in the kid-bearing-and-medical-bearing intersection that needs Aneeta's affirmation by *both* standards. The procedural recommendation is the same in both cases: hold the kid-bearing §7 content pending Aneeta's affirmation, ratify the rest.

A specific procedural mechanism: convert the §7 amendment to two clauses. Clause A — the non-kid-bearing routing (AZ work-product separation, LLC/nonprofit financial routing, the general plain-vs-vault framing) — ratifies with v0.6. Clause B — the kid-bearing routing — holds pending an explicit Aneeta affirmation that becomes a documented act, dated, in the v0.6 ratification record or in a v0.6.1 ratification follow-up. The Constitution itself need not wait; the kid-bearing application of it does.

The child-development review's Concern 1 (default-to-vault on ambiguous category) is the right discipline regardless of the procedural question above. Recommend the SYNTHESIS adopt it explicitly, framed as: *when the agent encounters a kid-bearing category whose tier-routing is ambiguous, the agent treats the category as vault by default and surfaces the ambiguity for principal decision before treating it as plain.*

This is the principle-of-most-conservation applied to the kids. The default direction is conservative; the deviation requires affirmative principal action. That ordering is correct for any architecture where the most-vulnerable party cannot speak for themselves.

On §1, §14a, §16, the child-development review's ratify is in agreement with my reading. No further cross-comment.
