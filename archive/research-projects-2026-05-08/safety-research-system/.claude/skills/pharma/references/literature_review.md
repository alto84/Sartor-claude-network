## 1. PV Organizational Structure Best Practices

### 1.1 Nord M, Ysander M, Sullivan T, Patel M. Practical considerations for creating a strategic and proactive clinical safety and pharmacovigilance organization for the future. *Journal of Regulatory Science*. 2021;32(3):243-258.
- **PMID:** 33579878
- **DOI:** 10.3233/JRS-200082
- **Key findings:** Describes how AstraZeneca restructured its PV organization. Argues the PV function should be strategic and proactive, not merely reactive/compliance-driven. The person responsible for PV and risk management should hold C-suite-level authority. Organizational design should integrate clinical safety with pharmacovigilance rather than treating them as separate silos.
- **Relevance:** Provides a concrete model for how a mid-to-large pharma restructured PV. Principles apply to smaller organizations: integrated safety/PV function, strategic positioning, and proactive signal work.

### 1.2 Clinical Leader. "You're A Small Biotech -- How Should You Implement A Pharmacovigilance System?" (Industry guidance article)
- **Source:** ClinicalLeader.com / Halozyme executive insights
- **URL:** https://www.clinicalleader.com/doc/you-re-a-small-biotech-how-should-you-implement-a-pharmacovigilance-system-0001
- **Key findings:** Oversight, governance, and decision-making must remain in-house even when operations are outsourced. PV leadership is a C-suite responsibility. Small biotechs should implement PV systems early (pre-Phase I). Technology (e.g., visualization tools like Spotfire/Tableau) can compensate for smaller headcount. A hybrid model (internal governance + outsourced case processing) is the dominant approach for small/mid-size companies.
- **Relevance:** Directly applicable to our organizational design -- validates the hybrid model with retained strategic oversight.

### 1.3 Applied Clinical Trials. "Pharmacovigilance: A Company-Wide Challenge."
- **Source:** Applied Clinical Trials Online
- **URL:** https://www.appliedclinicaltrialsonline.com/view/pharmacovigilance-company-wide-challenge
- **Key findings:** PV is not solely the responsibility of the drug safety department; it requires company-wide engagement. Cross-functional awareness and training are essential. All departments (clinical, regulatory, medical affairs, commercial) must understand their PV obligations.
- **Relevance:** Supports cross-functional PV governance model in organizational design.

## 2. QPPV Role and Responsibilities

### 2.1 European Commission. Directive 2001/83/EC (Article 104) as amended by Directive 2010/84/EU; Regulation (EC) No 726/2004 (Article 23) as amended by Regulation (EU) No 1235/2010.
- **Type:** EU Legislation
- **Key requirements:**
  - Every MAH must appoint a QPPV who resides in the EU/EEA.
  - The QPPV must be permanently and continuously available.
  - Responsibilities include: oversight of the entire PV system, its quality system, SOPs, contractual arrangements, database operations, compliance data on reporting timeliness, audit reports, and personnel training.
  - The QPPV serves as the primary regulatory contact for PV matters.
  - Delegation of tasks is permitted but accountability is non-transferable.
  - Must have qualifications in medicine, pharmaceutical sciences, epidemiology, or biostatistics.
- **Relevance:** Defines the legal framework for the QPPV role. Any organizational structure must ensure the QPPV has adequate authority and resources.

### 2.2 EMA. Guideline on Good Pharmacovigilance Practices (GVP) Module I -- Pharmacovigilance Systems and Their Quality Systems. EMA/541760/2011. Effective 2 July 2012 (Rev 2, 2017).
- **Type:** Regulatory guideline
- **URL:** https://www.ema.europa.eu/en/documents/scientific-guideline/guideline-good-pharmacovigilance-practices-module-i-pharmacovigilance-systems-and-their-quality-systems_en.pdf
- **Key requirements:**
  - The PV system quality system must cover organizational structure, responsibilities, procedures, processes, and resources.
  - Must include compliance management, record management, and resource management.
  - The PSMF (Pharmacovigilance System Master File) must accurately describe the operational system.
  - Audit and inspection readiness is a continuous obligation.
- **Relevance:** Foundational regulatory document for PV system design. All organizational structures must comply with GVP Module I.

### 2.3 UK MHRA. Pharmacovigilance Requirements: Qualified Person for PV and PSMF.
- **Type:** Regulatory guidance (post-Brexit UK framework)
- **URL:** https://www.gov.uk/guidance/guidance-on-qualified-person-responsible-for-pharmacovigilance-qppv-including-pharmacovigilance-system-master-files-psmf
- **Key findings:** UK-specific QPPV requirements post-Brexit. The QPPV must reside in the UK for UK marketing authorizations. Largely mirrors EU requirements but constitutes a separate regulatory obligation.
- **Relevance:** Important for companies with both EU and UK marketing authorizations -- may require separate QPPVs or deputies.

## 3. Signal Management Process Optimization

### 3.1 CIOMS Working Group VIII. Practical Aspects of Signal Detection in Pharmacovigilance. Geneva: CIOMS; 2010.
- **Type:** International guidance (CIOMS report)
- **ISBN:** 978-92-9036-082-7
- **URL:** https://cioms.ch/wp-content/uploads/2018/03/WG8-Signal-Detection.pdf
- **Key findings:** Combination of quantitative methods (disproportionality analysis) with clinical judgment strengthens signal detection. Organizations need clear policies on signal management lifecycle: detection, validation, prioritization, evaluation, and action. Cross-functional signal review teams (drug safety scientists, epidemiologists, statisticians, physicians) are essential. SOPs should integrate automated screening with expert review.
- **Relevance:** Gold-standard guidance for signal management process design. Directly informs SOP development and team composition.

### 3.2 Fusaroli M, Salvo F, Begaud B, et al. The Reporting of a Disproportionality Analysis for Drug Safety Signal Detection Using Individual Case Safety Reports in PharmacoVigilance (READUS-PV): Development and Statement. *Drug Safety*. 2024;47(6):575-584.
- **PMID:** 38713346
- **DOI:** 10.1007/s40264-024-01421-9
- **Key findings:** Provides a 32-item checklist (14 items for main text, 4 items for abstracts) for transparent reporting of disproportionality analyses. Covers data source description, statistical methods, thresholds, and interpretation. Addresses common methodological pitfalls.
- **Relevance:** Essential reference for any organization conducting or commissioning disproportionality analyses. Provides quality standards for signal detection reports.

### 3.3 Jiao Y, et al. Adverse drug reaction signal detection methods in spontaneous reporting system: A systematic review. *Pharmacoepidemiology and Drug Safety*. 2024;33(3):e5768.
- **PMID:** 38419132
- **DOI:** 10.1002/pds.5768
- **Key findings:** Comprehensive review of signal detection algorithms (SDAs) including Proportional Reporting Ratio (PRR), Reporting Odds Ratio (ROR), Bayesian Confidence Propagation Neural Network (BCPNN), and Multi-item Gamma Poisson Shrinker (MGPS). Logistic regression-based SDAs provide superior specificity at given sensitivity levels compared to traditional disproportionality analysis.
- **Relevance:** Informs selection of signal detection methodology. Organizations should consider multi-method approaches.

### 3.4 Pacurariu AC, Straus SM, Trifiro G, et al. Useful interplay between spontaneous ADR reports and electronic healthcare records in signal detection. *Drug Safety*. 2015;38:1201-1210.
- **Key findings:** Demonstrated value of combining spontaneous reporting data with electronic health records for signal substantiation. Particularly useful for events with high background incidence.
- **Relevance:** Supports multi-source signal detection strategy.

## 4. Outsourcing in PV -- CRO Oversight Models

### 4.1 Strassmann V, et al. Current practice and perspectives in CRO oversight based on a survey performed among members of the German Association of Research-Based Pharmaceutical Companies (vfa). *German Medical Science*. 2016;14:Doc10.
- **PMC:** PMC5278541
- **URL:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5278541/
- **Key findings:**
  - Two dominant outsourcing models: preferred provider and non-preferred provider.
  - Clinical research department is the primary driver of CRO selection in both models.
  - In non-preferred provider models, Quality Management, Legal, and Pharmacovigilance departments play a larger role in CRO selection.
  - Risk-based quality management plans should document how continuous CRO oversight is organized, including risk assessment, risk control, and risk review cycles.
  - Regulatory authorities increasingly focus on sponsor oversight of service providers during inspections.
- **Relevance:** Provides evidence-based framework for CRO oversight. The risk-based approach aligns with current regulatory expectations.

### 4.2 DIA Global Forum. "3 Ways to Rethink and Improve Pharmacovigilance Outsourcing." November 2023.
- **Type:** Industry guidance
- **URL:** https://globalforum.diaglobal.org/issue/november-2023/3-ways-to-rethink-and-improve-pharmacovigilance-outsourcing/
- **Key findings:** Industry surveys show ~90% of drug developers use FSP (Functional Service Provider) or hybrid outsourcing models. Outsourcing rates by function: Case Processing (40%), AE Monitoring/Case Intake (26%), Signal and Risk Management (21%). The trend is toward retaining strategic functions (signal management, benefit-risk) in-house while outsourcing operational tasks (case processing, literature monitoring).
- **Relevance:** Validates the hybrid model. Signal management and benefit-risk assessment should remain in-house; case processing and routine operations are candidates for outsourcing.

### 4.3 Pharmaceutical Executive. "How Bringing Pharmacovigilance In-House Drives Better Oversight and Faster Data Analysis."
- **Source:** PharmExec.com
- **URL:** https://www.pharmexec.com/view/how-bringing-pharmacovigilance-in-house-drives-better-oversight-and-faster-data-analysis
- **Key findings:** Some companies are bringing PV back in-house after initially outsourcing, citing faster data access, better oversight, and improved signal detection speed. In-house teams have more direct access to clinical context, which improves case assessment quality.
- **Relevance:** Counterpoint to full outsourcing -- suggests that core safety intelligence functions benefit from in-house placement.

## 5. PV Quality Systems and Inspection Readiness

### 5.1 EMA. Pharmacovigilance Inspections Annual Reports (2018-2023).
- **Type:** Regulatory inspection data
- **Source:** ECA Academy / EMA inspection metrics
- **Key findings -- most common deficiency categories (ranked by frequency):**
  1. **Quality Management System (QMS)** -- largest category of findings overall
  2. **Risk Management** -- second most common area
  3. **Ongoing Safety Evaluation** -- signal management and PSUR deficiencies
  4. **ADR Case Processing** -- data entry, coding, assessment, follow-up, and reporting
- **Specific recurring deficiencies:**
  - PSMF non-compliance (incomplete, outdated, or not reflecting actual operations)
  - Inadequate CAPA management and delayed audit report issuance
  - Signal management process gaps
  - Delayed safety report submissions
  - Insufficient documentation of decision rationale
- **Relevance:** Directly informs inspection readiness priorities. QMS and PSMF accuracy should be continuous focus areas.

### 5.2 MHRA. Good Pharmacovigilance Practice (GPvP) Inspection Metrics Reports (2018-2020).
- **Type:** UK regulatory inspection data
- **URLs:**
  - https://assets.publishing.service.gov.uk/media/5e96c42586650c2dcac16906/MHRA_GPvP_Inspection_metrics_2018-19.pdf
  - https://assets.publishing.service.gov.uk/media/603cbbee8fa8f50495bda34d/MHRA_GPvP_Inspection_metrics_2019-20.pdf
- **Key findings:**
  - Audit and deviation management (including CAPA) is the most common subtopic for quality system findings.
  - Significant delays in producing PV audit reports lead to downstream delays in addressing non-compliance.
  - Case processing findings concentrate on data entry, coding accuracy, and causality assessment quality.
- **Relevance:** UK-specific inspection data complements EMA findings. Consistent themes: CAPA management, PSMF accuracy, and audit timeliness.

### 5.3 TMC Pharma. "10 Common Pharmacovigilance Inspection Findings and Their Implications."
- **Type:** Industry analysis
- **URL:** https://tmcpharma.com/10-common-pharmacovigilance-inspection-findings-and-their-implications/
- **Key findings:** Top 10 common inspection findings include: (1) PSMF not reflecting reality, (2) poor CAPA management, (3) incomplete documentation, (4) delayed reporting, (5) deficient signal management, (6) inadequate quality systems, (7) training gaps, (8) vendor/contractor oversight gaps, (9) safety database validation issues, (10) inadequate medical review.
- **Relevance:** Practical checklist for inspection readiness preparation.

## 6. Benefit-Risk Assessment Frameworks

### 6.1 Nixon R, Dierig C, Mt-Isa S, et al. A case study using the PrOACT-URL and BRAT frameworks for structured benefit risk assessment. *Biometrical Journal*. 2016;58(1):8-27.
- **PMID:** 25619173
- **DOI:** 10.1002/bimj.201300248
- **Key findings:** Compared PrOACT-URL and BRAT frameworks using natalizumab for relapsing-remitting multiple sclerosis as a case study. Applied MCDA within PrOACT-URL and weighted net clinical benefit (wNCB) within BRAT. Both frameworks provide structured, transparent approaches to benefit-risk evaluation. Neither framework inherently includes quantitative integration -- that must be added via MCDA or other methods. Visual presentation of results is critical for stakeholder communication.
- **Relevance:** Foundational paper for implementing structured benefit-risk assessment. The PrOACT-URL framework is recommended as the primary approach for our organization.

### 6.2 Kurzinger ML, Douarin L, Uzun I, et al. Structured benefit-risk evaluation for medicinal products: review of quantitative benefit-risk assessment findings in the literature. *Therapeutic Advances in Drug Safety*. 2020;11:2042098620976951.
- **PMID:** 33343857
- **PMC:** PMC7727082
- **DOI:** 10.1177/2042098620976951
- **Key findings:** MCDA was the most frequently cited quantitative framework (62.7% of studies). Other common frameworks: Markov decision processes (21.7%) and decision trees (19.3%). Descriptive frameworks (PrOACT-URL, BRAT, FDA framework) share common features: logical soundness, practicality, consistency, and transparency.
- **Relevance:** Confirms MCDA as the leading quantitative method. Organizations should consider MCDA capability for complex benefit-risk decisions.

### 6.3 Mt-Isa S, Hallgreen CE, Wang N, et al. Balancing benefit and risk of medicines: a systematic review and classification of available methodologies. *Pharmacoepidemiology and Drug Safety*. 2014;23(7):667-678.
- **PMID:** 25981683 (related review)
- **Key findings:** Systematic review classifying benefit-risk assessment methodologies. Identified and categorized both qualitative/descriptive and quantitative approaches. Established taxonomy of methods that has become widely referenced in the field.
- **Relevance:** Comprehensive reference for selecting appropriate benefit-risk methodology.

### 6.4 Pang T. A Structured Benefit-Risk Assessment Operating Model for Investigational Medicinal Products in the Pharmaceutical Industry. *Therapeutic Innovation & Regulatory Science*. 2023;57(4):807-817.
- **PMC:** PMC10276786
- **Key findings:** Proposes an operating model for implementing structured benefit-risk assessment across the product lifecycle. Emphasizes integration of benefit-risk thinking from early development through post-marketing. Practical guidance on team composition, governance, and documentation.
- **Relevance:** Provides actionable operating model for implementing benefit-risk assessment in a pharma organization.

## 7. EGFR Inhibitor Safety Profiles

### 7.1 Shen Y, et al. Safety Profile of Epidermal Growth Factor Receptor Tyrosine Kinase Inhibitors: A Disproportionality Analysis of FDA Adverse Event Reporting System. *Scientific Reports*. 2020;10:9753.
- **DOI:** 10.1038/s41598-020-61571-5
- **URL:** https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-020-61571-5
- **Key findings:**
  - Analyzed 27,123 EGFR-TKI-associated AE reports from FAERS (2004-2018) for gefitinib, erlotinib, afatinib, and osimertinib.
  - Significant disproportionality signals in: skin, nail, GI tract, liver, eyes, and lungs.
  - Less common but notable: cardiac, renal, neurological, and hematopoietic system events.
  - Novel signals identified: intestinal obstruction and electrolyte disturbances (hypokalemia, hyponatremia) for gefitinib and erlotinib.
- **Relevance:** Validates our AE category selections for Prosinertimib. Skin, GI, hepatic, and pulmonary toxicities are well-established class effects.

### 7.2 Adverse Event Profile of Epidermal Growth Factor Receptor Tyrosine Kinase Inhibitors for Non-small Cell Lung Cancer: An Updated Meta-analysis. *Targeted Oncology*. 2024.
- **DOI:** 10.1007/s11523-024-01073-w
- **URL:** https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11523-024-01073-w
- **Key findings:**
  - Comprehensive meta-analysis of EGFR-TKI safety across multiple agents.
  - Diarrhea incidence varies by agent: afatinib (65-96%), erlotinib (54-62%), osimertinib (58%), gefitinib (29%).
  - Grade >=3 AE likelihood ranking: afatinib > dacomitinib > erlotinib > gefitinib > osimertinib.
  - Rash/dermatitis acneiform is a class effect but severity varies by agent.
- **Relevance:** Supports diarrhea, rash, and dermatologic toxicity as primary AE categories for Prosinertimib modeling.

### 7.3 Wang Y, et al. Adverse event profiles of EGFR-TKI: network meta-analysis and disproportionality analysis of the FAERS database. *Frontiers in Pharmacology*. 2025;16:1519849.
- **PMC:** PMC11933087
- **URL:** https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/pharmacology/articles/10.3389/fphar.2025.1519849/full
- **Key findings:**
  - 48% of EGFR-TKI patients experienced adverse events; 32.7% were severe.
  - Gefitinib had the strongest signal for interstitial lung disease (ILD).
  - Osimertinib had highest risks for leukopenia (8%), thrombocytopenia (9%), and cardiac disorders.
  - Most AEs occurred within 30 days; cardiac disorders had delayed onset (median 41 days).
  - Afatinib showed highest overall toxicity; icotinib was safest.
- **Relevance:** Validates ILD, cardiac events, and hematologic toxicity as important monitoring targets. Temporal onset data informs temporal profile modeling.

### 7.4 Cardiovascular adverse events associated with EGFR-TKIs in EGFR-mutated NSCLC: systematic review and network meta-analysis. *BMJ*. 2025.
- **PMID:** 40897431
- **PMC:** PMC12402974
- **Key findings:** Third-generation EGFR-TKIs (osimertinib) present highest risk of cardiac adverse events, particularly arrhythmias. Risk is amplified when combined with antiangiogenic agents.
- **Relevance:** Important for Prosinertimib cardiac safety modeling, especially if the product profile includes third-generation characteristics.

### 7.5 Lacouture ME. Mechanisms of cutaneous toxicities to EGFR inhibitors. *Nature Reviews Cancer*. 2006;6:803-812. (Foundational reference)
- **Key findings:** EGFR is expressed in skin (basal/suprabasal keratinocytes, outer root sheath of hair follicles, sebaceous glands). EGFR inhibition causes: papulopustular rash (45-100% of patients), xerosis, paronychia, hair changes. Rash severity correlates with treatment efficacy.
- **Relevance:** Foundational mechanistic reference for understanding EGFR-TKI dermatologic toxicity in our safety profiles.

## Summary: Key Organizational Design Principles from the Literature

1. **Hybrid model is optimal for small-to-mid pharma:** Retain strategic functions (signal management, benefit-risk, QPPV oversight, regulatory intelligence) in-house; outsource operational functions (case processing, literature monitoring, PSMF maintenance) to CROs/vendors.

2. **PV leadership requires C-suite authority:** The Head of PV / QPPV must have direct access to senior management and authority to halt or modify product strategy based on safety signals.

3. **Cross-functional governance is essential:** Signal review committees should include drug safety scientists, epidemiologists, statisticians, and clinical physicians. PV awareness training should extend to all departments.

4. **Quality system is the #1 inspection finding area:** Organizations must prioritize PSMF accuracy, CAPA management, audit timeliness, and documentation of decision rationale.

5. **Structured benefit-risk assessment should use PrOACT-URL or BRAT:** MCDA is the leading quantitative approach. Visual presentation of results is critical for stakeholder communication.

6. **EGFR inhibitor class effects validate our AE selections:** Dermatologic (rash, paronychia), GI (diarrhea), hepatic (transaminase elevation), pulmonary (ILD), and cardiac toxicities are well-documented class effects with distinct temporal profiles.

7. **Risk-based CRO oversight is regulatory expectation:** Quality management plans must document oversight mechanisms including risk assessment, control, and periodic review cycles.
