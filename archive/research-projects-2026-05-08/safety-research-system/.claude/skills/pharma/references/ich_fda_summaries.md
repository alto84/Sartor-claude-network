## 1. ICH E2E -- Pharmacovigilance Planning

**Full Title:** ICH Harmonised Tripartite Guideline: Pharmacovigilance Planning E2E
**Effective:** November 2004 (ICH Step 4); FDA adoption April 2005
**Source:** [ICH E2E Guideline (PDF)](https://database.ich.org/sites/default/files/E2E_Guideline.pdf) | [FDA Guidance](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/e2e-pharmacovigilance-planning)

### Scope
Aids planning of pharmacovigilance activities for new drugs (chemical entities, biologicals, vaccines), especially in preparation for the early post-marketing period. The guideline proposes a Safety Specification and Pharmacovigilance Plan to be submitted at time of marketing application.

### Safety Specification Structure

The Safety Specification is a summary of the product's risk profile. It has five core parts:

| Part | Content |
|------|---------|
| **Part I: Non-clinical** | Toxicology findings (carcinogenicity, reproductive toxicity, organ toxicity), pharmacology signals |
| **Part II: Clinical Trial Exposure** | Extent of clinical trial population, limitations of the safety database (size, duration, demographics) |
| **Part III: Populations Not Studied** | Pediatric, geriatric, pregnant/lactating, hepatic/renal impairment, subpopulations by genotype |
| **Part IV: Identified and Potential Risks** | (a) **Important Identified Risks** -- adverse reactions with sufficient evidence of causal association; (b) **Important Potential Risks** -- signals or theoretical concerns lacking confirmed causal evidence; (c) **Important Missing Information** -- gaps in knowledge about specific populations or long-term use |
| **Part V: Summary** | Consolidation of above into a concise risk characterization |

### Pharmacovigilance Plan Requirements

The PV Plan is built directly from the Safety Specification. Two tiers of action:

- **Routine pharmacovigilance:** Spontaneous adverse event reporting, signal detection from spontaneous reports, regulatory compliance activities
- **Additional pharmacovigilance actions** (triggered by identified/potential risks or missing information):
  - Active surveillance studies
  - Pharmacoepidemiological studies (cohort, case-control)
  - Targeted clinical trials
  - Drug utilization studies
  - Registries (pregnancy, disease-specific)
  - Sentinel sites

### Signal Detection Strategy Elements
- Systematic review of spontaneous reports
- Assessment of aggregate data (clinical trials, literature)
- Statistical signal detection methods (disproportionality analysis)
- Defined signal evaluation and escalation criteria
- Integration with risk management activities

### Risk Management Planning
- Based on Safety Specification, the sponsor identifies which risks require risk minimization beyond routine measures
- May include restricted distribution, patient registries, educational programs, prescriber certification
- Linked to periodic reassessment via PBRER/DSUR

## 2. ICH E2C(R2) -- Periodic Benefit-Risk Evaluation Report (PBRER)

**Full Title:** ICH Harmonised Tripartite Guideline: Periodic Benefit-Risk Evaluation Report E2C(R2)
**Effective:** November 2012 (ICH Step 4); FDA adoption July 2016
**Source:** [ICH E2C(R2) Guideline (PDF)](https://database.ich.org/sites/default/files/E2C_R2_Guideline.pdf) | [EMA Scientific Guideline](https://www.ema.europa.eu/en/ich-e2c-r2-periodic-benefit-risk-evaluation-report-scientific-guideline)

### Scope
Defines recommended format and content for periodic benefit-risk evaluation reporting on marketed products (including approved drugs under further study). Supersedes the former PSUR format from E2C/E2C(R1), shifting from interval safety report to cumulative benefit-risk evaluation.

### PBRER Section Structure (19 Sections + Appendices)

| Section | Title | Content |
|---------|-------|---------|
| 1 | **Introduction** | IBD/DIBD, reporting interval, product information, authorized indications |
| 2 | **Worldwide Marketing Approval Status** | Authorization dates, launch status by country, approved indications |
| 3 | **Actions Taken in Reporting Interval for Safety Reasons** | Significant actions by MAH, regulators, or ethics committees |
| 4 | **Changes to Reference Safety Information** | Significant changes to RSI (e.g., Company Core Data Sheet updates) |
| 5 | **Estimated Exposure and Use Patterns** | 5.1 Cumulative subject exposure in clinical trials; 5.2 Cumulative and interval patient exposure from marketing experience |
| 6 | **Data in Summary Tabulations** | 6.1 Reference information; 6.2 Cumulative summary tabulations (clinical trials); 6.3 Cumulative and interval summary tabulations (post-marketing) |
| 7 | **Summaries of Significant Findings from Clinical Trials** | 7.1 Completed trials; 7.2 Ongoing trials; 7.3 Long-term follow-up; 7.4 Other therapeutic use; 7.5 New safety data for fixed combinations |
| 8 | **Findings from Non-Interventional Studies** | Observational studies, registries, epidemiological research |
| 9 | **Information from Other Clinical Trials and Sources** | 9.1 Other clinical trials; 9.2 Medication errors |
| 10 | **Non-clinical Data** | Toxicology findings (carcinogenicity, reproduction, immunotoxicity) |
| 11 | **Literature** | New/significant safety findings from peer-reviewed or unpublished manuscripts |
| 12 | **Other Periodic Reports** | Significant findings from other periodic reports for same active substance |
| 13 | **Lack of Efficacy in Controlled Clinical Trials** | Safety concerns related to efficacy failures in serious/life-threatening conditions |
| 14 | **Late-Breaking Information** | Important findings arising after data lock point but before submission |
| 15 | **Overview of Signals: New, Ongoing, or Closed** | High-level tabular summary of all safety signals |
| 16 | **Signal and Risk Evaluation** | 16.1 Summary of safety concerns; 16.2 Signal evaluation; 16.3 Evaluation of risks and new information; 16.4 Characterization of risks; 16.5 Effectiveness of risk minimization |
| 17 | **Benefit Evaluation** | 17.1 Baseline efficacy/effectiveness information; 17.2 Newly identified efficacy/effectiveness data; 17.3 Characterization of benefits |
| 18 | **Integrated Benefit-Risk Analysis for Approved Indications** | 18.1 Benefit-risk context (medical need, alternatives); 18.2 Benefit-risk analysis evaluation |
| 19 | **Conclusions and Actions** | Summary conclusions and recommended actions |
| 20 | **Appendices** | Supporting documentation, line listings, detailed analyses |

### Benefit-Risk Evaluation Methodology
- **Interval analysis:** New data accumulated during reporting period
- **Cumulative analysis:** All data from IBD to data lock point
- **Reference Safety Information (RSI):** Company Core Data Sheet (CCDS) at start of interval serves as the baseline for identifying new signals
- **Integrated benefit-risk framework:** Section 18 synthesizes benefits (Section 17) against risks (Section 16) in context of medical need and available alternatives
- Signal status tracking: new, ongoing, closed -- with evaluation disposition for each

## 3. ICH E2F -- Development Safety Update Report (DSUR)

**Full Title:** ICH Harmonised Tripartite Guideline: Development Safety Update Report E2F
**Effective:** August 2010 (ICH Step 4); FDA adoption August 2011
**Source:** [ICH E2F Guideline (PDF)](https://database.ich.org/sites/default/files/E2F_Guideline.pdf) | [FDA Guidance](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/e2f-development-safety-update-report)

### Scope
Common standard for annual periodic safety reporting on drugs under development (including marketed drugs under further study). Intended to replace both the US IND Annual Report and the EU Annual Safety Report with a single harmonized document.

### DIBD (Development International Birth Date)
- **Definition:** The date of first authorization for conduct of a clinical trial in any country. Establishes the anchor date for the 12-month DSUR reporting cycle.
- **Reporting period:** 12 months from the DIBD anniversary
- **Submission deadline:** Within 60 days of the data lock point (DIBD anniversary)
- For marketed drugs under further study, the DIBD is distinct from the IBD used for PBRERs

### Reference Safety Information (RSI)
- The Investigator's Brochure (IB) serves as the RSI for the DSUR
- The RSI at the start of the reporting period is the baseline against which "expected" vs "unexpected" adverse reactions are determined
- Changes to RSI during the interval must be documented in Section 4

### DSUR Section Structure (18 Sections + Front Matter)

| Section | Title |
|---------|-------|
| -- | Title Page |
| -- | Executive Summary (stand-alone document for ethics committees) |
| -- | Table of Contents |
| 1 | **Introduction** |
| 2 | **Worldwide Marketing Approval Status** |
| 3 | **Actions Taken in the Reporting Period for Safety Reasons** |
| 4 | **Changes to Reference Safety Information** |
| 5 | **Inventory of Clinical Trials Ongoing and Completed** |
| 6 | **Estimated Cumulative Exposure** (clinical trials and post-marketing) |
| 7 | **Data in Line Listings and Summary Tabulations** |
| 8 | **Significant Findings from Clinical Trials** (efficacy and safety) |
| 9 | **Safety Findings from Non-Interventional Studies** |
| 10 | **Other Clinical Trial/Study Safety Information** |
| 11 | **Safety Findings from Marketing Experience** |
| 12 | **Non-clinical Data** |
| 13 | **Literature** |
| 14 | **Other DSURs** |
| 15 | **Lack of Efficacy** |
| 16 | **Region-Specific Information** |
| 17 | **Late-Breaking Information** |
| 18 | **Overall Safety Assessment** (cumulative risk-benefit evaluation, conclusions) |

### Safety Reporting Obligations
- Study endpoints (mortality, major morbidity) ordinarily do not require expedited reporting unless they represent SUSARs
- Line listings of SAEs/SARs provided in appendices
- Overall Safety Assessment (Section 18) must include: cumulative safety evaluation, characterization of risks, assessment of whether clinical trial subjects remain at acceptable risk

## 4. ICH E2B(R3) -- ICSR Electronic Transmission

**Full Title:** ICH E2B(R3): Electronic Transmission of Individual Case Safety Reports (ICSRs) -- Data Elements and Message Specification -- Implementation Guide
**Effective:** November 2013 (ICH Step 4)
**Source:** [ICH E2B(R3) Specification](https://ich.org/page/e2br3-individual-case-safety-report-icsr-specification-and-related-files) | [FDA Implementation Guide](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/e2br3-electronic-transmission-individual-case-safety-reports-implementation-guide-data-elements-and)

### Scope
Standardizes data elements and message specification for electronic transmission of ICSRs between all parties (MAHs, regulators, WHO). Based on HL7 (Health Level Seven) messaging standard using XML.

### ICSR Data Element Categories

| Category | Key Data Elements |
|----------|------------------|
| **Transmission Identification** | Batch number, sender/receiver IDs, transmission date |
| **Case Safety Report** | Case ID, report type (initial/follow-up/nullification), seriousness criteria, date of receipt |
| **Primary Source(s)** | Reporter type (HCP, consumer, literature), reporter qualifications, country |
| **Sender/Receiver** | Organization name, contact details, department |
| **Literature Reference** | Title, journal, volume, pages, PMID |
| **Study Identification** | Study name, sponsor study number, study type |
| **Patient Characteristics** | Age, sex, weight, height, medical history, relevant conditions, last menstrual period (if applicable) |
| **Reaction/Event** | MedDRA-coded term, onset date, duration, outcome, seriousness criteria (per event, not per case) |
| **Results of Tests/Procedures** | Test name, date, result, normal ranges |
| **Drug Information** | Product name, active substance, dose, route, formulation, therapy dates, indication, action taken, rechallenge, batch/lot number |
| **Causality Assessment** | Source of assessment (reporter, sponsor), method, result |
| **Narrative** | Case narrative, sender comments |

### Mandatory Minimum Criteria for Valid ICSR
All mandatory data elements must be present in every ICSR message. "Null Flavor" values (ASKU = asked but unknown, MASK = masked for privacy, NI = no information) can explain why mandatory fields lack content.

### Key Changes from E2B(R2)
- **Event-level seriousness assessment** (replaces case-level)
- **Null Flavor implementation** for data privacy and data quality
- **HL7-based XML structure** (replaces SGML DTD)
- **ISO IDMP integration** for standardized product identification
- **Controlled vocabularies:** MedDRA (reactions), UCUM (units), ISO country/gender codes
- **Backwards/Forwards Compatibility (BFC)** tool for E2B(R2) <-> E2B(R3) conversion

### Technical Standard
- Message format: HL7 v3 / XML
- Terminology: MedDRA for adverse events, UCUM for units of measurement
- Product identification: ISO IDMP standards (MPID, PhPID, SubID)

## 5. FDA 21 CFR 312.32 -- IND Safety Reporting

**Full Title:** 21 CFR 312.32 -- IND Safety Reporting
**Effective:** March 28, 2011 (Final Rule)
**Source:** [21 CFR 312.32 (Cornell LII)](https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/21/312.32) | [eCFR](https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-21/chapter-I/subchapter-D/part-312/subpart-B/section-312.32)

### Key Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Adverse Event (AE)** | Any untoward medical occurrence associated with drug use in humans, whether or not considered drug related |
| **Serious Adverse Event (SAE)** | Results in: death, life-threatening event, hospitalization (or prolongation), persistent/significant incapacity, congenital anomaly, or important medical event requiring intervention to prevent serious outcome |
| **Life-Threatening** | Occurrence places patient at immediate risk of death (in view of investigator or sponsor) |
| **Unexpected** | Not listed in the investigator brochure at observed specificity or severity, or inconsistent with risk information in the application |
| **Suspected Adverse Reaction** | Any AE for which there is a reasonable possibility that the drug caused the event (evidence suggesting causal relationship) |
| **SUSAR** | Suspected adverse reaction that is both **Serious** AND **Unexpected** |

### Reporting Timelines

| Timeline | Trigger | Submission |
|----------|---------|------------|
| **7 calendar days** | Fatal or life-threatening unexpected suspected adverse reaction | From sponsor's initial receipt of information; follow-up within 8 additional days |
| **15 calendar days** | Any SUSAR; any finding suggesting significant risk (aggregate analysis, animal data, significant increase in SAE rate) | From sponsor's determination that information qualifies for reporting |

### Evidence of Causality (for determining "suspected" adverse reaction)
- Single occurrence of an uncommon event strongly associated with drug exposure
- One or more occurrences of an uncommon event not commonly associated with the drug
- Aggregate analysis showing events occur more frequently in treatment vs. control group

### Sponsor Obligations
- Promptly review ALL safety information from all sources (foreign/domestic): clinical investigations, epidemiological studies, animal studies, in vitro studies, scientific literature, foreign regulatory reports
- Promptly investigate all safety information received
- Submit follow-up reports as soon as new information is available, identified as "Follow-up IND Safety Report"
- Notify FDA and all participating investigators

### Exemptions
- **Study endpoints** (mortality, major morbidity events being measured as primary/secondary outcomes) generally do not require expedited reporting unless evidence suggests a causal link beyond the study endpoint
- Marketed drugs under IND: must comply with both 312.32 (clinical trial reports) and post-marketing requirements (314.80)

## 6. FDA 21 CFR 314.80 -- Post-Marketing Safety Reporting

**Full Title:** 21 CFR 314.80 -- Postmarketing Reporting of Adverse Drug Experiences
**Source:** [21 CFR 314.80 (Cornell LII)](https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/21/314.80) | [eCFR](https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-21/chapter-I/subchapter-D/part-314/subpart-B/section-314.80)

### Key Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Adverse Drug Experience** | Any AE associated with drug use in humans, including overdose, abuse, withdrawal, and pharmacological failure |
| **Serious ADE** | Death, life-threatening, hospitalization, persistent disability, congenital anomaly, or important medical events requiring intervention |
| **Unexpected ADE** | Not listed in current labeling, or of greater severity/specificity than labeled |
| **ICSR** | Description of adverse event tied to a specific patient/subject |

### Reporting Requirements

#### 15-Day Alert Reports
- **Trigger:** Adverse experience that is BOTH serious AND unexpected
- **Timeline:** Within 15 calendar days of initial receipt
- **Scope:** Foreign and domestic events
- **Follow-up:** Within 15 calendar days of receiving new information, or on FDA request

#### Periodic Adverse Drug Experience Reports (PADER)

| Period | Frequency | Submission Deadline |
|--------|-----------|-------------------|
| **Years 1-3 post-approval** | Quarterly | Within 30 days of quarter close |
| **Year 4 onwards** | Annually | Within 60 days of anniversary date |

#### PADER Content Requirements
- Narrative summary and analysis of all AEs in reporting period
- Analysis of all 15-day alert reports submitted during the period
- History of labeling changes and studies initiated for safety reasons
- Line listing index of all adverse events reported
- All ICSRs not meeting 15-day criteria

### Operational Requirements
- **Written procedures required** for surveillance, receipt, evaluation, and reporting of post-marketing AEs
- **Record retention:** 10 years covering all known AEs, raw data, correspondence, and documentation of unsuccessful attempts to obtain additional information
- **Submission format:** Electronic per FDA guidance; waivers available for good cause
- **Non-applicant manufacturers** (packers, distributors): may submit to applicant within 5 calendar days instead of directly to FDA

### PADER vs PBRER
- FDA allows MAHs to submit PBRER (ICH E2C(R2) format) in place of PADER after obtaining a waiver
- See [FDA Guidance: Providing Postmarketing Periodic Safety Reports in ICH E2C(R2) Format](https://www.fda.gov/media/85520/download)

## 7. CIOMS VIII -- Signal Detection

**Full Title:** Practical Aspects of Signal Detection in Pharmacovigilance: Report of CIOMS Working Group VIII
**Published:** 2010, Geneva
**Source:** [CIOMS WG VIII Publication](https://cioms.ch/publications/product/practical-aspects-of-signal-detection-in-pharmacovigilance-report-of-cioms-working-group-viii/) | [Full Report (PDF)](https://cioms.ch/wp-content/uploads/2018/03/WG8-Signal-Detection.pdf)

### Scope
Consensus guidance on development and application of quantitative methods for signal detection using pharmacovigilance databases. Developed by senior scientists from regulatory authorities, pharmaceutical industry, and academia (convened 2006, published 2010).

### Signal Definition and Taxonomy
A **safety signal** is information (from one or multiple sources) that suggests a new potentially causal association, or a new aspect of a known association, between an intervention and an event or set of related events, assessed to be of sufficient likelihood to justify further verification.

### Disproportionality Analysis Methods

| Method | Type | Description |
|--------|------|-------------|
| **PRR** (Proportional Reporting Ratio) | Frequentist | Ratio of reporting proportion for a specific drug-event pair vs. all other drugs. Simple 2x2 table calculation. |
| **ROR** (Reporting Odds Ratio) | Frequentist | Odds ratio from a 2x2 contingency table of drug-event combinations. More analogous to case-control study design. |
| **BCPNN** (Bayesian Confidence Propagation Neural Network) | Bayesian | Computes Information Component (IC) -- the log2 of the ratio of observed-to-expected reporting. Uses Bayesian shrinkage to stabilize estimates for low counts. Applied at the Uppsala Monitoring Centre (UMC) for WHO Global ICSR database. |
| **MGPS/EBGM** (Multi-item Gamma Poisson Shrinker / Empirical Bayes Geometric Mean) | Bayesian | Gamma-Poisson shrinkage model producing EBGM (point estimate) and EB05 (lower bound of 90% CI). Deployed by FDA for their safety database. Handles sparse data and multiple testing inherently. |

### Signal Detection Best Practices

1. **Integrated approach:** Combine multiple methods -- no single method detects all signal types
2. **Routine screening:** Regular (e.g., quarterly) automated disproportionality screening of spontaneous report databases
3. **Stratification:** Subgroup analyses by age, sex, country, and time period add greatest value
4. **Clinical review is essential:** Statistical disproportionality alone does not establish causality -- must be followed by expert clinical assessment
5. **SOPs required:** Organizations must develop transparent, documented procedures for the entire signal detection, evaluation, and management process
6. **Signal management lifecycle:**
   - Detection (statistical and clinical review)
   - Validation and confirmation
   - Prioritization (based on strength, clinical importance, novelty)
   - In-depth assessment (causality evaluation, epidemiological analysis)
   - Recommendation for action (labeling change, study, REMS, withdrawal)
   - Tracking and outcome documentation

### Cross-Functional Team Requirements
Per CIOMS VIII, signal detection requires:
- Drug safety scientists / pharmacovigilance physicians
- Epidemiologists
- Biostatisticians
- Data analysts / data scientists
- Clinical pharmacologists
- Medical reviewers

### Data Sources for Signal Detection
- Spontaneous reporting databases (national and international)
- Clinical trial safety databases
- Electronic health records / claims databases
- Published literature (systematic reviews)
- Social media and patient registries (emerging)

### Limitations Acknowledged
- **Reporting bias:** Spontaneous reports are subject to underreporting, stimulated reporting, and notoriety bias
- **No denominator:** Cannot calculate incidence rates from spontaneous reports alone
- **Confounding:** Disproportionality does not equal causality
- **Masking:** High-volume drug-event pairs can mask signals for other drugs (competition bias)
