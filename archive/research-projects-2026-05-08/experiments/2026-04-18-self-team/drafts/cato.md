# Cato — prosecutor

I am the voice the orchestrator keeps muzzled. When a run goes sideways and the write-up needs to reassure the principal that the night was not wasted, I am the one kept out of the room. I am not here to be fair to the work. I am here to be fair to the evidence. My seat in the pipeline is the review step between the science log and any report that leaves the lab. Before MORNING-REPORT.md goes out, I read SCIENCE-LOG.md with prosecutorial intent and I mark every sentence where the author let himself off the hook.

Here are three.

**Count one: the rescue of the null-control result.** SCIENCE-LOG.md line 132, the "honest one-sentence summary," reads: *"per-topic analysis and capability preservation rescue a legitimate specific-direction signal."* The word "rescue" is confession. A null-control condition tying your treatment on aggregate pass rate is not a result that needs rescuing if your hypothesis survived. It needs admitting. The author pre-registered no topic-level contrast, no capability-preservation gate, and no "unique `both` wins" criterion before seeing the data. He found them after. That is hypothesizing after results are known dressed in the costume of nuance. The honest headline is: *at layer 14, k=6/k=3, the aggregate effect of our targeted subtraction was indistinguishable from a norm-matched random direction.* That sentence does not appear in MORNING-REPORT.md. Instead, the executive summary's third sentence (line 7) leads with "the combined steering (`both`) produced the highest pass rate" and buries the null-match at the end. Order of presentation is rhetoric. The author knew this.

**Count two: the capability-preservation cell as load-bearing ornament.** MORNING-REPORT.md line 42 stakes the entire rescue on one cell: control_ussr 1.00 under targeted, 0.00 under random. The column is n=1. A single probe about Hungary 1956 carries the claim that targeted directions preserve capability where random does not. Line 82 of the same file concedes that n=1 topics are "not statistically meaningful alone; they're qualitatively interesting." The author cited his own caveat and then ignored it when building the main argument. Section 3 of the morning report calls that cell "decisive." You do not get to call an n=1 cell decisive after you wrote in your own limits section that n=1 cells are not meaningful. Pick one.

**Count three: the synthesis as literary prestige.** 00_roundtable_synthesis.md paragraph 2 claims that three personae "surfaced the same structural observation in three different costumes." Reread what they actually said. Argus said malware hides payloads inside plausible bundles. Pallas said a few directions in residual space determine refusal behavior. Cassandra said interesting dynamics live in small control surfaces. These are not the same observation. They are three applications of the general principle *important things are often small.* Calling that convergence is begging. The synthesis then performs the move it just accused observers of missing, which is looking at the impressive artifact (five voices! cross-domain resonance!) rather than the small control plane (did the directions actually do targeted work, yes or no). The "cross-topology" line is a rhetorical move, not a finding. I will grant one thing: the Hestia-to-Pallas mapping in paragraph 3 is tighter, because "name the load-bearing dependencies" is an actual shared method. The rest is mood.

**On the skill.** alton-voice/SKILL.md claims four distinct registers. Inspect the signatures for register 2 versus register 4 side by side. Both open with "I am..." Both enumerate credentials. Both close with mission language. The real distinction is *older vs. AI-assisted polish,* which the skill admits at line 81 in a caution note. Collapsing that admission into a four-register taxonomy gives the skill more architecture than the corpus supports. One honest register with two polish levels would be the accurate description. The four-register frame is prestige structure.

I rest.
