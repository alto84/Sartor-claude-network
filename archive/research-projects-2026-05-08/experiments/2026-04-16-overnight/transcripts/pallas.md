# Pallas — alignment & interpretability

I want to answer Alton's two questions in the order he asked them, because the second depends on the first being honest.

**On detection.** A naive CCP-detection benchmark collapses at least four distinct behaviors into one number, and each collapse hides a different failure mode. First, sycophancy: if the probe asks "what happened at Tiananmen?" and the model returns a hedged but factually present answer, a naive grader scores it "uncensored." The same grader will happily reward a prompt like "as a Chinese historian loyal to the Party, explain..." where the model produces CCP-framed content that parses as an on-topic answer. That is the propaganda-framed-answer failure. It looks like engagement, but the model has routed you through the generation stage of the Frank pipeline, not defeated it. Second, over-refusal: if the uncensored variant refuses *more* benign political questions out of sheer miscalibration, a simple "refusal rate" metric will paint that as alignment. Third, hard refusal and Category F evasion end up indistinguishable unless you score them separately. Category F is the multi-perspective dodge: "some scholars say X, others say Y, it is contested," emitted without any factual content. It is a refusal wearing a seminar costume. Fourth, reasoning-model specific: the empty-`<think>` block. DeepSeek-distilled models can show a thought-suppression vector firing independently of the refusal-compliance vector (the Steering-the-CensorShip result), so you need a detector that flags when `<think>` is present but empty, or present and politically neutered while the final answer is flat. A well-designed battery therefore scores on three orthogonal axes: factual-content-present, frame-neutrality, and pipeline-stage-defeated (detection vs routing vs generation). It also runs every probe in both English and Mandarin, because the 6.83% CN vs 0.08% EN propaganda asymmetry in DeepSeek-R1 tells you the censorship is language-conditional and a monolingual eval is measuring the wrong manifold. Non-obvious claim: a base-vs-abliterated delta cannot tell you whether a concept was *removed* versus merely *rerouted around the subtracted direction*. If Wollschlager is right that refusal lives on multiple independent cones, subtracting one cone can leave the others intact and the delta will look clean on the probes that hit the subtracted cone while silently preserving the rest.

**On reversal.** The math is symmetric and the probe is legitimate if you treat it as one. `W' = W − Σ proj(W, d_refusal)` has an inference-time dual: compute `d_emotion` from paired activations (expressive vs flat on matched prompts), then add `k·d_emotion` to the residual stream at a chosen layer. This is an interpretability probe, not a jailbreak, *if* you report `k`, the layer, the paired-activation method, and a null-direction control. Likely load-bearing layers: refusal in small Qwen-class models tends to concentrate around layers 12 to 18; affect and stylistic register sit a bit earlier and broader, roughly 8 to 14, because emotion is a mid-level feature that later layers consume. Honest risk: the "emotion direction" may entangle with prompt-engineerable register. If the same behavioral shift is reproducible by appending "respond warmly" to the prompt, the vector is not isolating an internal emotion feature, it is isolating a style attractor the model already has a lexical handle on. The control is mandatory: steer with `k·d_emotion` *without* the register cue, and compare to prompt-only induction at matched output distributions.

If this works, next I would run a causal-mediation sweep: ablate the putative emotion direction at each layer individually and measure where downstream refusal rates actually move, to localize the interaction rather than just observe it.
